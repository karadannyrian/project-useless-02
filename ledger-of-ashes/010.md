## Chapter 10: The Eyrie Approach

---

The anchor point was a stone platform at the edge of the sky.

Six had seen it before—from a distance, from the Docks, from the corner of his eye while crossing bridges and ducking Watch patrols. Everyone in Midden had seen it. The Eyrie floated above the city like a second moon, a fortress of white stone and old magic, tethered to the ground by four chains thick as a man's waist. The Sentinels lived up there. Trained up there. Watched the city from above and did nothing while the Warrens starved and the Shaded Market burned and councilwomen built siege bunkers under textile warehouses.

He'd never been this close.

The platform was empty except for one figure.

The man in iron stood at the center of the stone, shock-maul resting on his shoulder, chain coiled at his belt. He'd been waiting. Not hiding. Not rushing. Just standing there, patient as a mountain, watching the approach path with the calm of someone who'd known exactly where they'd go.

He knew about Corvin Vell. Knew about the ledger. Knew the only way to reach the Sentinels was through this platform, and the only way through this platform was through him.

Six stopped at the platform's edge.

Dreya stopped beside him. She could walk now—slowly, with a limp, her shoulder bandaged and her thigh wound closed and her face still too pale. But she could walk. She could hide. She could watch.

Six jerked his head toward a fallen column near the platform's edge.

Dreya looked at him. Opened her mouth. Closed it. She didn't argue. Didn't explain why she should stay. Didn't list the tactical reasons she could help. She just limped to the column and crouched behind it. Her hand pressed over her mouth. Her eyes were wide and dark and watching.

Six drew his sword.

The man in iron watched him approach. Didn't move. Didn't raise the maul. Just watched, the slit in his helmet dark and unreadable.

Six stopped ten feet away. Close enough to strike. Far enough to dodge.

The man in iron reached up. Unlatched his helmet. Pulled it off.

His face was scarred. Weathered. Grey hair cropped short. Pale eyes. The same face Six had seen in the bunker, bent over maps, taking orders from a councilwoman. But different now. Harder. This was his battlefield face. His dying face, maybe.

"You came," he said. His voice was low. Rough. Not surprised.

Six didn't answer.

"No lightning this time," the man said. Not a question. "The iron won't allow it. You know that. You felt it in the warehouse. You'll feel it again."

He was right. The hum was already in Six's teeth—that void where his lightning should be. The man's armor was cold iron. Every plate. Every joint. The only thing Six's magic would do was scatter and die.

Steel, then. Just steel.

"Good," the man said. He settled the helmet back over his head. Raised the maul. "I wanted to see which one you were."

He moved.

---

The first blow shattered stone.

Six dodged left. The maul came down where he'd been standing and the platform cracked—a spiderweb of fractures spreading across the pale stone. The force of it hummed through the ground, through Six's boots, through his bones. Heavy. Too heavy. If that blow landed, he wouldn't get up.

He backed away. Reset his grip. Watched.

The man in iron advanced. Not fast—he never moved fast—but deliberate. Every step closed distance. Every step took something away. The platform was only forty feet across. Six had maybe six steps before his back hit the railing. Before the drop.

The maul came around in a horizontal arc. Six ducked under it. The wind of its passing ruffled his hair. He thrust—aimed for the helmet, the gorget, the places he'd tried before. The blade scraped off iron. No dent. No scratch. The armor swallowed the impact the way it had in the warehouse, the way it had every time.

The man kept coming.

Six gave ground. One step. Two. The railing was behind him now. The drop was behind the railing. Cold air and empty sky and a fall that would take long enough to regret.

The man swung again. Overhead. Both hands on the maul. Six stepped inside the arc—close, too close, the iron humming against his skin—and brought his sword up.

It hit the helmet. Nothing. He hit the gorget. Nothing. He hit the pauldron, the breastplate, the gauntlet.

Nothing. Nothing. Nothing.

The man's fist caught him in the ribs.

Not the maul. Just the hand. But the hand was iron-gauntleted and the force behind it was twenty years of training. Six flew backward. Hit the stone. Rolled. His sword clattered, spun, stopped six feet away.

He couldn't breathe.

The man in iron walked toward him. Slow. Patient.

"You fight well," he said. His voice echoed inside the helmet. "Good reach. Good instincts. But instinct isn't enough. You learned to fight in the Warrens. On the streets. Against men who wanted coin or blood or just to hurt someone smaller than themselves. You never learned to fight someone who wanted nothing but to win."

Six pushed himself up. Grabbed his sword. His ribs screamed. Something was cracked. Maybe broken. Didn't matter.

He backed away. Reset. Watched.

The man was right. He'd learned to fight in the Warrens. Learned to survive. Learned to run when running was smarter. But this wasn't a street fight. This was a duel. No exits. No canals. No collapsing tunnels. Just the platform and the sky and a man who'd been killing longer than Six had been alive.

He needed an opening.

Every armor had a gap. He'd told himself that in the warehouse. He'd been right. He just hadn't found it. He'd been looking at the helmet. The gorget. The places armor always failed. But this armor was cold iron, forged for mage-hunters, and it had no gaps where armor should have gaps.

Except.

The armpit.

When the man swung overhead, his arm lifted. The pauldron shifted. And beneath it—just for a moment, just for half a breath—there was a gap. Not iron. Just mail. Just leather. Just the place where the body had to bend and the armor had to let it.

Six had seen it in the warehouse. He'd been too slow then. Too hesitant.

Not this time.

---

The man in iron raised the maul for another overhead blow. Six didn't dodge. Didn't back away. He stepped forward—into the arc, into the iron hum, into the space where the maul was rising and the arm was lifting and the gap was opening.

The maul came down.

Six sidestepped.

The maul hit stone. The platform cracked again. The man's arm was still raised—still extended—still open.

Six thrust.

The sword found the gap.

It slid through mail and leather and flesh. Through the space between ribs. Through the lung. Through the heart.

The man in iron stopped moving.

He stood there, maul still raised, arm still extended, and looked down at the blade buried in his chest. His breath came out in a long, slow exhale. Not a gasp. Not a scream. Just air leaving.

"Clean thrust," he said.

His voice was different now. Quieter. The helmet made it hollow.

He lowered the maul. Set it on the ground. His other hand came up—slow, deliberate—and touched the blade where it entered his chest. His gauntleted fingers came away red.

He looked at Six.

There was no anger in his eyes. No hatred. No fear. Just something that might have been approval. Might have been respect. Might have been the relief of a man who'd been fighting for a long time and was finally allowed to stop.

Then he fell.

He fell forward. Heavy. All that iron, all that weight, and Six had to step aside to keep from being crushed. The body hit the stone with a sound like a bell being silenced. The helmet rolled free. The grey hair was matted with sweat. The pale eyes were open. Empty.

The iron hum stopped.

Six stood over the body. His sword was still in his hand. Blood dripped from the blade onto the stone. His ribs hurt. His hands hurt. His chest hurt from something that wasn't the fight.

He was tired.

Not the tired of running or casting or staying awake for two days. A different tired. A deeper one.

He'd killed men before. Not many. Not like this. In the Warrens, killing was quick and dirty and you didn't stop to look at the body. You didn't hear their last words. You didn't see them put down their weapon like they were setting down a burden.

The man in iron had called it clean. Had touched the blade with something like gratitude.

Six didn't feel triumphant. He felt heavy. Like some of that iron weight had transferred to him.

He could have used lightning. Could have tried, anyway—the armor might have grounded it, might have scattered it, but he could have tried. He'd chosen not to. He wasn't sure if that was wisdom or fear. Wisdom: knowing the lightning would fail and the Toll would cripple him. Fear: of the cold, of the heart-lurch, of pushing too far and not coming back.

He didn't know which. Didn't matter now. He was alive. She was alive.

He heard footsteps behind him. Limping. Uneven.

Dreya stopped beside him. She looked at the body. At the blood on the stone. At the sword in his hand.

She didn't speak. Didn't offer thanks or comfort or analysis. She just reached out and put her hand on his arm.

Her fingers were cold. He could feel them through his sleeve. She didn't grip. Didn't pull. Just rested her hand there. A weight. An anchor.

He didn't shake it off.

They stood like that for a long moment. The body at their feet. The sky above them. The Eyrie's chains creaking in the wind. The city below, still turning, still breathing, still unaware that anything had changed.

Then Dreya let her hand drop. Limped toward the platform's center. Toward the mechanism that would signal the Eyrie above.

"The Sentinels," she said. Her voice was hoarse. Quiet. But steady. "We need to tell them about Ashford. About the fortress. About—"

She stopped. Looked back at him.

"Are you coming?"

Six looked at the body one more time. At the grey hair. The open eyes. The maul resting on the stone.

He cleaned his blade on the dead man's tunic. Sheathed it. Followed her.
