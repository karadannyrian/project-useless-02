**Chapter 4: The Shaded Market**

---

The Shaded Market stinks of grilled meat and stolen goods.

Six moves through the crowd with Dreya limping beside him, her weight on his arm, her breath coming in short, wet hitches. The infection has set in—he can smell it now, under the market stink. Sweet. Wrong. The bolt wound needs more than a bandage. It needs a medic. It needs medicine he can't afford and she's too pale to wait for.

The market doesn't care. The market never cares. Stalls crowd both sides of the alley, tarps strung overhead to hide the lantern-light from Watch patrols. Sellers hawk stolen cargo, glass rods that wouldn't spark, meat of questionable origin. Buyers haggle in three languages. Someone's cooking onions on a flat iron griddle, and the smoke curls through the crowd like a grey river.

Exits: alley north, alley south, two gaps between stalls on the east side, one ladder to a rooftop west. Six counts them without thinking. Enter somewhere, know how to leave. The first thing. Always.

Dreya is still talking.

"—the infection progression suggests a bacterial agent introduced by the bolt itself, which means the bolt was either deliberately contaminated or the Archive basement had significantly worse sanitation than the Academy's maintenance logs indicated. Both possibilities are troubling, though for different reasons. The deliberate contamination would imply premeditation, which would mean someone knew I'd be there, which would mean—"

She stops talking.

Six feels it before he sees her. The crowd is moving—shouldering past, haggling, drinking—but there's a stillness at one of the food stalls. A gap in the current. Someone sitting very still while the world flows around her.

Young. Early twenties, maybe. But her hair is grey. Not silver-grey, not elegant—the dull, dry grey of something burned out. Dark circles under her eyes. Her face is twenty-four. Her hair is forty.

She's eating a skewer of grilled meat.

Watching them.

Six stops walking. His hand finds his sword hilt. Not drawing. Not yet.

Dreya follows his gaze. "Oh," she says. "That's an Imperial battle-mage."

The woman takes another bite of her skewer. Chews. Swallows. Wipes her mouth with the back of her hand. The motion is casual. Practiced. She's not hiding. She's not rushing. She's been waiting.

She stands.

She's shorter than both of them. The leather harness across her chest holds six iron cylinders, each the size of a finger. He didn't know the word for them. Knew what they meant. Her magic isn't hers. It's borrowed. Stored. Limited.

Six spells per fight. Then she's nothing.

"Give me the ledger," she says. Her voice is hoarse. Tired. "I'll let you walk."

Dreya straightens. Even leaning on Six, even with her leg oozing infection and her face the color of old paper, she straightens. "That's a lie."

The mage sighs. "It's a deal."

"Imperial mages don't have the authority to negotiate. The Magisterium's chain of command doesn't delegate treaty powers to field operatives. You're a battle-mage, which means you're attached to a legion, which means you answer to a legate, who answers to a prefect, who answers to the Imperial Council. None of whom would authorize a deal with witnesses to whatever's in this ledger. The Empire doesn't negotiate with witnesses. The Empire eliminates witnesses."

The mage stares at her for a long moment. Then she sighs again. Deeper this time.

"I was hoping you'd say no."

She reaches for a cylinder.

"Now I have to cast."

Six moves.

Not fast. Not with lightning—the market is too dense, too iron-heavy, the buzz in his teeth a constant warning. Just his body. Just the reflexes that have kept him alive for twenty-six years. He shoves Dreya behind a stall and draws his sword in the same motion.

The mage pulls a cylinder from her harness. Her thumb cracks a seal. The iron shell splits, and inside, something glows. A crystal. Small. Fierce. The light is the color of a forge at full heat.

She speaks a word Six doesn't know.

The firebolt comes.

It's not like Dreya's air blade. It's not subtle, not precise, not a surgeon's tool. It's a fist of flame the size of a man's head, and it moves fast. The heat hits before the fire does—a wave of dry, scorching air that makes the crowd scream and scatter. Onions burn on the griddle. A tarp catches.

Six doesn't think. The beat is already there—the rhythm of the crowd's panic, footsteps drumming on cobblestones, a woman shouting, a child crying. He taps it. Once. Twice. The rhythm catches.

Lightning stirs in his chest.

Not to attack. To push. A magnetic shove—not aimed at the mage, but at the iron in the stall beside him. The stall jerks sideways, dragging its tarp with it, and the firebolt hits the canvas instead of his chest. Flame explodes. Canvas burns. The stall collapses in a shower of sparks and smoke.

His hands go cold. Fingers numb. He ignores it.

The mage is already reaching for a second cylinder.

Dreya casts.

Six doesn't see it at first. He feels it—a pressure drop, a wrongness in the air. The smoke from the burning stall bends inward. The sound of the crowd warps, muffled, like a door closed between them and the world.

The mage's firebolt dies in her hand.

Not doused. Not deflected. It simply goes out. The crystal's glow gutters and dies, and the flame with it, and the mage's eyes go wide. Her hands fly to her throat.

She can't breathe.

Dreya is standing behind the stall where Six pushed her. One hand extended. Her fingers are shaping something—not a blade this time. A sphere. A bubble of nothing. The air around the mage's head shimmers. Vacuum.

The mage chokes. Her mouth opens. No sound comes out. Her face goes red, then purple. The cylinder drops from her fingers and rolls across the cobblestones. She claws at her throat like she can tear the nothing away.

Dreya's hand is shaking.

Blood drips from her nose. Her eyes are squinting—not at the fire, not at the smoke. At something inside her own skull. The head pain. Worse than before. Much worse.

Six watches. He doesn't move. He doesn't stop her. He files it away.

The mage's knees buckle.

Dreya drops her hand.

The vacuum collapses. Air rushes back in with a gasp that's half-sob, and the mage sucks in a breath like a drowning woman breaking the surface. She's on her knees. Coughing. Retching. Her grey hair is plastered to her face with sweat.

She doesn't reach for another cylinder.

She looks at Dreya. There's something in her eyes—not fear, not anger. Recognition. She knows what Dreya just did. She knows what it cost. She knows Dreya stopped.

The mage pushes herself up. Grabs the fallen cylinder. Backs into the crowd.

Gone.

---

Dreya is staring at her hands.

The blood from her nose has dripped onto her robes. The shaking hasn't stopped. Her eyes are unfocused—vision blurred from the head pain, or from something else. She's half-blind. Half-standing.

Six sheathes his sword. Takes her arm.

"That was a level three evocation," she says. Her voice is thin. Brittle. "Her form was sloppy. The crystal binding was unstable—you could see the light flicker before she even cast. She's been using those Mediums too long. They're degrading. Another few months and one of them would have detonated in her harness. She's burning out. Her hair. Did you see her hair? She's twenty-four, maybe twenty-five, and her hair is grey. That's not genetics. That's Toll. That's years of Imperial service. That's—"

She's talking too fast. The words are coming in bunches, running into each other. She's not looking at him. She's looking at her hands.

"I didn't even cast, technically. The nosebleed was from watching. The blood vessels in the nasal cavity are sensitive to pressure changes. I learned that in second-year anatomy. The lecturer was a dwarf from Frostmark who'd lost both legs to frostbite and somehow still made it to class every morning. He said pain was a diagnostic tool. If your nose bleeds when you see a spell, the spell is either too strong or you're too close. I was too close. I was definitely too close. I almost—"

She doesn't finish.

Six pulls her into the crowd.

She stumbles after him. Her hand is cold in his. The shaking hasn't stopped.

He finds an alley. Dark. Quiet. The market noise fades behind them, replaced by the drip of water and the distant clang of a Watch bell. Dreya leans against a wall. Slides down. Sits on the cobblestones with her bad leg stretched out and her head in her hands.

Her fingers are trembling.

Six crouches beside her. Checks her wound—the bandage is holding. Checks her face—the nosebleed has stopped. Checks her eyes—still unfocused, still looking at something he can't see.

She almost killed someone.

She stopped. Let go. He doesn't know why. Doesn't know if she knows why.

He stays where he is. Doesn't speak. Doesn't move.

Her hands are still shaking. Her breath is still shallow. But she's not talking anymore. The wall of words has crumbled. Behind it: just a scholar with blood on her robes and a question she can't ask out loud.

Six waits.

When she's ready to stand, he'll know. When she's ready to walk, he'll be there.

He's never stayed this long before. For anyone.

He doesn't examine it.
