## Chapter 12: The Final Stand

---

They emerged from the stairwell onto a platform that hung between the Eyrie and the sky.

Not the anchor point where the man in iron had waited. That was below, at ground level, where the chains met the earth. This was the mid-station—a stone circle suspended halfway down, connected to the fortress above by iron cable and to the city below by a single chain. The wind howled through it. The platform had no walls. No railings. Just stone and air and a drop that went down forever.

Six stopped at the edge.

The quiet one was waiting.

He stood at the center of the platform, calm as still water, a crossbow in his hands. The bolt was already loaded. The string was already drawn. He didn't fire. He was waiting for them to step fully onto the stone. Waiting for the moment when there was nowhere left to run.

Beside him, on her knees, was the Imperial mage.

She looked worse than before. Worse than the market. Worse than the God-Fall chamber. Her skin was grey—not pale, not tired, grey like old ash. Her hair had gone white at the temples. The harness across her chest was empty. She'd spent them all—on Sentinels, on running, on surviving long enough to reach this platform. One cylinder remained, clutched in her trembling hand. Her last Medium. The crystal inside flickered with dying light.

She was crying.

Not sobbing. Not making sound. The tears just ran down her grey face and dripped off her chin and she didn't seem to have the strength to wipe them away.

"I don't want to die here," she said. Her voice was cracked. Small. "I don't want to—"

The quiet one raised the crossbow.

He didn't speak. He never spoke. The string drew back that final inch. The bolt aimed at Six's chest.

Dreya stepped forward.

---

Six saw her face.

He'd never seen her face like that. Still. Past fear. Past everything. Her hands came up, and they weren't shaking. Her eyes were clear. Her mouth was set.

She cast.

The air didn't move. It ruptured.

A wall of force exploded outward from her hands—not a shield, not a blade, not a bubble. A concussive wave that tore across the platform like a thunderclap. The sound of it hit Six's chest before the wind did. His ears popped. His vision blurred. He dropped to one knee and braced against the stone and felt the whole platform shudder.

The quiet one flew backward.

He didn't scream. His arms went wide. The crossbow spun away into the void. His body tumbled once, twice, and then he was over the edge—falling, shrinking, swallowed by the space between the platform and the canal below.

The dark water took him without a sound.

The Imperial mage was thrown sideways. Her cylinder skittered across the stone. She scrambled after it on hands and knees, coughing, crying, her fingers closing around the iron shell like it was the only solid thing left in the world.

Dreya collapsed.

Not slowly. Not gracefully. Her legs gave out and her eyes rolled back and she dropped like a stone. Six caught her before she hit the platform. Her head lolled against his shoulder. Her breathing was shallow. Too shallow. Too fast. Her pulse fluttered under his fingers—wrong-beat, skip, wrong-beat—and then steadied.

She was alive.

He lowered her to the stone. Checked her head. Checked her breathing. Checked her heart. Alive. Unconscious. Spent.

He stood up.

The Imperial mage was on her knees in the center of the platform. Her cylinder was clutched to her chest. The crystal inside was flickering—dying, maybe, or just too weak to cast. She looked up at him. Her grey face was streaked with tears and blood. Her hands were shaking so badly she could barely hold the Medium.

"I don't want to die here," she said again. Quieter. Like a confession.

Six drew his sword.

She flinched. Closed her eyes. Waited.

He could kill her. It would be easy. She was on her knees, weapon spent, body broken. One thrust. The same thrust. Clean. Quick. She wouldn't feel it for more than a second.

But she was crying. And her skin was grey. And she'd aimed to miss in the God-Fall chamber—put the firebolt into the wall instead of into him, into Dreya, into the shaft they were climbing. She'd aimed to miss and he still didn't know why.

He looked at her. At the grey skin. At the blood on her lips. At the tears cutting tracks through the ash.

He lowered the sword.

Reached down. Took the cylinder from her hands.

She let him. Her fingers opened without resistance. The iron shell was warm. The crystal inside pulsed once—faint, faltering—and then went still. Without the Medium, she couldn't cast. Without the Medium, she was just a dying woman on a stone platform in the sky.

She stared at her empty hands.

"Why?" she whispered.

Six didn't answer. He didn't know why. He just hadn't wanted to kill her.

He turned away. Sheathed his sword. Crossed to where Dreya lay and lifted her onto his shoulder. She was limp. Heavy. Her breath was warm against his neck.

The chain waited at the platform's edge. Iron links as thick as his wrist, cold and rough and humming with the same void his lightning couldn't fill. He grabbed it with one hand. Dreya hung over his other shoulder. He stepped off the platform.

The wind hit him. The chain swayed. He didn't look down.

He climbed.

---

The descent took an hour. Maybe more. He didn't count.

His fingers went cold first. Then his hands. Then his arms. The iron leached heat from him with every link, every grip, every inch of downward progress. He could have used a speed boost—could have let the lightning surge and carried them both down in half the time—but Dreya was unconscious on his shoulder and the chain was iron and his heart was steady. He didn't want to change that.

So he climbed. One link at a time. Hand over hand. Dreya's weight pulling at his shoulder. The wind pulling at his coat. The city below growing larger, brick by brick, roof by roof, until he could see the canal and the Docks and the warehouse where Lot 7 hid its siege engines in the dark.

Behind him, above him, the platform shrank to a stone speck against the sky. The Imperial mage was still on her knees. Still staring at her empty hands. He didn't look back at her.

He didn't know why he'd spared her.

He just hadn't wanted to kill her.

He didn't examine it.

Hand over hand. Link by link. Dreya breathing against his neck. His heart steady in his chest. The cold spreading from his fingers to his wrists to his arms.

It was almost dawn when his boots touched stone.

The ground was solid. Dirty. Real. The anchor point from the morning before—the man in iron's blood still staining the stone, the shock-maul still lying where it had fallen. No one had come to clean it. No one had come at all.

Six lowered Dreya to the ground. Checked her pulse. Checked her breathing. Still shallow. Still too fast. But steady. Alive.

He sat beside her. His back against the stone. His sword across his knees. The Medium he'd taken from the Imperial mage was cold in his pocket.

Above him, the Eyrie floated in the grey pre-dawn light. Below him, the city stirred. Somewhere in the canals, the quiet one drifted with the current. Somewhere in the sky, a dying mage knelt on a stone platform and waited for an end that wouldn't come from him.

Six closed his eyes.

Dreya breathed beside him.

He didn't sleep. He just waited. He was good at waiting.
