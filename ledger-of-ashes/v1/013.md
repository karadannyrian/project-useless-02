## Chapter 13: The Iron Box

---

Dreya woke in stages.

First her breathing changed—slower, deeper, the rhythm of someone climbing out of sleep instead of lying where they'd fallen. Then her fingers moved. Twitched against the stone floor. Tested the weight of her own hands. Then her eyes opened.

She stared at the ceiling for a long moment. Low stone. Damp in the corners. Not the Eyrie. Not the platform. Somewhere else. Somewhere underground.

She turned her head.

Six was sitting against the wall across the room. His sword was propped beside him. His coat was draped over her—she hadn't noticed until now, but the weight on her shoulders was wool and leather and the faint smell of canal water. The stove in the corner had burned down to embers. He'd fed it twice while she slept. Maybe more.

He was holding a small iron box.

It sat in his palms like something alive. His thumbs traced the edges. The seam where the lid met the base. The lock that had no keyhole. He wasn't trying to open it. He was just holding it. The way she'd held his sword in the safehouse, days ago—not to use, just to have something solid.

She watched him for a long moment. The firelight on his face. The box in his hands. The stillness.

"What's in it?" she asked.

Her voice came out thin. Cracked. But it was her voice. She was alive. She was awake. She was talking again.

Six looked at her.

"I don't know."

Three words. Spoken to her. His voice was low. Rough from underuse. Calm.

She watched him turn the box over in his hands. The iron was dark. Old. The edges were worn smooth. He handled it carefully. The way you handle something that matters for reasons you can't explain.

He didn't open it.

She didn't ask again.

---

She sat up slowly. Her shoulder ached where the force bolt had hit. Her thigh ached where the crossbow bolt had been. Her head ached—a low, dull throb, the residue of the Toll she'd paid on the platform. But the fever was gone. The wounds were closed. She was weak and sore and alive.

The ledger was on the floor beside her. Charred at the edges. Still legible. Still damning. She picked it up. Turned the pages. The dates. The names. The shipments. Lot 7. Councilor Ashford. The siege engines under the textile warehouse. The conspiracy that went higher than they'd imagined and deeper than they'd feared.

"Fifteen days," she said.

Six looked up from the box.

"The first shipment of soldiers was scheduled for the end of the month. If the ledger is accurate, and I believe it is, and the timeline hasn't changed, and there's no reason to think it has unless Ashford knows we have the ledger, which she might, if Corvin told her, which he probably did, because he's a coward and cowards talk when they're scared—" She stopped. Took a breath. Started again. "Fifteen days. Maybe less."

Six nodded.

"But we can't take this to the Sentinels," she said. "Corvin will deny everything. He'll say we stole the ledger. He'll say we're Imperial agents. He'll say whatever he needs to say to save himself, and the Order will believe him. They'll believe their own before they believe a freelance mercenary and an exiled scholar. That's how institutions work. They protect themselves. They always protect themselves."

She was right. He nodded.

"We need to find someone who believes us," she said.

He nodded again.

"Who?"

He shrugged.

That was the problem. No allies. No safe harbor. The Empire wanted them dead. The Sentinels would want them dead as soon as Corvin finished telling his version of the story. Valeris had exiled her. Frostmark was too far. The Dominion was worse. There was no one. There was nowhere.

Dreya looked at the ledger in her hands. At the charred pages. At the proof that was useless without someone to give it to.

"There has to be someone," she said. Quieter. Less certain.

Six didn't answer. He was looking at the box again.

---

The iron was warm in his hands.

He didn't know when he'd taken it out of his pack. He didn't remember reaching for it. He'd been sitting against the wall, watching her breathe, feeding the stove, and then the box was in his hands and he was turning it over and over and the iron was warm.

Iron shouldn't be warm.

The stove was across the room. His hands were cold—they were always cold, the inside cold that never quite went away. But the box was warm. Not hot. Not burning. Just warm. Like something inside was alive. Like something inside had been waiting.

Mara had told him never to open it. She'd been dying. Winter illness. The same winter that killed half the Warrens. She'd pressed the box into his hands—he was fifteen, he was tall even then, but he'd felt small—and she'd said *Never open it* and then she'd said nothing else. She'd died the next morning. He'd carried the box ever since.

He'd never opened it.

He didn't open it now.

He turned it over one more time. Traced the seam with his thumb. Felt the warmth that shouldn't be there. Then he put it back in his pack.

Not yet. Not today.

Dreya was watching him. She hadn't moved. Hadn't spoken. Her eyes were dark in the firelight.

"We should sleep," she said. "We can't solve this tonight. We need rest. We need food. We need to not be dead. Those are the priorities. In that order. Sleep, food, not dead. Then we figure out who."

She lay back down. Pulled his coat over her shoulders. Closed her eyes.

"She's not dead," she said. "Your person. The one who gave you the box. You talk about her like she's not dead. You just don't talk about her at all."

She didn't open her eyes.

"That's how I know," she said.

Six didn't answer. He sat against the wall. The fire burned down to embers. The box sat in his pack. Warm. Waiting.

Dreya's breathing slowed. Evened out. She was asleep.

He stayed awake. Watched the door. Watched her. Thought about fifteen days and councilwomen and a box that shouldn't be warm.

Not yet. Not today.

But soon.
