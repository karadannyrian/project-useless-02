**Chapter 8: The Chase Underground**

---

The tunnels were collapsing.

Not randomly. Not from age or rot or the slow crush of limestone settling. Someone was bringing them down. Deliberate. Coordinated. The first collapse sealed the north passage—the one Six had planned to use, the one that led to the upper cisterns and the canal overflow. The second took the eastern junction. The third was closer.

Six ran.

Dreya ran beside him. Her limp was better but not gone. Her breath was ragged. Her face was still pale from the bolt extraction, still sheened with sweat from the fever that hadn't quite broken. But she ran.

Behind them: footsteps. Soft. Patient. Not chasing. Herding.

The quiet one.

Above them: heavy footfalls on stone. The man in iron, moving across the surface, tracking them through the Warrens like a hand moving across a map. He wasn't collapsing tunnels himself—he was directing it. Someone else. Someone with enough knowledge of the Warrens to know which passages to hit and when.

Or someone who'd been told where to strike.

The third collapse took the southern spur. They were being funneled. Driven toward something. A kill box. A dead end. A place where the quiet one could pick them off without rushing, where the man in iron could seal the exits and wait.

Six didn't know where. He only knew the shape of the trap—the narrowing walls, the closing doors, the path that curved east when it should have curved west.

He ran faster.

---

The crossbow bolt hit the wall beside his head.

Stone splinters. Dust. The bolt buried itself three inches deep in the limestone. Six ducked left, pulled Dreya with him, and another bolt followed—aimed at where he'd been standing, not where he was. The quiet one was leading his shots. Anticipating.

Dreya's hand came up.

The air in front of them rippled. A shield. Not solid—translucent, wavering, a heat shimmer without the heat. The next bolt hit it and stopped. Hung there for half a breath. Dropped.

Dreya made a sound—a sharp exhale through her nose. Blood trickled from her left nostril. Her eyes were squinting. The head pain. Worse than before.

"Keep moving," she said. Her voice was tight. "I can hold it. I can—"

Another bolt. The shield held. She stumbled.

Six caught her arm. Pulled her forward. The tunnel ahead was narrowing—old construction, rough-hewn, the kind of passage that hadn't been maintained since the Warrens were first carved. He knew this section. Dead end coming. Unless he could reach the—

The tunnel shook.

Behind them: a collapse. Closer than the others. The man in iron had found the passage they'd just left. Sealed it. Now there was only forward.

Forward was a dead end.

Unless.

---

The narrow tunnel was barely wide enough for his shoulders. He pushed through sideways, dragging Dreya behind him, and the stone scraped his coat and his skin and the hilt of his sword. The quiet one was somewhere in the dark behind them—not rushing, never rushing, just following at the exact pace that kept them at the edge of his range.

The tunnel opened into a chamber.

Old. Older than the Warrens. The walls weren't limestone—they were something darker, something that gleamed faintly in the dark. God-Fall stone. The remnants of something that had been built before Midden, before the Accordance, before any of the Five Powers drew their borders. The air smelled like ozone and old lightning.

Six knew this chamber. He'd been here once, years ago, on a dare from another Warrens kid. They'd dared him to touch the walls. He'd done it. His fingers had tingled for a week.

There was a way out. A shaft in the ceiling—old ventilation, maybe, or a natural fissure. It led up to the canal level. He'd climbed it before. He could climb it again.

But it was narrow. One person at a time.

And the quiet one was coming.

---

Dreya's shield shattered.

Not from a bolt—from the force of something heavier. A concussive blast that rippled through the chamber and blew the shield apart like glass. Dreya cried out—a short, sharp sound—and her hands flew to her temples. The nosebleed became a stream. Her knees buckled.

The Imperial mage stepped into the chamber.

She looked worse than before. The grey in her hair had spread. The circles under her eyes were deep enough to be bruises. She held an iron cylinder in each hand—Mediums, both of them cracked, both of them flickering with dying light. She was burning through them. Burning through herself.

"That's my last force bolt," she said. Her voice was hoarse. Hollow. "I have one firebolt left. Please don't make me use it."

Six didn't answer. He was watching her hands. The cylinders. The way her fingers trembled.

Dreya was on her knees. Blood dripped from her chin. She was trying to cast—her fingers were shaping something, air or vacuum or something worse—but the head pain was too much. Her hands wouldn't cooperate. The spell fizzled.

"Don't," the Imperial mage said. Not a threat. A plea. "I don't want to—"

She didn't finish.

The quiet one stepped out of the darkness behind her.

Six moved.

Not toward the quiet one—toward Dreya. He scooped her up, one arm under her knees, one arm behind her back. She was lighter than she should be. Lighter than before. The fever had eaten at her. The blood loss. The running.

He ran for the shaft.

The quiet one's crossbow came up. Six didn't look. He counted—one, two, three—and juked left on the fourth step. The bolt passed through the space he'd been in. Hit the God-Fall stone. The stone hummed. Swallowed it.

The Imperial mage was saying something. Shouting, maybe. Her voice cracked on the words. Six didn't listen.

The shaft was above him. Too high to reach from the ground. He set Dreya down—propped her against the wall—and jumped. His fingers caught the lip of the stone. His shoulders screamed. The cold in his hands made his grip weak. But he held. Pulled himself up. Reached down.

Dreya was looking at him. Her face was streaked with blood and sweat and something else. Tears, maybe. She didn't seem to know they were there.

She reached up. He grabbed her wrist.

---

The Imperial mage threw the firebolt.

Not at them—at the shaft. At the wall beside it. The God-Fall stone detonated in a spray of black shards and blue light. The force knocked Six sideways. His grip on Dreya slipped. He caught her again—one hand on her forearm, one hand on the shaft's edge—and pulled.

The firebolt had been aimed to miss.

He noted it. Would think about it later.

They climbed.

The shaft was narrow and dark and slick with old moisture. Six pushed Dreya up ahead of him. She climbed with one hand and both feet, her injured leg dragging, her breath coming in sobs that broke against the stone. Above them: the sound of water. Below them: the quiet one, not following, just watching. The Imperial mage, not throwing anything else.

They emerged into the canal overflow.

Black water. Cold. Moving slow.

Six didn't hesitate. He pulled Dreya against his chest and dropped.

---

The water closed over them.

It was colder than the river. Older. The canal overflow ran through the deepest parts of the Warrens, where the sun never reached and the water never warmed. The cold hit Six like a second shock—different from the inside cold, but feeding it, layering on top of it. His fingers were already numb. His lips were already pale. He could feel the shivering starting in his chest.

Dreya was still in his arms. She wasn't swimming. Wasn't casting. Just floating. Her eyes were open. Staring at nothing.

The current carried them.

The ceiling of the overflow channel was low—inches above the water. No light. No sound except the lap of water against stone. Six floated on his back, Dreya against his chest, and watched the darkness slide past.

The quiet one didn't follow. Couldn't swim. Wouldn't swim.

The man in iron was somewhere above, but the overflow channel ran deep—deeper than any map he might have, deeper than any exit he could seal from the surface.

The Imperial mage had aimed to miss.

He didn't know why. Didn't have time to think about it.

---

The channel widened into a cistern. Old stone. Brackish water. A maintenance ladder leading up to a grate. Through the grate: starlight. The Docks. The river.

Six dragged Dreya onto the ladder. She climbed. Slow. Mechanical. Not talking.

They surfaced in an alley near the old customs office. The same alley they'd stumbled into after the bridge. After the first chase. After the river.

Dreya sat against the wall. Pulled her knees up. Pressed her forehead to them.

She wasn't talking. Wasn't lecturing. Wasn't deflecting. Just breathing. Her shoulders shook—not from cold, though she was cold, they were both cold. Something else.

Six stood at the alley mouth. Watched the street. No footsteps. No iron hum. No soft-soled shoes on stone. Not yet.

Behind him, Dreya hadn't moved.

She'd screamed.

When the force bolt hit her shoulder—when the concussive blast threw her to her knees—she'd screamed. The sound had torn out of her before she could stop it. Raw. Involuntary. A sound he'd never heard her make before.

He'd heard wounded people scream before. He'd heard dying people scream. This was different.

Now she wouldn't look at him. Her face was hidden against her knees. Her hands lay limp at her sides instead of gesturing, pointing, shaping spells out of habit.

The scream had done something to her. He didn't know what.

He didn't ask.

He crouched beside her. Didn't speak. Didn't touch her. Just stayed there, his back to the alley wall, his sword across his knees, his eyes on the street.

The shivering in his chest was worse now. The cold had settled deep—past his fingers, past his lips, into his bones. He needed warmth. A fire. A dry coat. Soon. Not yet.

Dreya's shoulders stopped shaking. Slowly. In stages.

She didn't look up.

He didn't make her.

The starlight fell through the grate above them. The water dripped somewhere in the dark. Neither of them spoke.

She'd screamed. He'd heard it. It changed nothing.

He stayed where he was.
