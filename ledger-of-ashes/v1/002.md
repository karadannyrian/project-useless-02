**Chapter 2: The Scholar's Bridge**

---

She talks while he carries her.

"I can walk, I'm just slowly walking."

Six doesn't answer. One arm hooked under her shoulders, the other free for his sword. She weighs nothing but the angle is wrong—her bad leg drags, her weight shifts with every step, and the bolt in her thigh catches on his coat. She keeps talking.

"The Archive's structural integrity has degraded significantly since the last Academy survey. I read the report. Nineteen years ago, if you can believe that—nineteen years without a follow-up. The sub-levels were never meant to house active constructs. The humidity alone would—"

The night is cold. Real cold. The Archive had been warm, at least. The bridge ahead stretches dark over black water. No moon. No stars. Just lanterns at intervals, too far apart to help.

"—compromise the binding seals. Which, obviously, it did. That thing should have been inert for decades. The crystal's decay pattern suggests a cascading containment failure, which is fascinating, actually, because God-Fall remnants usually degrade at a predictable rate, but the iron concentration in the Archive district might have accelerated—"

Six exhaled through his nose. Slow. Measured. Not a sigh. A pressure release.

She shifted in his grip. Winced. "I'm Dreya, by the way. Dreya Castellan. I'd offer to shake hands, but—" She gestured vaguely at the bolt in her thigh, her arm over his shoulder. "You're Six. I know. I said that already. In the Archive. When I was bleeding. I'm still bleeding, actually. Is that a good sign or a bad sign? Medically speaking, continued blood flow suggests the bolt didn't hit the femoral artery, which is good, but the volume suggests—"

He exhaled again. Slower.

She didn't notice.

"I should document this. Once we're not actively fleeing. Assuming we survive the fleeing part. Which, statistically, is becoming less likely by the minute, given the coordination pattern of the footsteps upstairs and the fact that my leg is currently—"

Six stops.

They've reached the bridge.

Scholar's Bridge arches over the River Vein, a spine of old stone and older iron, connecting the Archive District to the Docks. The river runs fast below. Lanterns burn at intervals along the railings, spaced too far apart. Pools of light. Rivers of dark.

The water sounds cold.

Six scans the bridge. Empty. Too empty. The Archive is a dead district after dark, but there's always someone—a drunk scholar, a night watchman, a courier running late. Tonight: nothing.

The buzz in his teeth says iron. The bridge has it in the bones. Reinforcing rods, old nails, the rusted understructure. His magic will stutter here if he calls it.

He steps onto the stone.

His boots make no sound. He learned that young. Weight on the outside edge. Roll through the toe. Years of walking rooms where noise meant a beating, or worse. The Warrens taught quiet the way cold teaches shivering—without words, without choice.

Dreya has not learned quiet.

"—the bridge was built during the Second Expansion, you know. The stonework is unmistakable. You can tell by the mortar composition—limestone and crushed river shell, which was only used for about forty years before they switched to volcanic ash from the Wyrm-Teeth. Of course, the iron reinforcements are later additions, probably post-Shatter, when they started reinforcing everything against—"

He stops walking.

Something is wrong.

Not a sound. An absence of sound. The river should be loud. It's not. The bridge is swallowing noise—no echo from his footsteps, no bounce from Dreya's voice. The air feels draped. Heavy. Like a room with the door shut.

Then the hum changes.

Not iron-buzz. Something else. A frequency he knows. Someone is using magic nearby—not casting, but holding. A drawn breath before the exhale.

He doesn't have time to shout.

The shape drops from the underside of the bridge.

It shouldn't have been there. The beams are narrow, the drop is thirty feet to black water, and there's nothing to hold onto but rusted iron and crumbling mortar. But the man—the shape—unfolds from the darkness like he was born in it. Arms. Legs. A blade in each hand. Not blades. Needles. Long and thin, catching lantern-light.

He lands without sound.

Six drops Dreya.

She hits the stone with a grunt of protest, but he's already moving. Sword out. Weight low. The needle comes fast—too fast—and he parries by feel more than sight. Metal kisses metal. The sound is wrong. High. Thin. The needle slides off his blade and the man is already somewhere else.

Gone.

Six pivots. Empty bridge. Lanterns flickering. Dreya on the ground behind him, struggling to sit up, mouth open to ask what's happening.

Then the second needle comes from above.

He doesn't see it. He feels it—a whisper of air, a change in pressure. His body knows what his mind hasn't named yet. He twists. The needle passes through the wool of his coat, grazing his ribs, missing skin by a finger's width. It skitters across the stone and stops, glistening. Something clear beads on the tip.

Not blood. Something worse.

The man is on the railing now. Balanced on the stone balustrade like a bird on a wire. Hooded. Face invisible. The lantern behind him turns him into a silhouette, but Six can see the shape of him—lean, compact, built for small spaces. Built for ambush.

He holds a third needle.

Six raises his sword.

The man doesn't attack. He waits. Head tilted. Studying.

Then he moves.

Fast. Faster than a body should move. Six tracks him by sound—the scuff of a soft-soled shoe on stone, the rustle of fabric—and swings where the man should be. His blade cuts air. Wrong. He was behind. Now he's not. Now he's—

Dreya screams.

Not fear. Pain.

The needle is in her arm.

She stares at it. Her lips move. No sound. The needle juts from her forearm just below the elbow, and the skin around it is already darkening. Not bruising. Something else. The darkness spreads in threads, crawling toward her shoulder.

Her fingers go rigid.

Her mouth is still moving. The words don't come. Her whole body locks up—legs, arms, neck—and she topples sideways, a statue tipping. Her eyes are the only thing still moving. They're furious.

Six doesn't look at her. Can't. The man is still here.

He comes from the side this time. Low. The needle aimed for Six's thigh. Six brings his sword down in a short arc, two-handed, but the man flows around it like water around a stone. Already pulling back, already retreating, already gone.

Strike and vanish. Won't stay for a trade. Hit and run.

Can't win like this.

Six's left hand is still holding the sword. His right hand is free. He flexes it. Cold already—not the outside cold. The inside cold. The hum in his chest is building. The lightning wants out. But the iron in the bridge will scatter it. The rhythm will be hard to find. Harder to hold.

He finds it anyway.

The tap starts on his sword hilt. Two fingers. Three. A beat borrowed from the river below—fast water, steady current. His breathing slows. The world narrows. The lanterns don't matter. The cold doesn't matter. Dreya doesn't matter. Only the beat. Only the target.

The man is ten feet away. Pausing. Resetting.

Six lets the lightning in.

A current through his legs. Brief. Controlled. His fingers go cold—then colder. Skin tightening across the knuckles. He shoves the feeling aside.

The world blurs.

He closes the distance in a breath. The man's eyes—visible now, pale, startled—widen. He didn't expect speed. He throws a needle. Too slow. Six is inside his reach. Sword coming up.

The man drops.

Not wounded. Choosing. He lets himself fall off the railing, twisting in the air, and catches the bridge's underside. The beams swallow him. Gone. Just darkness and the sound of hands and feet moving through the ironwork. Fast. Confident. He's done this before.

Six stops at the railing. Looks down. Water. Black. Cold. Moving fast toward the Docks.

He can't follow through the beams. Too narrow for his shoulders. Too dark to see. The man knows this territory. Six doesn't.

Dreya is on the ground behind him. Paralyzed. Dying, maybe. The darkness has spread to her shoulder now. Her breathing is shallow. Her eyes, still furious, find his.

Can't win. Not here. Not like this.

He makes the choice without making it.

Three strides to Dreya. He scoops her up—rigid body, locked limbs, dead weight—and in the same motion, with the same momentum, he throws her over the railing.

She can't scream. But her mouth opens. The sound she would have made is written across her face. Indignant. Outraged. *You could have warned me.*

He jumps after her.

The fall is short. The cold is not.

The river hits like a wall. The shock empties his lungs. Black water closes over his head, and the world becomes sound—the roar of the current, the thunder of his own heart, the muffled silence of everything above the surface. He can't see. Can't breathe. Can't find up.

He can't swim.

Never learned. The canals in the Warrens were too shallow, too foul. The river was a thing you crossed by bridge. He'd watched swimmers from the banks—arms and legs in some pattern he couldn't make his own body do. His coat drags him down. His sword pulls at his back. The cold is everywhere. Not the inside cold. The outside cold. River cold.

His lungs burn.

Then something touches his face.

Not water. Air.

A bubble forms around his head. Clear. Cool. The water presses against its edges, but it holds. He gasps—air, real air—and his vision clears.

Dreya is beside him.

She's still paralyzed. Her body is a rigid shape in the dark water, arms locked, legs frozen. But her eyes are closed now. Concentrating. Her lips move, slow and deliberate, shaping a spell around the edges of whatever the toxin has done to her. The air bubble pulses with each syllable. Holds. Holds.

She cast through paralysis.

They drift downstream. The bridge's shadow covers them. The current carries them toward the Docks, away from the Archive, away from the man on the beams. Six can't swim, but he doesn't need to. The river does the work. Dreya's bubble does the rest.

She saved him.

He didn't ask her to.

He files that away.

---

The Docks stink of fish and tar.

Six drags Dreya onto a stone embankment below a shuttered warehouse. The air bubble pops when they break the surface—a soft, wet exhale—and then it's just night air and river water and the sound of both of them gasping.

Dreya coughs first. She can move again—the paralysis fading, receding from her limbs like a tide pulling back. Her fingers twitch. Her legs spasm. She rolls onto her side and vomits river water onto the stone.

Six lies on his back. Breathing. Waiting for his heart to find its rhythm.

The cold in his fingers is still there. Deeper now. The river made it worse—water leaches heat faster than air—and his coat is soaked through. He needs warmth. A fire. A dry room. Soon.

Dreya pushes herself upright. Her braid has come completely undone. Her robes are plastered to her body. The bolt in her thigh has shifted—still embedded, but the angle is different now. Blood seeps fresh around the shaft.

She looks at her arm where the needle went in. The darkness is fading. The veins are still visible, black threads under pale skin, but receding. Slower than they spread. But receding.

She clears her throat.

"Fascinating," she says.

Six turns his head to look at her.

"The neurotoxin appears to be a modified tetrodotoxin derivative. The paralysis mechanism targets voltage-gated sodium channels, which explains the rapid onset and the spiral visual distortion—I'm seeing spirals, by the way, everything is spiraling slightly to the left, which is consistent with occipital lobe involvement. The fact that I could still cast suggests the toxin doesn't cross the blood-brain barrier uniformly. The motor cortex must be affected before the speech centers, though that's unusual. Most neurotoxins follow a descending pattern—eyelids first, then throat, then limbs. This one started in my arm and spread inward. That's not standard. That's engineered."

She pauses. Takes a breath. Her voice is raw from river water.

"My vision is blurring in a spiral pattern. I mentioned that, yes? It's quite pronounced now. Everything looks like a seashell."

Six sits up. Water runs off his coat in streams. He checks his sword—still in its sheath. Checks the book—still in his coat, wet but intact. The leather has held. The seals might survive.

Dreya is still talking.

"—which suggests the toxin was designed for capture rather than elimination. If they wanted me dead, they'd have used something faster. A cardiac glycoside, perhaps, or a straight paralytic at a higher dose. This is a retrieval toxin. They wanted me alive. Which means they want information. Or leverage. Or both."

Six stands. His legs hold. The cold in his fingers is manageable. The beat in his chest is steady.

He looks down at Dreya. Soaked. Shivering. Bleeding. Still talking.

The corner of his mouth moves. Not a smile. Almost.

She saved him. She cast through paralysis. She's still analyzing the poison while her leg drips blood onto the stone.

He reaches down and pulls her up. She sways. Leans into him.

"The Docks," she says. "We should find shelter. I know a place. Well, I've read about a place. A safe house near the old customs office. It's supposed to be abandoned, but the Academy keeps a few properties for field researchers. There should be supplies. Bandages. Possibly dry clothing. The bandages are the priority, obviously, because this bolt needs to come out, and I'd prefer not to bleed to death in a warehouse district. The death part, specifically. I'd prefer to avoid that."

Six doesn't answer. He starts walking. She limps beside him, one arm over his shoulder, still talking.

Behind them, the river runs dark and fast. The bridge is a distant shape against the black sky. No one follows. The man on the beams doesn't swim.

But Six knows: the kind that strikes from hiding doesn't give up. Repositions. Waits. Finds another bridge.

They'll see him again.
