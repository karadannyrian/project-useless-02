**Chapter 1: The Archive Basement**

---

Sword connects before the sound reaches him.

The impact shivers up through the steel, into his hands, into his teeth. A wrong note. The construct's joint doesn't shear—it buckles, rust grinding against rust, the whole armature shuddering. Half a heartbeat to decide. Six slides the blade free and steps back, weight on the rear foot, tip up, watching.

The guardian fills the Archive basement's center. Twelve feet of God-Fall iron, scabbed with corrosion, its torso a cage of ribs that housed something once. Now it houses nothing. The crystal that animated it is dead or dying—the glow in its chest flickers like a candle in a draft. But dead gods leave dangerous scraps.

It lurches.

Not toward him. Wrong. The construct's left knee folds without warning, geometry collapsing, and it topples sideways with a shriek of tortured metal. The floor takes the impact. The floor loses. Stone cracks in a spiderweb, and something below that—something hollow—punches through.

Six tilts his head.

The guardian had been standing on a safe.

He circles the wreckage, sword still up, watching the glow in the construct's chest sputter and die. The light goes out. The hum in his teeth—the buzz of old magic, a frequency iron always carries—fades to nothing. Dead. He lowers the blade.

Dust settles. Late-afternoon light leaks through a narrow window high on the basement wall, the kind of pale, grudging light that means the sun is already dropping toward the rooftops. He's been underground maybe an hour. The Sentinel had found him that morning, near the canal, coin in hand and few words. *Basement guardian. Old. Should be quiet.* Should be.

The safe is a floor vault, stone-lipped and iron-banded, cracked open like split wood. Inside: one object. A small book, leather-bound, no title on the cover. Six hooks it with two fingers and lifts it out. The leather is dry but sound. Old. Cared for, then forgotten.

He opens it.

Imperial seals. Double-eagle wax, pressed into the first page, the kind merchants in the Shaded Market flash when they want you to know they're protected. The words beneath them swim. His eyes track across the script—formal, dense, the letters curling like they're proud of themselves—and he catches maybe one word in five. A date. A name he doesn't recognize. A number with too many digits.

He doesn't understand it. That's fine. He doesn't need to. What matters is the seal. Seals mean value. Value means coin, or leverage, or both. He closes the book and tucks it inside his coat.

From above: a footstep.

Six goes still.

Not the Watch. The Watch walks with weight, with the clank of issued boots on stone, with the casual noise of men who belong somewhere. These footsteps are different. Soft. Deliberate. Shoes, not boots. They pause between steps. They listen.

Three sets. Spreading out.

Six knows this pattern. He's used it himself. Room-by-room search, coordinated, each footstep placed where the last one wasn't. Whoever's up there is trained.

The basement has one exit. A narrow stairwell up to the Archive's main floor. He moves toward it, sword sheathed across his back, the book a small weight against his ribs. His fingers find the hilt over his shoulder. Not drawing. Just checking.

The stairs are stone. His boots are quiet. He moves the way he learned to move—weight on the outer edge, rolling through the toe—and makes less sound than the building settling around him.

He emerges into the Archive's ground level.

The room is a ruin of toppled shelves and scattered paper. Someone knocked over a lantern; oil has pooled and guttered out, leaving a black smear across the floorboards. The air tastes like dust and dry paper. And blood. Fresh.

He sees her before she sees him.

Pinned behind a collapsed bookshelf near the main desk. Young. Early twenties, maybe. Dark braid coming undone. Academy robes—not a student, the trim is wrong, a researcher's markings—torn at the shoulder. A crossbow bolt buried in her thigh, just above the knee, the shaft jutting at an angle that says it's seated deep in muscle.

She's trying to cast.

Her lips move. No sound. Her fingers shape a spell—air, the way dust lifts and spins around her palm—but it won't catch. The magic fizzles, scatters, dies. Her hands shook. The dust settled. She couldn't hold it.

He watches her fail twice more. Her jaw tightens. She doesn't scream. She doesn't cry. She just resets her fingers and tries again.

He stayed where he was.

Their eyes meet.

She freezes. Not the freeze of prey—the freeze of someone running calculations. Her gaze flicks to his height, his sword, his coat. Recognition lands.

She mouths it: *There are three of them.*

Six assesses.

Bolt in the leg. Blood on the floor. Academy robes. Still trying to cast.

He could leave. The stairwell back down to the basement, wait for the footsteps to pass, slip out the back. He's done it before. The job was the construct. The construct is dead. The contract didn't say anything about stranded researchers.

He doesn't leave.

He crosses the room in four long strides and crouches beside her. Up close, the bolt wound is worse than it looked—blood has soaked through her robes and pooled on the floor beneath her thigh. She's been bleeding for a while. Her skin is pale. Her breathing is shallow. Shock, soon, if she doesn't get help.

She looks up at him and speaks.

"Oh, you're the one." Quiet. Almost a whisper. "The mercenary. The tall one. I've read about you. Well, not read—there aren't files, exactly—but I've heard accounts. The lightning kinetics. Self-taught. Fascinating, really. Most mages with your raw power burn out before they find control, but you've developed a rhythm-based focus, haven't you? I noticed the tapping. It's quite elegant—"

He shrugs.

It's a single motion. Shoulders up, shoulders down. It says: *Now is not the time.* It says: *You're bleeding.* It says: *Be quiet.*

She doesn't take the hint. But she does lower her voice further.

"There's a back way. Behind the main desk. A service corridor. It leads to the old shipping annex. They haven't found it yet."

Six looks at her leg. At the blood. At the bolt. Then back at her face.

She reads the question in his expression. "I can walk."

She cannot walk.

He doesn't argue. He just watches her try to stand, watches her leg buckle, watches her catch herself on the shelf before she hits the floor. Her face goes white. Her breath hisses through her teeth. But she doesn't make a sound.

Still interesting.

Six reaches down and pulls her arm across his shoulders. She's light. The robes hide how thin she is—a body built on library meals and forgotten dinners. She leans into him, good leg taking what weight it can, and together they move toward the desk.

Above them, the footsteps pause.

Then change direction.

They're heading for the stairs.
