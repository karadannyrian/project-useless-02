**Chapter 9: The Canal**

---

They'd stayed in the alley until Dreya's silence became fever and her fever became words that didn't make sense.

At first she'd just breathed. Forehead pressed to her knees. Shoulders shaking, then still. Six had watched the street and let her have the quiet. She'd earned it. They both had.

But the quiet stretched. The shaking stopped and the heat climbed and when he'd touched her forehead—just a brush of his knuckles against her temple—her skin had been dry and wrong and burning. She'd mumbled something about a library. About a restricted section. About a book she'd never finished.

That was when he'd pulled her up. Half-carried her to the water's edge. Found the barge moored at the cargo dock, its hull low and dark, and slipped them both underneath.

Now he floated on his back, one arm hooked under her shoulders, the other trailing in the water to steer. The barge above them was a wooden ceiling—old oak, tar-sealed, the hull black with years of canal filth. It hid them from the docks. From the Watch. From anyone looking down.

The water was cold. Not the inside cold—the outside cold. Canal cold. It seeped through his coat and into his bones and found the inside cold already there, already settled, and made itself at home. His fingers were still numb from the chase. His lips were still pale. He could feel the shivering now, low and constant, a tremor that started in his chest and spread outward.

He ignored it.

Dreya was burning.

Her skin was hot against his arm—fever-hot, infection-hot, the kind of hot that meant the wound in her shoulder was worse than it looked. The force bolt had torn something. Not broken bone—she could move her arm, she'd climbed with it—but torn tissue, maybe, or burst a vessel. The water around her was faintly pink where her blood seeped through the bandage.

She was talking.

Not to him. Not to anyone. Just talking, the words spilling out in fragments, in pieces, in the broken rhythm of fever-sleep. Her voice was thin. Cracked. Nothing like her lecture voice. Nothing like the voice that explained architecture and neurotoxins and beam dimensions. This was smaller. Older.

"—the library was cold. Always cold. The Academy kept it cold to preserve the manuscripts. I didn't mind. I wore three layers. Four, in winter. The librarian said I'd freeze to death and no one would find me for days. He was joking. I think he was joking. I read the entire restricted section in one semester. Not the dangerous books. Just the old ones. The ones no one wanted anymore. They were still good books. They were still—"

She stopped. Her head lolled against his shoulder. Her breath came in short, wet gasps.

Six kept them moving. The barge creaked above them. Water dripped from the hull. Somewhere on the dock, a bell rang twice. Night watch. Far off.

"—my uncle said it was for the family. Everything was for the family. He had a speech. He always had a speech. 'The honor of House Castellan demands action.' I was in the library when he did it. I was always in the library. I didn't know. I didn't—they came to the Academy. Three men in Council livery. They pulled me out of a lecture. Third-year elemental kinetics. I never finished that course. I never—"

She coughed. Wet. Wrong.

"The Council didn't charge me. No evidence. I was in the library. I was always in the library. But it didn't matter. My name was on the registry. House Castellan. Guilt by association. They didn't have to prove anything. They just had to wait. And the other students—they knew. They didn't say anything. They didn't have to. They just stopped sitting next to me. Stopped inviting me to study groups. Stopped—"

Her voice broke. She didn't seem to notice.

"I left before they could make me leave. I thought that would help. I thought if I wasn't there, they'd forget. But they didn't forget. They exiled my whole house. My mother. My father. My brother. I don't know where they are. I don't—it's been two years. Two years and three months and—and I don't know if they're alive. I don't know if—"

Six floated. Listened. Didn't speak.

"Aunt Mira," Dreya said.

Her voice changed. Softer. Younger.

"Aunt Mira used to bring me books. From the capital. From Frostmark, even. She traveled everywhere and she always brought me books. She said—she said I'd read the whole world before I saw it. She was the only one who understood. The only one who didn't think I was strange. 'You're not strange, little bird. You're just hungry.' That's what she called me. Little bird. I haven't—I don't know where she went. After the exile. She disappeared. She just—she just disappeared and no one will tell me—no one will—"

She was crying.

Not sobbing. Not making sound. The tears just ran down her face and mixed with the canal water and she didn't seem to know they were there.

Six kept his arm under her shoulders. Kept floating.

"Aunt Mira," she said again. Quieter. Like a prayer. Like a wound. "I'm sorry. I'm sorry I wasn't there. I'm sorry I was in the library. I'm always in the library. I'm sorry—"

She faded. The words trailed into murmurs, into breath, into nothing.

The barge creaked. The water lapped. Six floated in the dark and felt the heat of her skin against his arm and the cold of the canal against his legs and the shivering in his chest that wouldn't stop.

He stored what she'd said. Didn't know why. Didn't examine it.

---

The canal boat was moored in a side channel near the old ropeworks.

It wasn't much—a narrow vessel, flat-bottomed, built for cargo not comfort. The paint was peeling. The ropes were frayed. But the hull was sound and the cabin was dry and there was a stove in the corner and a bunk against the wall and a locker that might have medicine inside.

Six knew the boat. Knew the man who owned it. A smuggler named Jereth who'd hired him for muscle work a few times, years ago. Jereth wasn't here. The boat was dark. Had been dark for a while—the ropes were frayed from neglect, the deck was thick with grime. Dead or missing or just moved on. Didn't matter.

Six broke the lock. It was cheap iron. Rusted. It gave with one twist of his knife.

Inside: a single cabin. Low ceiling. The bunk was unmade. The stove was cold. The locker held exactly what he'd hoped for—bandages, a jar of salve, a bottle of something that smelled like antiseptic and burned when he tested it on his own skin. No fever tea. The salve would have to do.

He laid Dreya on the bunk. She didn't wake. Didn't stir. Her skin was still hot. Still burning. But her breathing was steadier now—slower, deeper. The delirium had passed. Or gone underground.

He lit the stove. The kindling caught on the first try. The wood caught on the second. The fire was small, but it was fire. Heat filled the cabin by inches.

He cleaned her wounds. The shoulder first—the force bolt had split the skin, not deep, but ragged. He packed it with salve. Bandaged it tight. Then the thigh—the bolt wound was still clean, still healing, the infection beaten back by Yanna's medicine. He changed the dressing anyway.

She didn't wake.

When he was done, he sat against the cabin wall. His sword across his knees. His coat dripping canal water onto the floor. His eyes on the door.

She slept.

---

Twelve hours.

He counted them by the bells from the dock—every hour, the watchman's bell, two tones, then the number of the hour. He counted them by the fire, feeding it when it guttered, watching the wood turn to embers turn to ash. He counted them by her breathing. In. Out. In. Out. Slower than before. Deeper.

She didn't talk. Didn't call for anyone. Didn't apologize for being in the library.

Just slept.

At some point, he slept too. Sitting up. Sword still across his knees. The fire burned down to embers and he let it.

When he woke, she was still breathing. Still alive.

He could have left her on the barge.

She was dead weight. Dead weight in the water, dead weight in his arms, a scholar who couldn't walk and couldn't cast and couldn't stop talking except when she did. She'd screamed when the force bolt hit. She'd cried in her sleep for an aunt who'd disappeared. She carried guilt that didn't belong to her and a ledger that belonged to everyone and a fever that should have killed her two days ago.

She was a liability. She was also the only reason he knew about Lot 7. About Ashford. About the siege engines under the textile warehouse. She was useful.

That was why he stayed.

He was almost sure of it.

He fed the fire. Watched the door. Listened to her breathe.

Didn't examine the almost.
