**Chapter 3: Dockside Warehouse**

---

The loading dock is slimy with old fish oil and river scum. Six hauls himself up one-handed, the other arm dragging Dreya by the back of her robes. She coughs. Spits. Tries to stand and fails twice before her good leg holds.

"This is disgusting," she says.

Six doesn't answer. He's scanning the dock. Warehouses in both directions. Most are dark. A few have night watch lanterns burning outside their doors—not those. He needs dark. Empty. Something with more than one exit.

"The odor composition is remarkable. Decomposing fish oil, primarily, but there's an undercurrent of—" She sniffs. Winces. "—bilge water and something I believe is fermenting grain. Which shouldn't be possible in a fish-packing facility. Cross-contamination of this magnitude suggests either criminal negligence or a deliberate disregard for basic sanitation protocols."

She's shivering. Hard enough that her teeth click between words.

"—the third possibility is that this warehouse hasn't been used for fish in years and the smell is simply residual organic matter trapped in the floorboards. Which would make the grain smell even more puzzling, unless the building was repurposed at some point without proper cleaning, which, given the state of the Docks district, is statistically—"

Six puts his hand over her mouth.

She goes still. Her eyes find his. Questioning, not frightened.

He tilts his head. Listening.

The dock creaks. Water laps against the pilings below. Somewhere distant, a night watchman's bell rings twice. Then three times.

Then the warehouse wall explodes.

Not an explosion. A man. Walking through the wall. The rotten wood tears like wet paper, and he steps through the hole he's made like he's stepping through a door. Splinters rain onto the loading dock. Dust blooms in the lantern light.

He's big. Not tall—Six is taller—but thick. The kind of thick that comes from wearing full plate armor and never taking it off. Cold-iron plate. Every surface. The chest. The arms. The legs. Even the gauntlets are iron, the fingers articulated with so little gap between the plates that Six can't see where one ends and the next begins.

The armor hums.

Six feels it in his teeth. A frequency he knows. Not the scattered buzz of iron in walls, in beams, in bridge mortar. This is concentrated. A void where his lightning should be. The hum is the sound of his magic having nowhere to go.

Dreya feels it too. She sucks in a breath. "That's—"

He pushes her behind him.

The armored man stops in the center of the loading dock. In his right hand, a hammer. Not a warhammer. A maul. Two-handed, iron-headed, the haft wrapped in worn leather. In his left, a chain. Fifteen feet of iron links, coiled like a snake.

He doesn't speak.

Six draws his sword.

The man watches. The slit in his helmet is dark. Six can't see eyes. Can't read expression. Just the helm tilting slightly, like a dog that's heard a strange sound.

Then he moves.

Not fast. He doesn't need to be fast. He walks forward, and every step is deliberate, and the dock shudders under his weight. The hammer comes up. The chain stays coiled.

Six knows this type. He's fought armored men before. Watch enforcers in plate, mercenaries in mail, the occasional bounty hunter who thought steel skin made him invincible. Armor has joints. Gaps. Places where the body has to bend and the metal has to let it.

This armor doesn't.

He swings. High. Testing. The blade connects with the pauldron and scrapes off with a sound like a nail dragged across stone. No dent. No scratch. The iron doesn't even ring—it absorbs the impact, swallows it, the way deep water swallows a thrown stone.

Wrong. That's wrong.

The hammer answers.

Six ducks. The maul passes through the space his head occupied and keeps going, and the man doesn't overbalance, doesn't stumble, doesn't even slow. The follow-through is controlled. Practiced. He's done this thousands of times.

Six backs up. The dock is too narrow. One wrong step and he's in the river. Dreya is behind him, somewhere near the warehouse wall, and he can hear her moving—dragging herself toward cover, he hopes, not trying to cast, please don't try to cast—

She tries to cast.

The air twists. Six feels the pressure change—a sudden drop, like a door opening onto a storm. An air blade. Dreya's hand is extended, her fingers shaping the spell, and the blade is barely visible, just a shimmer, a ripple, a suggestion of something sharp.

It hits the man's helmet.

He stops walking.

The spell scratches across the iron like a thrown pebble. It doesn't cut. It doesn't dent. It makes a sound so small—a whisper of displaced air—that Six almost misses it.

The man turns his head toward Dreya.

And laughs.

Low. Rumbling. A chuckle that starts in his chest and vibrates through the armor. It's the first sound he's made. Not cruel. Not warm. Just... amused. The way a mountain might be amused if a bird flew into it.

Dreya's face goes pale. Not from fear—from the Toll. Six can see it hitting her. A twitch at her temple. A squint, like the lantern light is suddenly too bright. Head pain. Bad, from the look of her.

"His armor should have joints," she says. Her voice is thinner now. Strained. "Every armor has joints. The laws of metallurgy require—"

The man takes a step toward her.

Six moves.

He doesn't think. The beat is already there—not tapped, not yet, but felt. The rhythm of the river under the dock. The rhythm of the man's footsteps. The rhythm of Dreya's breathing, fast and shallow. He slots himself into the gap between one footstep and the next and swings.

The blade hits the helmet.

Nothing. The iron swallows it.

He swings again. The gorget. Nothing.

The man turns back to Six. Slow. Patient. The hammer comes around in a horizontal arc, and Six has to drop to one knee to get under it. The wind of its passing ruffles his hair.

Can't use lightning. The armor grounds everything. Sword only.

His jaw tightens. Grip adjusts.

The math is bad. He's faster. The man is invulnerable. Speed doesn't matter if nothing hurts him. Reach doesn't matter if nothing cuts. Every exchange costs Six energy. Every exchange costs the man nothing.

He glances at Dreya.

She's propped against a crate near the warehouse wall. Her hands are working at her thigh—trying to rebandage the bolt wound. The river water soaked through the old wrap. Blood is seeping down her leg in thin rivulets. She's talking while she works, probably quoting anatomy texts, probably getting it wrong.

She's getting it wrong.

The bandage is slipping. She's trying to tie it one-handed, and the knot won't hold, and the angle is wrong for pressure. You have to wrap above the wound first. Tight. Then down. She's wrapping below. The blood won't stop that way.

The man swings again.

Six sidesteps. The maul hits the dock and splinters a plank. The dock groans. Water sloshes below.

He needs an opening. He needs time. He needs the man off-balance.

The crates.

A stack of them near the loading door. Barrel crates. Old, but still heavy. Six has tipped crates before. In the Warrens, running from bigger kids. In the Shaded Market, when a deal went wrong. He knows where to hit. Knows how fast they fall. Knows how much noise they make when they go.

He feints left. The man follows. Six pivots right, puts a crate between them, and kicks.

The stack topples.

Barrels tumble. Salted fish, from the smell—old stock, abandoned when the warehouse closed. They crash down in a wave of rotten wood and brine, and the man is underneath them, and the sound he makes is not a laugh this time. It's a roar. Frustration, not pain. The barrels won't hold him. They'll buy seconds.

Six crosses to Dreya in three strides.

"The bandage," she says. "I know what I'm doing. The femoral artery runs along the medial aspect of the thigh, and pressure should be applied—"

He's already undoing her knot.

She blinks. "What are you—I was applying pressure—"

He wraps above the wound. Tight. Twice around. Then down, overlapping each pass by half the width. The knot is simple—two throws, pull, one more throw. He learned it from a canal medic who owed Mara a favor. He was twelve. He still remembers.

The bleeding slows.

Dreya stares at him. "The textbook says—"

Wrong.

He doesn't say it. He stands up, turns back toward the dock, and the crates are already moving. The man is shoving through them, barrels rolling aside, and his hammer is in both hands now. The chain is still coiled. He hasn't used it yet.

He's been playing.

Six feels the cold settle in his chest. Not the inside cold. Just cold.

The man takes a step forward.

Six jerks his head toward the far door. The gesture says: run.

Dreya doesn't argue. She pushes herself up, using the crate for support, and starts limping toward the back of the warehouse. Fast as she can. Which isn't fast.

Six backs up. Sword still raised. Watching the man. Watching the hammer.

The man doesn't chase.

He just stands there, waist-deep in broken barrels, and watches them go. The slit in his helmet is dark. The armor hums. The laugh is gone.

Six turns and runs.

Behind him, the roar comes again. Not frustration this time. Something else. Something that sounds like: we'll finish this later.

---

The alley behind the warehouse is narrow and dark. Dreya is leaning against a wall, clutching her thigh, breathing hard. The head pain has settled in—she's squinting against the dark like it's too bright, one hand pressed to her temple.

"That," she says, "was a level three abjuration failure. His armor should have joints. Every armor has joints. Why doesn't his armor have joints?"

Six doesn't answer. He's watching the warehouse door. Listening. The man isn't following. Not yet.

He should have killed him.

Not tonight. Not in that fight. But in the first exchange—when the man stepped through the wall, when he was still orienting, when Six had one clean second before the hammer came up—he should have found the gap. Every armor has a gap. He just couldn't see it. He hesitated.

He won't hesitate next time.

Dreya is still talking. "—unless it's not constructed armor at all. Unless it's grown. There are theories about organic iron bonding, but the metabolic cost would be catastrophic. The human body can't process ferrous compounds at that concentration without—"

Six pushes off the wall and starts walking.

She limps after him. "Where are we going? I still know where the safe house is. Theoretically. It's near the old customs office. Two streets over. Possibly three. The map I saw was seventeen years out of date, but buildings don't move, generally speaking, unless there's been a sinkhole or a demolition or—"

He keeps walking.

She keeps talking.

Behind them, the warehouse is silent. The man in iron isn't coming. But Six can still feel the hum in his teeth. The void where his lightning should be. The weight of an enemy he doesn't know how to hurt.

He'll figure it out.

He always does.
