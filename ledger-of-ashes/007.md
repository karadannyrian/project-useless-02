**Chapter 7: The Hidden Fortress**

---

The ledger was words. Six wanted stone and iron.

He'd read it—slowly, painfully, Dreya filling in the words he stumbled over—while the fever tea steeped and the poultice dried on her wound. Shipments. Payments. Names. One name kept appearing. A location. Lot 7. A warehouse in the textile district. The manifest listed looms and dye vats and wool bales. The cost breakdown didn't add up. The tonnage was wrong. Stone. Iron. Steel beams. More than any textile operation needed.

Dreya had read over his shoulder, squinting through the fever haze. "The beam dimensions seem off by at least three inches. Unless they're reinforcing the foundation for something much heavier than a loom floor—"

She'd been talking for an hour. The fever was down, the tea had helped, but the bolt was still in her leg. The poultice had drawn some of the infection out—the skin around the wound was less red, less hot—but the bolt was still in her leg. Had been in her leg since the Archive basement. Two days. Maybe three. He'd lost track.

He knelt beside her. Pulled his knife.

She stopped mid-sentence. Blinked at him. "What?"

The blade was clean. He'd boiled it while she slept—water from the canteen, heated over the stove, the steel sitting in the bubbling water until it was too hot to touch. He'd let it cool. Now it was ready.

He met her eyes. Tilted his head toward her thigh. The bolt. The knife.

She understood.

"Oh," she said. Her voice was smaller than usual. "Yes. That should come out. The risk of secondary infection increases exponentially the longer a foreign body remains in the wound. The bolt head is barbed, of course—standard Imperial design, they're meant to stay in—so you'll have to cut around it. The tissue will have started adhering to the shaft. If you sever the lateral femoral cutaneous nerve, I'll lose sensation in the outer thigh. If you hit the femoral artery, I'll bleed out in under two minutes. The artery runs through the femoral triangle, which is bounded by the inguinal ligament superiorly, the sartorius laterally, and the adductor longus medially. The bolt is approximately two inches medial to the sartorius, which means—"

She was scared.

She wasn't saying it. Wasn't stopping talking long enough to feel it. But her hands were gripping the rags beneath her. Her knuckles were white.

Six waited. Knife in one hand. The other resting on her knee. Not pressing. Just there.

She stopped talking.

He held her gaze for a beat. One beat. Then he looked down at the wound. The bolt. The knife.

He cut.

The blade opened the wound channel—a single clean incision along the shaft of the bolt. Blood welled. Dark. Venous. Not arterial. Good. The tissue around the bolt was angry and red, but not black. The poultice had done its work. No rot. No poison.

Dreya's breath hissed through her teeth. Her whole body went rigid. But she didn't scream. Didn't move. Didn't look away.

He worked the blade deeper. Found the barb. Cut around it—one small incision, then another. His fingers were still cold from the Toll two days ago, still a little slow, but they knew this work. They'd done it before. On himself. On others. The Warrens taught many things. Wound care was one of them.

"Sartorius," Dreya whispered. Her voice was strained. Tight. "You're lateral to the sartorius. That's good. That's—" She sucked in a breath. "—that's very good, actually. The artery is medial. You're clear. You're—"

He pulled the bolt free.

It came out in one motion—the barb, the shaft, the head. The sound it made was wet and wrong. Blood followed, a fresh rush of it, but Six already had the poultice ready. He pressed it hard against the wound. Held it there. Counted to thirty.

Dreya's breath came in short, sharp gasps. Her eyes were wet. But she was still looking at him. Still not screaming.

"Fascinating," she said. Her voice cracked on the second syllable. "The pain is—it's quite remarkable, actually. I've read about pain. Studied it. The nociceptive pathways, the—the substance P release, the—" She laughed. A short, shaky sound. "It's different. Experiencing it. Than reading about it."

He kept pressure on the wound. Watched her face. The color was coming back—slowly, in patches.

"I'm going to pass out now," she said. Very calmly. "I think that would be best. Medically speaking. Vasovagal syncope. Perfectly normal response to—to acute pain and blood loss and—"

She didn't pass out. She stayed conscious. Stayed looking at him. Her hand found his wrist—the one holding the poultice—and gripped it. Hard.

She didn't say anything else. Neither did he.

When the bleeding slowed, he finished dressing the wound. Fresh bandage. Tight. Clean. The bolt sat on the floor beside him—an ugly thing, iron-headed, the barb still slick with her blood. He'd clean it later. Keep it. Might be useful.

Dreya watched him work. She wasn't talking. The silence was different from before. Not the silence of fear. Not the silence of exhaustion. Something else.

When he was done, she tested her leg. Flexed her foot. Winced.

"The beam dimensions," she said. Her voice was hoarse but steadier. "I was talking about the beam dimensions. They were off by at least three inches. Unless they're reinforcing the foundation for something much heavier than a loom floor—"

He almost smiled.

Almost.

---

Now they moved through the upper tunnels, toward the district where the Warrens met the surface. Dreya limped beside him. The limp was better now—the bolt was out, the pressure was gone, the healing could start. She was still slow. Still pale. But she was moving.

"For science," she'd said when he'd told her the plan. "And also because I want to know if the construction manifests were accurate. Which they won't be. They're never accurate. I once audited an Academy building project where the contractor had skimmed thirty percent of the stone budget and replaced it with—"

She was still talking. He let her. The sound filled the tunnel. Better than the drip of water. Better than the silence.

---

Lot 7 was a textile warehouse.

It looked like a textile warehouse. Smelled like one. Wool and dye and the sharp chemical bite of mordant. The upper floors were lit—night shift workers moving between looms, their shadows crossing the windows. A legitimate operation. That was the point.

Six and Dreya watched from a drainage culvert across the alley. The entrance was guarded. Two men. Not warehouse workers. They stood like soldiers.

"Side door," Dreya whispered. "Loading dock. It'll be locked, but the lock will be mechanical. I can't pick it, but if you can get it open without noise—"

Six was already moving.

The lock was old iron. Rusted. He broke it with a twist of his knife and caught the pieces before they hit the ground. The door swung inward. Darkness. Stacks of wool bales. The air was thick with lanolin and dust.

Dreya slipped in behind him. Closed the door.

The warehouse floor was a maze of crates and bales. Six moved through it without sound. Dreya followed, her limp making her slower, her breathing louder than he'd like. But she was quiet. Even she knew when to be quiet.

"The false wall," she breathed. "It should be on the east side. The manifests showed more square footage than the building footprint allows. Unless there's an interior room that doesn't appear on the external measurements—"

She found it.

Behind a rack of uncut wool bolts, the wall was wrong. The mortar was too new. The stone didn't match the surrounding foundation. Dreya ran her fingers along the seam. Pressed something. A catch clicked.

The wall swung inward.

Stairs. Going down.

The cold-iron hum hit Six before he saw anything. A buzz in his teeth that grew stronger with every step. Not the scattered buzz of iron nails and old tools. This was concentrated. Deliberate. A void where magic should be. The walls below were lined with cold iron. Every surface. Ceiling. Floor. Someone had paid a fortune to make this basement a dead zone.

No lightning. No air blades. No spells. Just steel.

Six descended anyway.

---

The staircase opened into a bunker.

Siege engines. Six of them. Half-built, their iron frames crouched in the dark like sleeping beasts. Torsion mechanisms. Launch cradles. Piles of stone shot stacked against the walls. Enough to break a city gate. Enough to break several.

Beyond them: barracks. Bunks for fifty soldiers. Maybe more. Footlockers. Weapon racks. The air smelled of oiled steel and old sweat. Someone had been living here. Recently.

Beyond that: an armory.

Racks of swords. Spears. Crossbows. Crates of quarrels. Armor stands holding plate that gleamed in the low lantern light. Not city watch gear. Military. Standardized. The kind of equipment that came from Imperial forges.

Six counted. Stored the numbers away. The ledger hadn't exaggerated. If anything, it had understated.

Voices.

He froze. Dreya froze behind him. Her hand found his arm. Squeezed once.

They pressed against the wall. The cold iron hummed against Six's back. In front of them, through a gap in the armory shelving, the bunker opened into a command room. Maps on a table. A lantern burning. Two figures.

One was the man in iron.

He was out of his armor—just a man now, broad-shouldered, grey-haired, his face scarred and weathered. He wore a plain tunic. His hands were bare. He looked smaller without the plate. Not small. Just human.

The other figure was a woman.

She wore Accordance council robes. Deep blue. Gold trim. The sash of a Trade Attaché across her chest. She was older—fifty, maybe—with silver hair pulled back in a tight knot. Her face was sharp. Unsmiling. The kind of face that gave orders and expected them to be followed.

Dreya's hand tightened on Six's arm.

"That's Councilor Ashford," she breathed. Her lips were almost touching his ear. "Trade Attaché. She's on the ledger."

Six watched the woman. Memorized her face. The shape of her jaw. The way she stood—weight on both feet, shoulders squared, a woman who'd never had to run from anything. Her eyes were pale blue. Cold. She had a small scar on her left temple. Old. Faded. She wore a signet ring. He couldn't see the seal. Didn't matter. He'd remember the ring.

Ashford. Trade Attaché. Face. Remember.

He stored it. Wouldn't forget.

---

The man in iron was speaking. Six couldn't hear the words—the iron hummed too loud, the distance was too far—but he could read the body. The man gestured at a map. Pointed at something. Ashford nodded. Asked a question. He answered. She didn't like the answer. Her jaw tightened. She spoke. The man inclined his head. Not a bow. An acknowledgment.

She was giving orders.

The man in iron took orders from a councilwoman.

Six watched. Counted. Remembered. Dreya would figure out what it meant.

They stayed for half an hour. Maybe more. Long enough for Six to memorize the layout of the armory. The number of bunks. The weak points in the siege engines—the torsion mechanisms, the launch cradles, the places where a well-placed blow could cripple the whole machine. Long enough to watch Ashford's hands as she pointed at the map.

Then Dreya tugged his sleeve.

Time to go.

They retreated the way they came. Up the stairs. Through the false wall. Through the warehouse floor, past the wool bales, past the dye vats, out the side door. The pieces of the broken lock were still on the ground where Six had left them. No one had noticed.

They didn't speak until they were back in the drainage culvert. Back in the dark. Back where the iron was just old nails and rusted beams and the scattered buzz of a city's bones.

---

Dreya leaned against the culvert wall. Her face was pale again. The bolt was out, but the wound was fresh, and she'd pushed too hard. Too much walking. Too many stairs.

"She's Trade Attaché," she said. Her voice was thin. "Fourth highest position in the Accordance Trade Council. She negotiates trade agreements with the Empire. Tariffs. Embargoes. She's been in office for twelve years. I've seen her speak at Academy functions. She gave a lecture on intercontinental commerce three years ago. I took notes. I have those notes somewhere. She talked about the importance of maintaining diplomatic neutrality between—"

She stopped. Swallowed.

"She's on the ledger," she said. "The ledger says she's been diverting funds to this fortress. For months. Maybe longer. The Trade Council controls the city's import taxes. She's been stealing from her own government to build a siege bunker under a textile warehouse."

Six didn't answer. He was still seeing Ashford's face. The scar. The ring. The way she stood without flinching.

Just the cold. The counting. The memorizing. Facts that might be useful later.

He didn't know if that was strength or a missing piece.

"Come on," Dreya said. She pushed off the wall. Tested her leg. Winced. But the leg held. "We need to get back to the safehouse. I need to write this down. The Trade Council. The siege engines. The beam dimensions were definitely off, by the way. At least three inches. Maybe four. I told you."

She limped down the culvert. Still talking.

Six followed.

Behind them, the warehouse hummed with iron and secrets. Above them, the councilwoman made her plans. Somewhere in the tunnels, the quiet one waited. Somewhere in the city, the man in iron put his armor back on.

None of it mattered yet. They had names. They had faces. They had proof.

That was enough for now.
