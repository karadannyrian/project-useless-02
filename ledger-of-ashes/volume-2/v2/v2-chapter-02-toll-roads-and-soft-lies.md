# Chapter Two — Toll Roads and Soft Lies

-----

She assessed the room in four seconds upon entry: twenty-three occupants, mixed — travelers, local laborers, two merchants by their coats, one ecclesiastical clerk in the corner eating his supper with the detached efficiency of a man who considered inns a professional hazard. Six exits visible. Three doors, two windows large enough to be functional, one that was decorative and sealed. One empty stool at the far end of the bar.

Six went to the stool.

She went to find the merchants.

-----

The brothers — Holvast and Pellery, by the introduction — had three stones they wanted verified before the morning market. She verified them at their table while they watched with the mutual suspicion of people who had been in business together long enough to know each other’s methods. Two genuine, one faceted to look charged when it had been depleted for years. She pointed this out. Holvast looked at Pellery with an expression that suggested this specific deception had been a point of contention between them for some time. Pellery looked at the table.

Two silver coins and a meal voucher.

While she worked, they talked. Merchants talked as a reflex — information was a resource like any other, and sharing it was how you established credit. The Duke’s sun-stone authentication levy had been extended to the Thornfield corridor, apparently: the “Cerdon tariff,” Holvast called it, in the tone of someone pronouncing a medical diagnosis they’d been hoping to avoid. Pellery said the exchange rate had been adjusted accordingly. Holvast said the adjustment was designed to look like compensation when it was in fact an additional extraction. Pellery agreed. Neither of them said what they intended to do with this information, which meant they intended to do nothing, which was the reasonable position when the person setting the levy controlled the roads.

She noted it. Continued working.

-----

Across the room, Six had not moved.

She checked on him periodically — a professional habit, the same instinct that counted exits — and each time he was in exactly the same position: facing forward, drink in hand, occupying the far stool with the concentrated stillness of someone who had decided to be in this specific place and considered the matter settled. The conversation radius around him had been approximately three feet when she arrived. It was now closer to five.

She was watching when the carter arrived.

Large man — the architecture of someone who had spent thirty years loading cargo and considered the physical consequences of this to be someone else’s problem. He crossed to the bar, arrived at the stool situation, and proceeded to communicate his displeasure through the mechanisms of large men who feel the wrong person is occupying a resource they believe is theirs: pointed presence, pointed proximity, a cleared throat deployed as a social instrument.

Six did not respond.

The cleared throat again. Louder.

She attempted to analyze the specific quality of his non-response. It was not absence — it was the presence of something else, a kind of absolute residential calm, like a fixed geographic feature that had no opinion about the weather. He appeared to have noticed the carter the way a cliff face notices the sea: continuously, without concern.

The carter said, loudly, that this was his usual stool.

Six turned his head and looked at him.

She was too far away to read the expression precisely. She observed its effect: twelve seconds of calculation on the carter’s part — a calculation that appeared to involve Six’s height, the specific quality of the attention being directed at him, and some private accounting — and then the carter moved to a different stool. Six looked back at the wall.

She returned her attention to Holvast and Pellery and accepted the coins.

*Either a refined technique,* she thought, *or something more fundamental. Possibly both.* She was genuinely uncertain which was the more instructive interpretation, and the uncertainty itself was instructive.

-----

Later, while finishing a second authentication for a gem-seller who had found her on the merchants’ recommendation, she became aware that Six was looking at her from across the bar.

She looked up. He made a gesture she did not recognize: two fingers raised toward his mouth, then a small circular motion. She ran it against what she had of his grammar — a small and largely unsystematic collection at this stage, derived mostly from observation rather than any intentional study — and found nothing.

He made it again, slightly slower. With the patience of someone simplifying a concept for an audience that was having difficulty.

She mouthed: *Are you hungry?*

He nodded once, with the gravity of someone relaying navigational information.

She held up one finger — *one minute* — and turned back to the gem-seller.

Four minutes later: the gesture again. She held up two fingers without looking at him and continued her conversation. The gem-seller seemed to find this exchange interesting. She did not explain it.

The gesture appeared a third time. A very small muscle in her jaw did something involuntary.

He was doing that on purpose. He was absolutely doing that on purpose. Whatever the gesture meant precisely, it had now appeared three times in approximately twelve minutes, at intervals that corresponded not to genuine hunger but to the natural rhythm of a person checking to see what would happen.

Though.

She was not, in fact, entirely certain of that.

This was the more disconcerting possibility.

She completed the authentication, thanked the gem-seller, accepted payment, and went to inform the bar that two people required food.

Six, when she arrived, received this development with the equanimity of someone whose plan had proceeded as expected. She sat on the adjacent stool and looked at the wall in front of them.

“I am going to need a complete lexicon,” she said. “In writing.”

He said nothing. She had not expected him to.

The food arrived. She ate. He ate his faster, which raised the statistical probability that the gesture had, in fact, indicated genuine hunger, which resolved nothing to her satisfaction and introduced a variable she had not adequately accounted for.

She ordered more bread.

The analysis was not yet complete, and there was nothing like a controlled variable.
