# Chapter Four — The Notation System

-----

The journal was from the waystation — blank, unlined, the kind sold to merchants for ledgers. She had bought it on the same trip that produced the pamphlet. She had been more circumspect about discussing the journal.

She set the notation framework first, before she entered anything: three columns. Physical description. Observed context. Confidence rating, expressed as a percentage, because a binary known/unknown failed to capture the actual epistemological situation, which was more nuanced than that.

The first entry was *look behind you,* which she was now confident in — she had the gesture, had confirmed it through correction, had applied it successfully twice in the last twenty-four hours when she thought she’d heard something on the road. Two-finger extension, specific wrist angle, directed backward over the shoulder. She noted the variant: one degree too far forward equaled a different meaning, approximately hat-related, confidence on her interpretation of the variant: 40%.

That had been satisfying to document.

*How far* was clear — she’d had this one since their first day on the road. Two fingers, flat palm directed toward the destination. She gave it 91%, hedging only because she hadn’t yet confirmed what he did when the destination was unknown rather than distant, and there might be a distinction she hadn’t seen.

*No* had given her trouble initially because she’d confused it with *not yet* and *stop* before she’d accumulated enough instances to triangulate. She wrote the physical form, wrote the three contexts in which she’d observed it, wrote: “strong evidence for the negative reading; moderate evidence for the temporal-delay reading; insufficient instances for the prohibitive reading. Provisional confidence: 78%, subject to revision.” Then she crossed out *subject to revision* because all entries were subject to revision. That was the point. The notation was the live document.

*Wait* had at least three applications: stand still, don’t speak, and one she’d observed once that might have meant *I am thinking and need a moment* or might have meant *wait* in a more general sense. She documented all three and gave the first two high confidence and the third a question mark and the note: *one instance insufficient for categorization. Monitor.*

*Danger* was the clearest of all of them. She was 97% confident. The remaining 3% was professional standard-setting.

Then she arrived at the hunger sign.

She wrote the physical description: two fingers raised toward the mouth, small circular motion. She wrote: *observed context — waystation bar, approximately 20:00, pre-meal.* Then she looked at what she’d written.

The problem was that context was the least helpful data point available, because she couldn’t confirm whether the context had produced the gesture or the gesture had preceded the context. She wrote: *Hypothesis A: genuine nutritional communication (hunger).* She wrote: *Hypothesis B: sarcasm, or deliberate implication of prior failure to attend to basic requirements.* She paused. She wrote: *Confidence split: approximately 47/53 in favor of Hypothesis B, based on timing of third repetition and twelve-minute interval pattern, which exceeds physiological urgency thresholds for a subject of his size and observed metabolic rate.*

Then she wrote: *Note — controlled replication would require inducing reliable hunger state on demand, which introduces significant methodological problems* and stopped, looked at the end of the sentence, and crossed out everything after *problems* and wrote *is inadvisable.*

She looked at this sentence for a moment.

She moved on.

-----

The headache arrived at mid-afternoon, which she registered as a data point before she registered it as a problem. Left temple. Behind the eye socket — not at the surface, deeper, the quality of pressure that announces itself by already being there when you notice it.

She put the journal down.

Her first working hypothesis was Toll residue — accumulated debt, the sympathetic channel pre-triggering from build-up rather than from active expenditure. She’d seen this in the literature and had once experienced something adjacent after a sustained casting week in her third year. The profile fit: pressure rather than sharp, unilateral, building rather than sudden onset.

The treatment protocol for Toll residue was rest and reduced magical activity. She was not currently casting. She pulled her hood forward, angled herself away from the direct afternoon light, closed her eyes for thirty seconds, breathed evenly.

The headache did not respond the way Toll residue responded to rest.

She opened her eyes. Repeated the protocol — a full minute this time, eyes closed, attention redirected away from the sensation.

The headache was the same when she opened her eyes. Not better, not worse. Unchanged — which was not how Toll residue behaved. Toll residue was sensitive to rest. This was not sensitive to anything she was doing.

*Either accumulation threshold is higher than estimated and requires a longer rest period, or —*

The cart slowed.

She looked up. Six was facing forward, reins even, posture unchanged from the last four hours. The horse was moving at approximately two-thirds its previous pace. He had not spoken. Had not signaled. Had not done anything she could point to as a direct response to her condition.

She went through the candidates: something on the road. Something ahead. Terrain she hadn’t noticed. Or — the hypothesis she kept returning to — he had observed some change in her posture, her reduced activity, her unusual orientation away from the afternoon light, and had made an adjustment without requiring an explanation. She could not determine which. She noted the adjustment, noted that he hadn’t asked, and picked up the journal.

*Second hypothesis: something else is happening.*

She wrote it precisely because she didn’t want to. Precision was the point. If her body was generating Toll-profile symptoms without the Toll, that had implications she was not yet prepared to analyze fully, and the responsible course was to document the onset carefully before the implications caught up.

*Onset: mid-afternoon. Location: left temple, posterior to the orbit. Quality: pressure. Duration: ongoing. Response to standard Toll mitigation: none. Accumulated magical activity since last Toll event: none in prior seventy-two hours.*

She looked at the last line for a moment.

The cart moved forward in silence. The headache was present. She kept writing.
