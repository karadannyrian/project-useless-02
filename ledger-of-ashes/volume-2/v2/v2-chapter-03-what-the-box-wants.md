# Chapter Three — What the Box Wants

-----

The camp was quiet enough to hear the grass. Dreya was on the other side of the fire — not asleep, or not entirely: her breathing had the particular pattern of someone performing sleep for an audience that wasn’t watching. He wasn’t watching. He knew anyway.

He unwrapped the box.

The oilcloth fell back and the box sat in his palm — cold iron, which it shouldn’t have been at this temperature, and then, as always once he’d been holding it more than a few seconds, warm. The warmth came in through the palm first. That was familiar.

Tonight it moved.

Up from the palm, past the heel of his hand, into his wrist where the pulse lived. And then — he held still — the warmth and the beat arrived at the same rhythm. Not one responding to the other. Not one leading. Just the same timing, the way two things that have been running separately sometimes find they’ve been going the same pace all along.

He hadn’t done anything to make that happen.

He held it for two breaths. Then he shifted his grip and it was over — warmth again, ordinary warmth, iron no longer pretending to be cold.

*When it gets warm, you’re close.*

Mara’s voice. He had been twenty-four. She hadn’t said close to what, and he hadn’t asked — the kind of question that seemed like it would answer itself eventually, which was what he told himself about most things that required more time than he currently had.

He turned the box over. The warmth was even on every face of it. He noted: the warmth had direction. It wasn’t ambient. It was moving toward something the way a current moves toward somewhere without announcing the destination.

He wrapped the box and put it back under his coat.

-----

His body audit was a nightly habit from the Warrens, when knowing what condition you were in meant knowing what you could do tomorrow. He ran it now. Starts at the hands, works up.

Hands: cold at the knuckles. Faster than before Midden. He hadn’t used the current today and the cold was there anyway — residual now, something that had stopped fully clearing between uses. He flexed both hands once. The cold didn’t change.

Chest: the beat.

He had his own notation for it since the chain descent — not the medical word, which he didn’t know, but his: *the wrong beat.* The skip that corrected too hard and then settled. It happened sometimes at rest now, briefly, arriving and leaving. It had started on the anchor platform and he had categorized it as a cost and continued. He was re-categorizing it now as a cost that was staying around longer than the original assessment.

He noted this. Kept moving.

The rest of the audit came back clean. He put it away and looked at the fire.

-----

Dreya turned over.

Not asleep. He had known. She had known he had known. They left it there, which was the arrangement.

He held up his left hand. Two fingers extended, wrist angled back over the shoulder, the weight of the gesture in the final degree of turn. He held it.

She looked at it. He could see her replicating it in her fingers — running through it, measuring herself against what she’d seen. Then she made the gesture.

The wrist angle was off. One degree too far forward, which moved the meaning from *look behind you* to something adjacent but distinct — closer to *there is a problem with your hat,* which was a less useful signal in most situations.

He made the gesture again, correctly.

She watched. She made it again. Still the angle.

He broke it down: wrist position first, held it. Then the finger extension. Then the final degree of turn. Each component, each pause.

She set her wrist at the angle.

He nodded once.

She added the fingers. The angle held.

He nodded.

She added the turn. The angle held.

He nodded.

She assembled it from the beginning — the whole gesture, the full motion, the wrist angle landing where it was supposed to land.

He tapped once.

She looked at him with the expression she used when she was placing something in a category she hadn’t fully built yet. Then: “I’m going to need a key. A written key. With indications of which degree of variation produces which alternate meaning.”

He tapped twice.

She turned back toward the fire. He heard her breathing change — real this time, the rhythm slowing toward something that wasn’t watching anything.

He stayed awake a while longer.

The beat in his chest kept its rhythm. Slightly wrong. Familiar by now.

He listened to it.
