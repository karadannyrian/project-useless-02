# Chapter 4 — House Varetta's Ghost

**POV:** Dreya  
**Setting:** The town of Duskmere; a mixed Valerian-Imperial market town

---

Duskmere announced itself as a haze of woodsmoke on the horizon, then as a sprawl of slate roofs and bell towers, then as a gate. The gate was stone and iron and guarded by two men in Valerian livery who waved the cart through without stopping it. Dreya noted this with the particular relief of someone who had been holding a breath she hadn't admitted to taking. The border was still two weeks south. This was just a market town. A market town with mixed jurisdiction and a seasonal fair and, according to the merchant pamphlet she had finished that morning, a surprisingly good cheesemonger.

She did not mention the cheesemonger to Six. She was saving it.

The cart rolled through the main thoroughfare, past stalls that sold everything from dyed wool to Imperial-style lanterns to sun-stones displayed under glass like relics. The architecture was hybrid—Valerian white stone on the ground floors, Imperial dark timber on the upper storeys, as if the town had started as one thing and grown into another. The effect was not harmonious. It was functional. Dreya had learned, in two years of exile, that functional was its own kind of beauty.

"Provisions," she said, from the cart bed. "I need to find a dry goods merchant, an apothecary, and possibly a notary, depending on whether the transit writ needs renewing before the border or at the border. Also, we're low on tea."

Six did not turn around. He did make a gesture—one hand, a small forward wave—that she now recognized as *proceed.*

"You're not coming?"

He tilted his head toward Provenance. The horse needed stabling. The cart needed watching. The sword needed to stay out of sight, and Six needed to stay out of sight with it, because a man his height in a market town full of Imperial merchants was a description waiting to be filed.

"Fair," Dreya said. "Find a stable with a loft. We may need to stay the night."

He tapped once on the reins. She hopped down from the cart—her leg pulled, a brief sharp reminder, already fading—and straightened her jacket. She had dressed for the town: hair pinned properly, jacket brushed, the silver ring on her thumb polished to a dull gleam. Valerian scholar, not fugitive. The performance was in the details.

Six clicked his tongue at Provenance and the cart creaked away toward the stable district. Dreya watched him go for a moment—the breadth of his shoulders, the way he slouched to seem smaller even on the cart bench—and then turned toward the market square.

She had work to do.

---

The dry goods merchant was an Imperial expatriate with the harried expression of someone who had been interrupted in the middle of inventory and was not enjoying the interruption. Dreya purchased flour, dried meat, a wedge of hard cheese, and a small sack of tea that cost more than it should have. She negotiated the tea down by citing the wholesale price in Midden, which she knew from a consultation job she'd done for a tea merchant six months ago. The dry goods merchant did not know this. He only knew that the small woman in the Valerian jacket had just named a price that was exactly two coppers above his cost, and that arguing further would cost him more in time than in margin.

She paid. She pocketed the tea. She allowed herself a small, private satisfaction.

The apothecary was three streets over, in a crooked building that had once been a residence and now served as a dispensary for everything from willow bark to wound salve to the particular kind of migraine powder that Dreya had been buying since her Academy days. The apothecary was an older woman with ink-stained fingers and the sharp, assessing gaze of someone who had spent forty years determining who was genuinely ill and who was simply bored.

"Willow bark," Dreya said. "The concentrated tincture, not the dried leaves. And a tin of your headache powder, if you have it."

The apothecary looked at her. "Migraines?"

"Yes."

"With aura or without?"

"Without. Usually. Pressure behind the left eye, light sensitivity, nausea if it goes too long."

"How long is too long?"

"Six hours."

The apothecary nodded and turned to her shelves. "You know your symptoms."

"I've had practice."

The woman filled two packets and named a price. Dreya paid without negotiating. Some things were not worth bargaining over.

She was tucking the packets into her jacket when she heard her name.

Not her full name. Not Dreya. The other one. The one she had been trying not to hear for two years.

"Dreyara Castellan?"

She turned. The man standing in the doorway of the apothecary's shop was thin and stooped and wore the particular expression of someone who had spent his entire career in administrative roles and had never once been promoted beyond them. He was holding a sheaf of papers, and he was looking at her with the startled recognition of someone who had just solved a puzzle he hadn't known he was working on.

"Grett," she said. The name surfaced from memory: Academy clerk, third-floor records office, had processed her graduation paperwork. Had been there when the news about her uncle broke. Had been the one to hand her the letter of expulsion with the expression of a man who wished he were somewhere else. "You're a long way from the College."

"I—yes. Yes, I left. After the restructure." He blinked at her, still processing. "You're alive. I mean—we heard you'd gone to Midden. Some people thought—well. It doesn't matter what some people thought." He stepped further into the shop, lowering his voice. "What are you doing here? This far south? Are you—are you going back to Valeris?"

Dreya's face had arranged itself into an expression of pleasant, professional calm. It had done this without her permission, which was good, because her conscious mind was still catching up to the situation. *Clerk. Academy. Knew her name. Knew the scandal. Had access to records.*

"Research," she said. "Independent. I'm consulting on a translation project for a private client." The lies came easily now. They hadn't always. "What about you? What brings you to Duskmere?"

"Administrative work. The Valerian trade commission has an office here—I manage their archives. It's not exciting, but it's stable." He was still looking at her with that expression—the one that was part curiosity and part something else. "I heard about the Gazette notice."

Dreya's stomach went cold. Her face did not.

"The Gazette?"

"The collapse of House Varetta. It was published about six months after you left. All the details—the assassination attempt, the investigation, the dissolution of titles. Your father's exile. Your mother's retreat. Your uncle's..." He trailed off. "It was thorough. Some of us thought it was excessive. Publicizing a family's ruin like that. But the Council wanted transparency."

Dreya filed this information in the part of her mind that was still functioning analytically. The Gazette was the Valerian public record. If her family's collapse had been published there, it meant the Council had wanted the scandal documented. It also meant anyone in Valeris with access to the Gazette could look up House Varetta and find her name. Her real name. The one she had been using in Midden because she hadn't thought she needed to hide it.

"I see," she said. "I hadn't heard."

"There were factions who were sympathetic," Grett said. "Quietly. People who thought the family was punished too harshly. Your father had allies. Your aunt—" He stopped. "Forgive me. I shouldn't gossip."

"My aunt," Dreya said, and her voice was very steady. "What about my aunt?"

"She disappeared, didn't she? After the trial. Some people said she went to Luminarch. Others said the Empire." He shook his head. "I don't know anything more than that. I'm sorry."

Dreya nodded. The cold in her stomach had settled into something harder. "Thank you, Grett. It's good to see you." She was already stepping toward the door. "I should go. My client is expecting a report."

"Of course. Of course." He stepped aside, then paused. "Dreyara—I should tell you. The Gazette notice about Midden. There was a description. Two persons of interest, they said. A scholar and a tall associate. It didn't name you. But..." He looked at her. "Someone might connect the two. The Varetta scholar who disappeared and the Midden scholar who's wanted for questioning. The description is thin, but it's there."

Dreya's face did not change. "Thank you for the warning."

She walked out of the apothecary's shop and into the market square and kept walking until she found a quiet alley between a cooper's workshop and a boarded-up storefront. Then she stopped. She put her back against the wall. She breathed.

*The Gazette. The Midden notice. A clerk in Duskmere just connected me to both, and he's not even trying to find me. He just recognized my face.*

She touched her silver ring. Turned it once on her thumb. Stopped herself.

*This is a problem. This is a specific, actionable problem with a timeline. The notice is circulating. Someone in the next town might recognize me the way Grett did. Someone with less sympathy.*

She ran through her options the way she had been taught to run through a crisis at the Academy: sequentially, without emotion, one variable at a time. The transit writ was still valid, but it had her real name. The scholar's endorsement had her real name. If the Midden notice was circulating with a description, then her real name plus her real face in a town with a Valerian trade commission office was a risk she couldn't calculate. She needed new papers. She needed them before the border. And she needed to leave Duskmere before Grett mentioned the encounter to anyone else.

She straightened her jacket. She walked back into the market square.

She did not look for a notary. She looked for Six.

---

She found him in the stable district, in a loft above a hay barn that smelled of horses and old leather and the particular mustiness of a place that had been sheltering travellers for a century. Provenance was stabled below. The cart was parked against the far wall. Six was sitting on a hay bale near the loft's single window, his sword across his knees, a whetstone in his hand. He was not sharpening the blade. He was holding the stone and the sword and looking at the door, and when Dreya climbed the ladder and stepped onto the loft floor, he was already looking at her.

"What," he said. Not a question. An observation.

She sat down on the hay bale across from him. Her leg complained. She ignored it.

"We have a problem. The Valerian Gazette published the Varetta collapse. My name is in it. My family's history is in it. Anyone with access to the public record can look me up." She paused. "The Midden notice is circulating. A clerk I knew from the Academy recognized my face. He mentioned the notice. He connected the two."

Six set the whetstone down.

"The transit writ has my real name," she said. "The scholar's endorsement has my real name. If the Midden description and the Gazette records intersect at a checkpoint—"

He made a gesture: one hand flat, one finger tracing a line across it. *Where is the clerk now?*

"At the trade commission office. He works there. He's not a threat—he was sympathetic, I think—but he talked to me. He'll mention it to someone. He'll describe me. He already described the Gazette notice to me. He'll describe me to the next person."

Six looked at her for a long moment. Then he made another gesture—both hands, a sweeping motion, a question in the angle. *Can we leave tonight?*

"Yes. We should. We should have left an hour ago, but I needed to tell you first." She pressed her fingers to her temple. The pressure behind her left eye was beginning—a dull, familiar ache, like a bruise forming somewhere behind the bone. "We need new papers before the border. I can forge a basic transit writ, but it won't hold up to a Church seal inspection. We need someone who can produce documents with real provenance. A notary, or a forger, or preferably both."

Six tapped twice. *Acknowledged.*

"That means money. More than we have."

He made the gesture for *we'll solve that.*

"Yes. We will." She lowered her hand. The ache was settling in, a steady pressure that made the light through the loft window feel sharper than it should. "The clerk—Grett—he mentioned Aunt Mira. He said some people thought she went to Luminarch. Others thought the Empire. He didn't know." She paused. "He said your name isn't in the description. Just 'a tall associate.' They don't know who you are. They know what you look like."

Six tilted his head. *And you?*

"They know my face. My name. My family. They know I'm travelling with someone tall. They know we're connected to Midden." She met his eyes. "If we get separated—if something happens at the border—don't use my name. Don't give them anything they can connect to the Gazette."

He didn't respond. He picked up the whetstone and set it down again, a small precise motion, and she realized he was not going to agree to that. He was not going to argue with it either. He was going to do what he always did: file the information, wait for the situation to develop, and act when acting was necessary.

The ache behind her eye sharpened. She closed her eyes for a moment. The darkness helped.

"Dreya." His voice. Low, rough, the way it always was. She opened her eyes. He was holding out a hand—not to help her up, not to touch her. Just open, palm up, a gesture she didn't recognize.

"What does that one mean?"

He made the gesture again, slower. Both hands this time. A slow inward motion, as if gathering something toward himself.

"You're asking if I'm alright."

He tilted his hand. *Close.*

"You're asking me to tell you if I'm not."

He tapped once.

She looked at his hand. She didn't take it. She said, "I'm not not alright. I'm... processing. The headache is starting. That's separate." She paused. "Grett told me my family's ruin was published for public record. Like a weather report. Like an inventory of assets. And I didn't know. For two years, I didn't know that anyone could look me up and find out exactly what happened and what I lost."

Six didn't speak. He lowered his hand, but he didn't look away.

"I thought I was hiding," she said. "I thought I was being careful. And it turns out my name has been sitting in a public archive for eighteen months, waiting for someone to read it."

The ache pulsed. She pressed her fingers to her temple again.

"This is a new thing," she said. "The headache without magic. It started a few weeks ago. Pressure behind the left eye, light sensitivity. Exactly the same as the Toll headaches, except I haven't been casting. I don't know what it is."

Six made a gesture she knew: two fingers, a small forward motion. *Continue.*

"That's all. That's the data. I'm cataloguing it. I don't have a diagnosis yet."

He tapped twice. Then he made another gesture—the one he had made at the inn, the one she now recognized as *the road is the road.* Or possibly *what's already in motion stays in motion.* She was still working on the nuance.

"I know," she said. "We keep moving."

---

She spent the afternoon in the loft with her notebook. Not the research notebook—the other one. The one she had started two nights ago, in the cold field, after she had tried to make the sign for *look behind you* and gotten the finger angle wrong.

She had been cataloguing. It was what she did when she was stressed. It was what she did when she was tired. It was what she did when the world was too large and too loud and she needed to reduce it to something she could hold in her hands. The notebook was now thirty-seven pages long, divided into sections that she had organized with the same systematic precision she had once applied to her Academy thesis.

At the top of the first page, in her neat, compressed handwriting: *SIX'S GESTURAL LEXICON — A WORKING TAXONOMY.*

She had divided the gestures into categories. *Directional (spatial). Directional (attentional). Interrogative. Affirmative variants (confirmed: at least four).* There was a section on modifiers—the small adjustments in angle or speed that changed a gesture's meaning—and a section on what she was calling *composite signs,* gestures that combined two or more elements to create a new meaning. The sign for *hungry* was a composite: *mouth* plus *circular motion,* which she had initially interpreted as *eating* but now understood as *the state of requiring food, possibly urgent, possibly exaggerated.*

Under a heading she had titled *UNCLEAR INTENT,* she had listed twelve gestures she could not parse.

Entry twelve read: *Both hands, one flat, one curved, repeated motion — used after the incident with the cheese merchant and also once while watching a very large cat in the Duskmere market. Unknown referent.*

The cheese merchant incident had happened that morning, while she was purchasing provisions. She had been negotiating the price of a wheel of hard cheese—the merchant had wanted three silvers, which was absurd—and Six, standing behind her with his arms full of flour sacks, had made the gesture. She had not seen it. The merchant had. The merchant had blinked, lost his train of thought, and accepted her counter-offer of one silver and six coppers. When she turned around, Six was looking at the cheese display with the peaceful expression of someone who had not just done anything.

She had added the gesture to the *UNCLEAR INTENT* list and moved on.

Now, in the loft, with the headache pressing behind her eye and the afternoon light slanting through the window, she was cross-referencing. She had observed fourteen distinct gestures in the past three days. She had confirmed the meaning of eleven. The remaining three were on the *UNCLEAR INTENT* list, plus the new one he had made that morning—the slow inward gathering—which she had filed under a separate heading: *NEW (UNILATERAL — HE INVENTED THIS ONE FOR THIS CONVERSATION).*

She heard the loft ladder creak. Six climbed through the hatch, his boots soft on the straw, and crossed to where she was sitting. He looked at the notebook.

She looked at him.

"I have a working taxonomy," she said. "It's not complete. The modifiers are complicated. The grammar is—" She stopped herself. "I believe I have the core vocabulary. I'm uncertain about the modifiers."

Six looked at the notebook. He turned it sideways. He pointed at entry twelve under *UNCLEAR INTENT* and tapped the page.

She read it aloud. "Both hands, one flat, one curved, repeated motion—used after the incident with the cheese merchant and also once while watching a very large cat in the Duskmere market. Unknown referent."

Six made the gesture. Slowly. Deliberately. One hand flat, one hand curved, repeated motion.

Dreya watched his hands. She watched his face.

"This is a ridiculous situation and I find it entertaining," she said.

He tapped once.

She wrote it down. Under *UNCLEAR INTENT,* beside entry twelve: *CONFIRMED: expression of amusement at absurdity. Possibly situational. Check for variants.*

Six made another gesture. A single finger, circled at his temple.

"You are doing too much."

He tapped once.

She wrote that down too. Then she looked up at him.

"You could just tell me what they mean."

He tilted his head. The expression was approximately: *where is the satisfaction in that.*

"I don't know why I expected a different answer." She closed the notebook. The headache was still there, a steady pressure, but it had receded slightly—or she had stopped noticing it. She wasn't sure which. "The clerk. Grett. He mentioned the Gazette notice about Midden. I didn't tell you everything. The description includes your height and the fact that you don't speak. It doesn't name you. It doesn't have your face. But it has enough."

Six was still. Not the predator's stillness—the other one. The one he used when he was processing information that mattered.

"We need new papers," she said. "New names. New cover. We need them before the border, and we need them from someone who can produce documents that survive inspection."

He made a gesture: both hands forward, a questioning tilt. *Where?*

"There's a town three days south. Faros. It's a festival stop—travelling merchants, seasonal fairs, the kind of place where documents change hands without too many questions. If we can find a forger there, or a notary who's flexible about provenance, we can get what we need."

He tapped twice.

"We leave tonight," she said. "Grett will talk to someone. He's not malicious, but he's a clerk. Clerks share information. It's what they do."

Six was already standing. He moved toward the ladder, then paused. He made a gesture she recognized—the same one from that morning, both hands, the slow inward gathering. *I'm noting that you're not alright.*

She looked at his hands. She looked at his face.

"I know," she said. "I'll be alright later."

He held her gaze for a moment. Then he tapped once and climbed down the ladder.

Dreya sat with the notebook in her lap and the headache behind her eye and the weight of her name—her real name, the one that had been sitting in a public archive for eighteen months—pressing against her chest like a stone.

Then she opened the notebook to a fresh page and began to write.

*New heading: FORGERY REQUIREMENTS — TRANSIT DOCUMENTS.*
*New heading: NAMES — POSSIBLE ALIASES.*
*New heading: SIGNS I NEED TO LEARN BEFORE THE BORDER.*

She wrote until the light failed. Then she packed her things, climbed down the ladder, and found Six waiting in the stable with Provenance already harnessed and the cart loaded and his expression the same as always: patient, still, ready for the road.

"Let's go," she said.

He clicked his tongue at the horse. The cart creaked forward. Duskmere receded behind them, its lights small and warm and already fading, and Dreya did not look back.
