# Chapter 6 — Welcome to Valeris

**POV:** Dreya  
**Setting:** Sondell; a traveller's hostel, ground floor back room

---

The back room of the Sondell hostel smelled of old smoke and older damp, the kind of smell that had been settling into the walls for decades and saw no reason to leave now. Dreya had positioned herself near the window—not for the light, which was grey and fading, but for the sightline. Old habits. The window looked out on a narrow alley between the hostel and a chandler's workshop, and if anyone approached from the main road, she would hear their footsteps on the cobbles before they reached the door.

Six was in the corner by the door, his chair tilted back against the wall, his sword across his knees. He had not spoken since they arrived. He had not needed to. The chair's position gave him a view of the door and the window and the single lamp that lit the room, and the way he sat—still, patient, his thumb resting on the crossguard—was the way a man sat when he was not expecting trouble but was prepared to meet it if it arrived.

Vane sat across from Dreya at the small, scarred table that was the room's only furniture besides the chairs and a narrow cot pushed against the far wall. He had been waiting for them at the hostel when they arrived, as promised. He had not seemed surprised to see them. He had seemed like a man who had calculated the odds and found them acceptable.

Now he was laying out the shape of a conspiracy with the same calm precision Dreya had once heard Academy lecturers use to describe the migratory patterns of griffons.

"The Magisterium's involvement in Midden wasn't a single operation," Vane said. "It was the first phase of a broader strategy. The coup was meant to destabilize the Accordance—remove key arbiters, compromise the Council, create a vacuum that the Empire could exploit. When you stopped it, you didn't end the threat. You delayed it."

Dreya had her notebook open on the table. She was writing—not transcription, exactly, but a shorthand of her own devising, key words and connecting lines that would let her reconstruct the argument later. *Magisterium. Midden. Phase one. Delayed, not ended.*

"You said the woman we're looking for is in Luminarch," she said. "Mira. You said she was taken."

"Yes. By Iron Lictors, on the Duke of Cerdon's authority. She was at an estate called Thornhaven, east of the capital—a minor noble's property—analyzing pre-Radiant documents when they came for her." Vane paused. "She went quietly, I'm told. Set the steward's hat on fire first, using only the air currents from the fireplace. Then she went quietly."

Dreya's pen stopped moving. The image landed somewhere in her chest—her aunt, precision and defiance, using a fireplace draft to make a point before surrendering. It was exactly the kind of thing Mira would do. It was also exactly the kind of thing Dreya had not allowed herself to imagine for three years.

"That's her," she said. Her voice came out steadier than she expected.

Vane nodded. "She's been in the Duke's compound since then. Not in a cell—he's not stupid enough to mistreat his only leverage over the pre-Radiant documents. She's in a tower room. Working. He's extracting her analysis piece by piece."

"The ledger," Dreya said. "The cipher—it's hers. Her notation style. I'd recognize it anywhere."

"I assumed as much. The cipher was designed for her resistance cell in Luminarch." Vane leaned back in his chair. "The cell was four people. Two left the city after she was taken. One is dead. One stayed."

"Who stayed?"

Vane shook his head. "Not yet. That information is operational, and I don't share operational intelligence with people I'm still assessing."

Dreya's jaw tightened. "You approached us."

"I gave you the macro picture at the border. Magisterium involvement. Mira's location. The fact that she didn't go to Luminarch by choice. That was enough to get you here." His voice was calm, unapologetic. "What I know about safe houses, guard rotations, the Duke's security architecture—that's a different category of information. Information parceled is information controlled. I'm an intelligence officer, or I was. Old habits."

"So you're still deciding whether to trust us."

"I'm still deciding whether trusting you is operationally sound. There's a difference." He met her eyes. "You're a scholar. You understand the value of verification."

Dreya held his gaze. She understood, also, that he was right—she would have done the same thing in his position. That didn't make it less frustrating. It made it more frustrating.

"Then tell us something you can share," she said. "Who filed the description? The one that reached the border checkpoint?"

Vane's expression shifted—a fractional tightening around the eyes, the only sign that the question had landed somewhere he hadn't planned for. "A Magisterium field agent. Midden-stationed. She survived the coup's collapse and filed her report before she was recalled south."

"Survived," Dreya said. "The Grey-Haired Mage. The one who pursued us through the God-Fall tunnels."

"Yes. She's been reassigned. She's not in Midden anymore." He didn't say where. The omission was deliberate.

Six, in the corner, had not moved. But Dreya saw his thumb pause on the crossguard—a fraction of a second, the tapping interrupted—before it resumed its rhythm. He had heard. He had filed it.

"The description she filed," Dreya said. "It's still circulating."

"It's reached the Valerian ecclesiastical network. The Church is cooperating with the Magisterium on this—not officially, but practically. Your description is in the hands of every checkpoint officer between here and Luminarch. The border officer logged your papers. That log will spread. Your window is closing."

"We knew that."

"I'm telling you the scope. The Magisterium wants the ledger. The Church wants stability. The Duke wants to keep his sun-stone monopoly intact. All three of them have reasons to stop you before you reach Luminarch."

Dreya wrote: *Magisterium wants ledger. Church wants stability. Duke wants monopoly. Converging threats.* The headache was beginning—a pressure behind her left eye, the familiar prelude to something worse. She had not cast anything. This was the new thing. She logged it and kept writing.

"The second phase," she said. "The Magisterium's strategy. You said Midden was the first phase. What's the second?"

Vane was quiet for a moment. When he spoke, his voice was flatter than before—the tone of someone reporting facts he didn't like. "I don't know. The second phase target was siloed above my clearance. I know it involves the Accordance and a timeline connected to the summer session in Luminarch. That's all."

"Summer session. That's six weeks away."

"Approximately."

Dreya wrote: *Second phase — Accordance — summer session — target unknown.* She drew a line connecting it to *Magisterium* and another connecting it to *Luminarch*. The shape was incomplete, but the direction was clear. Whatever the Empire was planning, it was going to happen in the city where Mira was being held.

She set her pen down. The pressure behind her eye sharpened into something more acute—a spike of pain that made the lamplight feel like a blade. She pressed her fingers to her temple.

Vane noticed. "You're injured."

"Headache. It's not—" She stopped. The pressure was building, a sinus blockage that she recognized from years of managing the Toll's aftereffects. It wasn't the Toll this time, but the mechanism was the same. A small air-burst would clear it. She had done it a hundred times. It was barely a spell—a whisper of pressure, the magical equivalent of clearing her throat.

She raised her hand and let the air move.

The relief was immediate. The Toll was not.

The nosebleed started before she lowered her hand—hot and sudden, a thin stream running down her upper lip. She pressed her sleeve to it, but the damage was already done. The world tilted. The words she had been about to say—*I'm fine, it's nothing, don't look at me like that*—dissolved before they reached her mouth. For a long, strange moment, she couldn't find them. She knew what she wanted to say. The shape of the sentence was there. The words were gone.

She blinked. The lamplight swam. She was aware, dimly, of Vane handing her a cloth—a plain square of linen, clean, folded. She took it without speaking. She couldn't speak. The words were still missing.

Ten seconds passed. Then twelve. Then the words came back, sliding into place like letters on a composing stick, and she said, "That happens. Sometimes. I'm fine."

She was not fine. The cloth was red. The headache had receded, but the space where it had been felt hollowed out, like a room after something heavy had been moved. She pressed the cloth to her nose and looked at Vane, daring him to comment.

He didn't. He simply nodded, once, and turned back to his notes.

Six, in the corner, had gone very still. Not the predator's stillness—the other one. The one he used when he was cataloguing something he didn't like. She didn't look at him. She couldn't, not yet.

---

"The cell," Dreya said, when the bleeding had stopped and the cloth was folded on the table and her voice was her own again. "Mira's resistance cell. You said four people."

"Yes."

"She taught me air-suspension exercises until I passed out from the Toll. Three times. On the same afternoon. Of course she was running a resistance cell." She heard the tone in her own voice—the particular mix of exasperation and affection that Mira had always provoked. "She never told me. She taught me precision and control and how to hold a vacuum bubble steady for twelve minutes, and the whole time she was organizing a network against the Duke."

"Would you have joined her?" Vane asked.

"I was in the library. I was always in the library." Dreya shook her head. "I didn't know anything about politics. I still don't, not really. I know research. I know analysis. Mira knew how to act."

"The cell was built for action. Smuggling documents, protecting witnesses, documenting the Duke's abuses." Vane paused. "The cipher in your ledger was her design. She created it so the cell could communicate without the Church's monitors intercepting. It was meant to be read by exactly one other person—her second. The one who stayed in Luminarch."

"The one you won't name."

"The one I won't name until I'm certain you're not going to get them killed." Vane's voice was still calm, but there was an edge under it now—something harder, more personal. "I've lost assets before. I've lost people. I'm not losing this one because I shared information too quickly."

Dreya looked at him. She saw, for the first time, the lines around his eyes—faint, but present. The weight of someone who had been doing this work for a long time and had the scars to prove it.

"Fine," she said. "Keep your operational intelligence. But tell me one thing. The ledger—if I can't decode it without Mira, and Mira is in the Duke's compound, how do we reach her?"

"That's the question I can't answer yet. What I can tell you is that the Duke arrives in Luminarch in two weeks. Before then, his security is lighter—he travels with a full Lictor escort, and they go with him. The compound will be understaffed during his absence. That's your window."

"Two weeks," Dreya said.

"Less, now. You'll need new papers to reach Luminarch. You'll need a plan to get into the compound. You'll need Mira's cooperation once you're inside. And you'll need to do all of it before the summer session, because whatever the Magisterium is planning, it won't wait for you to be ready."

Dreya wrote: *Two weeks until Duke arrives. Lighter security before. Papers. Plan. Mira's cooperation. Summer session deadline.* The list was getting longer. The weight of it was settling onto her shoulders, familiar and unwelcome.

She looked at Six. He was still in the corner, still still, but his eyes were on Vane. He had been watching the entire exchange, and she knew—without him making a single gesture—that he had already filed everything Vane had said and was now filing the things Vane hadn't said.

---

Vane stood. "I'm going to find food. The hostel kitchen closes in an hour, and I need to check the road for any updates from my contacts." He paused by the door and looked at Six. "Do you trust me?"

Six made a gesture. One hand flat, the other making a slow side-to-side motion.

Vane studied it. Then he said, with the particular confidence of someone who had been trained in field interpretation and was not going to admit when he was out of his depth: "Cautious agreement. You're willing to proceed with me."

Dreya looked up from her notebook. She said, "He's saying he wants to wait and see. The side-to-side on the modifier means the matter is unresolved. Not that he's agreeing."

Vane blinked. "How do you know that?"

She held up the notebook. The gesture was becoming familiar—a small, private satisfaction in the middle of a conversation that had no room for satisfaction. The notebook was open to the section she had titled *AFFIRMATIVE VARIANTS AND MODIFIERS*, and under it, in her neat handwriting, the side-to-side motion was annotated with three possible meanings and the contexts in which each applied.

Six looked at the notebook. He made a gesture—a single finger, circled at his temple.

*You are doing too much.*

Dreya pointed at him. She wrote something in the margin. *Refuses to provide direct glossary. Continues to critique my documentation. See also: STUBBORN.*

Vane looked between them. "The two of you have a system."

"We're building one," Dreya said. "It's a work in progress."

"Like everything else." He opened the door. "I'll be back in an hour. Don't leave the room. Sondell is small, and your description is in circulation."

He left. The door clicked shut. The lamp flickered.

Six did not move from his chair by the door. He made a gesture—small, barely visible, the one Dreya had begun to think of as *acknowledged and filed.* She made the same gesture back at him, deliberately, her fingers angled the way his had been when he taught her in the cold field three nights ago. She got the angle right.

He tapped twice on his knee.

"Did I get it right?"

He tapped once.

"The modifier was correct?"

He tapped once.

"Good." She closed the notebook. The headache had faded to a dull ache, the hollowed-out feeling slowly filling with something she didn't have a name for. "He's holding back. Vane. He knows more than he's telling us."

Six tapped twice.

"I know. It's what intelligence officers do. He said as much. But he knows who stayed in Luminarch. He knows where the safe house is. He knows how to get into the compound." She pressed her fingers to the bridge of her nose. "He's waiting to see if we're worth the risk. And we're waiting to see if he's telling the truth. We're all waiting."

Six made the gesture for *the road is the road.* Or possibly *what's already in motion stays in motion.* She was still working on the nuance, but the meaning was clear enough.

"Yes," she said. "We keep moving."

---

She slept eventually. The cot was narrow and the blankets were thin, but she had slept in worse places—the Archive basement, the canal barge, the cold field with nothing but her coat and the fire. She fell asleep with her notebook under her pillow and the silver ring cool on her thumb and the sound of Six's breathing steady in the corner.

She woke once, in the deep part of the night, and saw him still in the chair by the door. He had not moved. His sword was across his knees. His thumb was tapping a rhythm on the crossguard—slow, steady, the beat she had come to recognize as his baseline. His heartbeat rhythm. The one he used when he was not fighting, not waiting to fight, just existing.

He didn't look at her. He was looking at the door. But she saw the tapping, and she let it settle into the back of her mind, a quiet counterpoint to the silence.

She closed her eyes.

When she woke again, it was dawn, and the tapping was still going.
