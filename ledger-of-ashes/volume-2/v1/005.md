# Chapter 5 — The Aethelian Problem

**Six**  
**Valerian border checkpoint, dusk** 

---

The checkpoint rose out of the dusk like something that had been built to outlast the road it guarded. Stone walls, iron gates, a watchtower with a sun-stone beacon rotating slow above the battlements. The beacon painted long shadows across the cart and the horse and the two people sitting on the cart bench, and Six watched those shadows stretch and turn and stretch again, counting the rotation in the back of his mind. Twelve seconds per sweep. A beat he could use if he needed one.

He didn't need one yet. He stored it.

The road had widened as they approached the border, swallowing cart tracks and foot traffic and a thin stream of merchants heading the other way—northbound, their wagons empty, their faces carrying the particular wear of people who had paid a tax they hadn't wanted to pay. The Duke's tariff, Dreya had called it. Six had filed it under *things that make money disappear before it reaches your hands.*

Now the checkpoint gates were closing for the evening, a slow iron grind that said *you're the last ones through or you're sleeping outside.* Six flicked the reins. Provenance picked up her pace, not because she wanted to, but because she had learned in the past three days that the tall man holding the reins didn't ask for things twice.

"Border inspection," Dreya said, beside him on the bench. Her voice was the particular register she used when she was reciting information she already knew, for the benefit of anyone who might be listening. "Valerian ecclesiastical checkpoint. Church officer, probably a sun-stone assessor, possibly a magistrate's adjutant if there's a trade dispute pending. Standard procedure: transit papers, cargo manifest if applicable, verbal declaration of intent. They'll ask about sun-stones. Everyone asks about sun-stones."

Six tapped the reins twice. *Acknowledged.*

"The papers will hold," she said, quieter now, not reciting. "They held at the waystation. They held in Duskmere. They'll hold here."

She didn't say *they have to hold.* Six heard it anyway.

The cart rolled under the gate arch and into the inspection queue, and the iron closed around them like a hand.

---

Six felt the iron before he saw it.

The cold-iron dampening was different from the ambient iron of Midden's buildings. Midden's iron was old, passive, a buzz in the teeth that you learned to ignore. The checkpoint's iron was deliberate. The gates were cold iron, forged and blessed and maintained by someone who knew exactly what they were doing. The effect hit Six's nervous system like a wave of static—not pain, not yet, but a pressure at the edges of his awareness, a fog where his magic usually hummed. The hum went quiet. The clean current he'd felt since leaving Midden flickered, guttered, settled into something muffled.

His hands went cold. Not the Toll's cold. The iron's cold. The iron pulled the heat out of everything.

He flexed his fingers once, twice. The blood was moving. Slower than it should have been. He filed this under *checkpoint conditions, adjust expectations* and let his face settle into the expression he used when he needed to look like a man who was not calculating escape routes.

The inspection yard was a wide stone courtyard lit by sun-stone lamps on iron posts. Three other carts waited ahead of them—two merchant wagons, one private carriage with a coat of arms Six didn't recognize. Guards in Valerian livery moved between the vehicles, checking papers, opening crates, asking questions in the flat tone of people who had been asking the same questions since dawn. At the far end of the yard, near the inner gate, stood a figure that was not a guard.

Iron Lictor.

Full cold-iron armor. Head to foot. The metal drank the light from the sun-stone lamps, turning gold into grey. The Lictor didn't move. It stood with its arms at its sides and its faceplate blank and its weight planted like a thing that had been set there and told to wait. The cold radiating from it reached Six where he sat, a second layer of pressure under the gate's iron, and his fingers went from cold to numb.

He made a fist. Released it. The motion was slower than it should have been.

"Lictor," he said, low.

Dreya followed his gaze. Her face didn't change, but her hands, resting on the ledger in her lap, went still. "I see it. That's... that's a lot of iron."

Six tapped twice.

"I've read about them. The armor is solid cold iron throughout. Grounds magic on contact—not just the wearer's magic, any magic within a certain radius. The radius depends on the purity of the iron and the ambient magical field, but in an enclosed space like this—" She stopped herself. She was lecturing. She knew she was lecturing. She said, instead: "Don't use the lightning."

He tilted his head approximately two degrees in the direction of *I had already reached that conclusion.*

"Right. Of course you weren't going to." She touched her silver ring, stopped herself, put her hand flat on the ledger. "The good news is, the Lictor won't care about us unless we give it a reason. They're enforcers, not inspectors. They're here for mages who try to fight the checkpoint, not scholars with forged papers."

Six made a gesture—small, below the bench rail where no one else could see it—that she now recognized as *we'll see.*

"That's not comforting."

He didn't tap. He was looking at the inspection queue. The queue was moving slowly, and between the carts, in the shadow of a sun-stone post, a man was standing who had not been there a moment ago.

Medium height. Medium build. Brown hair, brown eyes, unremarkable clothes. He was waiting in the inspection queue—or rather, he was standing where someone waiting in the inspection queue might stand, except he wasn't attached to any of the carts, and he wasn't carrying anything, and his stance was not the stance of a traveller. Weight balanced. Hands visible. The kind of stillness that meant the person was either very comfortable or very dangerous.

Six watched him. The man caught his eye and began to walk toward the cart—not fast, not slow, the pace of someone who had every right to be there.

He stopped beside the cart, close to Six's side of the bench, and looked up at him.

"I know what you are," the man said, quietly. Not a threat. A statement. "I've read the reports. The Archive basement. The construct. The lightning."

Six didn't move. He looked at the man's hands—empty, still, positioned where they could be seen. He looked at the man's face—unremarkable, forgettable, the kind of face intelligence officers trained themselves to have. He looked at the man's eyes—steady, not hostile, assessing.

The man met his gaze. Held it.

This was a mistake.

Six had nothing in his schedule for the next twenty minutes except waiting in a queue, and he could wait with extraordinary stillness. The man was clearly someone who was used to controlling conversations, controlling encounters, controlling the space between himself and the people he spoke to. Six did not give him the space. He simply looked at him—amber eyes flat, patient, the way a pond waits for a stone to sink.

Forty-three seconds passed.

The man looked away. He did it smoothly, under the pretense of adjusting his pack strap, but he looked away. Six continued looking at the space where the man's face had been for three more seconds, then returned his gaze to the Lictor's patrol pattern.

"My name is Vane," the man said, still quiet, still composed, but with a fractional shift in his posture that hadn't been there before. "I'm with Aethelian Intelligence. Or I was. I need you to know something, and I need to tell you before you reach the inspection desk."

Dreya had gone very still on the bench. Her face was the face she used when she was cataloguing everything someone said for later analysis. "You're a defector."

"Yes."

"And you're approaching us in the middle of a border checkpoint."

"Because it's the only place I could be certain you'd stop long enough to listen." Vane looked at the queue ahead of them—two carts now, the guards working through a merchant's cargo. "I'll be brief. The Midden coup wasn't a local operation. It was planned by the Aethelian Magisterium. The hunters, the construct, the councilor—all of it was Imperial, funded through the Midden embassy. You stopped the first phase. There will be a second."

"We knew about the Magisterium," Dreya said. "The hunters were using Imperial munitions."

"Then you know the scope. What you may not know is that the woman you're looking for—Mira—is in Luminarch. She's being held by the Duke of Cerdon. Not as a prisoner, exactly. As a resource. He's extracting her analysis of something. I don't know what." Vane paused. "She didn't go to Luminarch by choice. She was taken."

Dreya's face didn't change. Six watched her hands. One of them had closed around the edge of the cart bench, the knuckles white.

"Why should we believe you?" Dreya asked.

"You shouldn't. Not yet." Vane looked at Six, and this time he didn't try to hold the gaze. "I'll be in Sondell. Traveller's hostel on the east market road. If you want to know more—if you want to know what I know about the Duke's security, about the Magisterium's network, about how to get into Luminarch without being flagged at every checkpoint—find me there." He stepped back. "That's all I can give you here. The rest requires trust, and trust requires time."

He walked away. Not into the trees, not vanishing—just back to the edge of the inspection yard, where he joined a small cluster of travellers waiting for a northbound cart. He didn't look back.

Six watched him go. Then he turned to Dreya.

She was breathing carefully. Her hand was still clenched on the bench.

"He knows about Mira," she said. "He knows she's alive. He knows she was taken."

Six tapped twice.

"If he's lying—"

He made a gesture: one hand flat, the other making a slow side-to-side motion. *We'll see.*

"I know that one." She unclenched her hand, one finger at a time. "The side-to-side on the modifier means the matter is unresolved. You're not agreeing. You're not refusing. You're watching."

He tapped once.

"The staring contest," she said, and something in her voice shifted—the academic register cracking slightly, a different tone pushing through. "You stared at an Aethelian intelligence officer until he looked away. In the middle of a border checkpoint."

Six tilted his head approximately three degrees.

"Forty-three seconds," she said. "I counted."

He made the gesture for *approximate.*

"That is not a denial."

He made it again.

She looked at him. The muscle in her jaw was doing its thing. Then she made a gesture—both hands, one flat, one curved, repeated motion. The sign for *this is a ridiculous situation and I find it entertaining.* Her right wrist was still slightly off. The angle wasn't quite right. But it was close. It was very close.

Six looked at her hands. He looked at her face. He tapped twice.

*Good.*

"Don't," she said. "Don't encourage me. An Aethelian defector just told us my aunt is a prisoner and you're giving me sign language feedback."

He made the gesture for *continue but briefly.*

She made a sound—the short one, through the nose, immediately suppressed—and faced forward. The queue had moved. They were next.

---

The inspection was worse than the queue.

The guard was young, thorough, not tired enough to be sloppy. The ecclesiastical officer—Aldren, he called himself—was older, sharper, holding a folded paper that Six recognized before it was unfolded. The Midden description. Height. Does not speak. May be armed.

Six used the pond-face. Dreya used the scholar's voice. The officer compared. The silence stretched.

The officer noted the irregular seal. He logged it. He let them through—not because he believed them, but because the description was vague enough to create doubt, and the doubt was enough to make him cautious. A false arrest on shaky grounds would cost him more than a log entry. Six understood this calculus. He had used it himself, in different contexts.

They rolled through the inner gate, past the Iron Lictor whose faceplate tracked them a fraction of an inch and then returned to stillness, and out onto the dark road beyond the checkpoint's sun-stone lamps.

Dreya didn't speak until the iron's pressure had faded and Six's fingers had begun to warm. Then she said, quietly, "That was the last trip on these papers."

Six tapped twice.

"The officer had the description in his hand. He compared it. He logged the irregular seal. The next checkpoint will have the log. The next officer will know what to look for."

Six made a gesture: both hands forward, a questioning tilt. *How long?*

"Before the papers are useless? I don't know. The log will move faster than we can. Someone will put the pieces together." She pressed her fingers to her temple. The pressure was back. She didn't mention it. "We get new papers before Luminarch. We find a forger in Faros, or we don't go to Luminarch at all."

Six tapped once.

He flicked the reins. Provenance started forward.

Behind them, the checkpoint's lights shrank to points. The Iron Lictor's cold receded. The iron box against Six's chest was warm—steadier now, more insistent, as if it knew something he didn't.

He filed: *Vane. Aethelian defector. Intelligence on Mira. Conditional alliance. Will be in Sondell.*
He filed: *Possibility of Grey-Haired Mage hunter filed the description. Still out there.*
He filed: *Dreya's sign was almost correct.*

The road continued. He tapped his thumb against the rein—once, twice—and found the beat still steady, the syncopation absent for now, the rhythm holding.
