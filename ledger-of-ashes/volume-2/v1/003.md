# Chapter 3 — What the Box Wants

**POV:** Six  
**Setting:** Roadside camp; open field, night

---

They made camp in a field an hour past the last farmhouse. The field was flat and cold and offered nothing but grass and a stone wall that had been falling down for long enough that the stones had forgotten they were ever a wall. Six chose it for the sightlines. Dreya chose it because she was too tired to argue with the sightlines.

She was asleep now—or something close to it—in her bedroll on the far side of the fire. The fire was small, built for warmth rather than light, and Six had positioned it so the flames wouldn't silhouette them against the open field. The habit was old. The Warrens had taught him that light was a liability. Fire was necessary. Fire was also a beacon. He had learned to build fires that kept you warm without announcing your location to every pair of eyes between here and the horizon.

The fire was also a heat source. He sat close enough for his hands to feel it, and he flexed them slowly, letting the warmth work into the joints. The cold from the day's driving had settled deep—not the Toll's cold, just the ordinary February cold, but it was hard to tell the difference sometimes. His body kept a running tally of temperatures: ambient cold, wind cold, the particular cold that arrived after he used the lightning. The ordinary cold was a background fact. The Toll's cold was sharper, more personal. It started in the fingers and moved inward.

His fingers were cold now. He flexed them again and decided it was the ordinary kind.

Dreya's breathing had settled into a rhythm he recognized: slow, even, the particular cadence of someone who was genuinely asleep. He had been listening to her breathe for two nights now—in the cart, in the waystation, in the field—and he had filed the patterns without meaning to. Awake and reading: faster, shallower, interrupted by small sounds when she found something interesting. Awake and thinking: slower, with occasional pauses where she held her breath to work through a problem. Asleep: this. Steady. The rhythm of someone who wasn't fighting anything.

He let the breath-rhythm settle into the back of his attention and turned to the thing he had been avoiding since they broke camp.

The iron box was still warm against his chest. He unwrapped it carefully—oilcloth first, then the leather cord, then the box itself, small and dark and humming with its own heat. It sat in his palm like a living thing. Not alive. But not inert either. Something in between.

He had carried it for eleven years without opening it. He had carried it through the Warrens, through the iron cell, through every flophouse and warehouse and canal boat he'd slept in since he was fifteen. He had never asked what was inside. Mara had told him not to open it, and that had been enough. He didn't need reasons. He needed instructions.

But the warmth was new. The box had been cold iron for eleven years—cold in the way of iron, cold in the way of something that had been built to contain. It had started warming two days out of Midden, slowly at first, then steadier. Now it radiated heat like a stone left in the sun, except there was no sun, and the heat was coming from inside.

He held it in his left hand and let his right hand rest on his knee. The warmth traveled up his wrist. It moved through his forearm. And when it reached his chest—not physically, not a heat he could track with his fingers, but something deeper, something that moved through him rather than across him—it found his heartbeat.

It locked on.

The warmth did not pulse with his heart. It *matched* his heart. The way a tuning fork finds a pitch. The way his rhythm found the beat in dripping water or forge hammers or the clank of a distant bell. The box was not generating heat. The box was using something in him to know where to point.

He sat with this for a long moment. The fire crackled. The wind moved across the field. Dreya's breathing held steady.

He did not know what the box was using. He did not know what it was pointing at. He added both questions to the drawer in the back of his mind where he kept things he couldn't solve yet, and he noted, with the same precise attention he gave to unexpected sounds in a sleeping building: *the box does not just get warm. It uses something in me to know where to point.*

He rewrapped it. Oilcloth. Leather cord. The warmth settled against his sternum like a hand.

---

Mara had told him, once, about the box.

He had been fourteen, maybe. Old enough to know she was sick, not old enough to know how sick. She had been sitting in the chair by the window in their tenement room, the one that caught the afternoon light, and she had been holding the box in her lap. Just holding it. Not opening it.

*"When it gets warm,"* she had said, *"you're close."*

He had asked close to what. She had looked at him with the expression she used when the answer was too big for the question and said: *"Close to where it's supposed to go."*

He had not asked anything else. He had been fourteen, and he had learned by then that some questions didn't have answers he could hold. Mara's answers were like that—they settled into you slowly, over years, and you didn't understand them until you'd grown into the shape of them.

He had not thought about that conversation in eleven years. It surfaced now, in the cold field, with the fire dying and the box warm against his chest and Dreya's breathing steady on the other side of the flames.

*When it gets warm, you're close.*

Close to what, she hadn't said. Close to where, she hadn't said. He didn't know if the box was pointing toward something or away from something or toward a version of himself he hadn't become yet. He didn't know if Mara had known.

He filed: *the box is warm. We are moving toward something. I don't know what.*

He filed: *Mara knew it would warm. She didn't tell me why.*

He filed: *Check again later.*

---

He ran the nightly audit without thinking about it—the way he checked his sword for rust, the way he checked the horse's hooves for stones. A body was a tool. Tools needed maintenance.

He started with his hands. The cold was ordinary. Good. He flexed his fingers and found the full range of motion, no numbness, no delay. The scarred pads from years of self-taught lightning practice were pale but functional. He made a fist, released it, made it again.

He moved to his chest. The cardiac syncopation was new.

Not new as in *tonight.* New as in *since Midden.* The beat had been steady for most of his life—a rhythm he could find and hold, a foundation he built everything else on. In the past week, the beat had developed a skip. Not a flutter. Not the arrhythmia that followed a hard discharge. A syncopation. A beat that arrived slightly off, like a drummer who had lost the count and found it again a half-step late.

It happened more often now. Once a day, maybe twice. It had happened three times in the cart today, and he had not mentioned it to Dreya. He had not mentioned it because it was not yet a problem. It was a data point. He was collecting data points.

The cold-hands effect was arriving faster too. In Midden, he could go forty minutes of moderate output before his fingers started to numb. Outside Midden, with the iron weight lifted and the magic running clean, it should have been longer. It was not longer. It was shorter. Thirty minutes, maybe, before the cold crept in. He had not tested it precisely. He would need to test it precisely.

He added these observations to the drawer: *cardiac syncopation, frequency increasing. Cold-hands threshold, decreasing. Cause unknown. Monitor.*

The audit was not worrying. Worrying was an emotion, and emotions were not useful for diagnostics. The audit was data. Data was neutral. Data told you what was happening. What you did with it was a separate question.

He had not yet decided what to do with it.

---

"You're doing the thing."

Dreya's voice came out of the dark, low and rough with sleep. Six did not startle. He had heard her breathing change—the shift from asleep to awake, the small pause before she spoke. He had been listening without listening.

"The thing with your hands," she said. "The counting thing."

He looked down. He had been running the audit on autopilot, flexing and releasing, and somewhere in the process he had started tapping. His thumb against his forefinger. The rhythm of his heartbeat. He hadn't noticed.

He stopped.

"It's fine," Dreya said. "I wasn't really asleep. The ground's cold."

She was propped on one elbow now, her hair escaping its knot in the particular way it did when she'd been lying down. The firelight caught the edge of her face, the sharp line of her jaw, the way she was looking at him with the expression she used when she was working through a problem she hadn't solved yet.

"What does it mean," she said, "when you do this?"

She made a gesture.

Six looked at it. She had been watching him—he knew that, he had filed it—but he hadn't realized she'd been memorizing. The gesture she made was a reasonable approximation of his sign for *look behind you.* The general shape was correct. The fingers were wrong. One finger was angled a degree too far left, which changed the meaning from *look behind you* to something closer to *there is a problem with your hat.*

He looked at her hand. He looked at her face.

He made the correct version. Slow. Deliberate. The angle right.

She watched his hand. She tried it again. Still wrong.

He made it a third time, slower.

She got the finger.

"And what does that mean?" she asked.

He tapped twice on his knee. *Acknowledged. Yes. I heard you.*

She didn't know that one yet. He could see her trying to parse it—the two taps, the context, the way he had used it earlier in the day with the garrison rider. She was good at patterns. She would figure it out.

"I'm going to need a key," she said, and her voice had the particular frustration of someone who was used to understanding things and did not enjoy the experience of not understanding them.

Six looked at the fire. The flames had burned down to embers, red and gold and pulsing with a rhythm he could feel in his chest. The syncopation was there—the off-beat, the half-step delay. He let it pass.

He had never explained the gestures to anyone. There was no formal system. He had invented them out of necessity and preference, the same way he had invented his rhythm. They were not a language. They were a shorthand. A way of communicating without opening the door that words opened. Words had weight. Words created obligations. A gesture was lighter. A gesture could mean *look behind you* or *pay me* or *thank you* without requiring anything else.

Teaching someone else was different. Teaching required translation. Internal grammar into external instruction. He had never done it. He had never needed to.

He discovered, sitting in a cold field at an hour when the only things awake were him and Dreya and the wind, that he did not mind trying.

---

He made the gesture again. *Look behind you.* Slow. Then he pointed at her hand.

She looked at her hand. She looked at his. She adjusted the finger.

"Like that?"

He tapped once.

"And it means... something directional. Behind me. Something behind me."

He tapped once.

"That's the affirmative tap. The one you do when you mean yes."

He tapped once. Then, because he could see her filing it in real time—the same way he filed things, except she did it with words and notebooks and a system she had probably invented when she was twelve—he made another gesture. A single finger, circled at his temple.

She narrowed her eyes. "Is that—are you telling me I'm overthinking this?"

He tilted his head. *Approximate.*

"You are the most irritating person I have ever met."

He tapped twice on his knee. *Acknowledged.*

She made a sound. The short one. Through the nose. She caught it faster this time—she was getting better at that, too—but he heard it. He filed it. He did not allow his expression to change.

"Show me another one," she said.

He considered. Then he made the gesture for *hungry.*

She looked at it. "That's... two fingers to the mouth, then a circle. Something about eating? Food?"

He tapped once.

"Hungry. That's the hungry sign."

He tapped once.

"You've been signing *hungry* at me for two days, and I thought you were just—" She stopped. She looked at him. "You do that on purpose."

He tilted his head approximately three degrees in the direction of *I don't know what you're talking about.*

"You absolutely do it on purpose." She was sitting up fully now, the bedroll pooled around her waist, the cold forgotten. "The inn. The stool. The bar. You made the sign three times in twelve minutes. I counted. You were doing it on purpose."

He made the gesture for *approximate.*

"That is not a denial."

He made it again.

"The word you're looking for is *yes.*"

He tapped twice. *Acknowledged.*

Dreya stared at him for a long moment. Then she lay back down, pulling the bedroll up to her chin, and said, to the sky: "I'm travelling with a man who has been secretly mocking me in a language I didn't speak."

He made the gesture for *continue but briefly.*

"I know that one now. That's the one you did when I was reading the pamphlet. 'Continue but briefly.'" She turned her head to look at him. "You are not as subtle as you think."

He tapped once.

"That was the affirmative. You just agreed that you're not subtle."

He tapped once.

"That's also the affirmative."

He tapped once.

She made the frustrated sound—the one that was not quite a laugh and not quite a groan, the one she made when she was losing an argument with someone who was not using words. Six filed it. He filed the way she pulled the bedroll tighter. He filed the way she was still looking at him, even now, even tired, even cold.

"Goodnight," she said.

He tapped once.

She closed her eyes. Her breathing began to slow. The fire settled into its embers. The wind moved across the field, and somewhere in the distance an owl called twice and was silent.

Six stayed where he was. He did not sleep. He never slept first—that was another Warrens habit, one he had never examined and never broken. He sat with his back to the stone wall and his sword within reach and the iron box warm against his chest, and he listened to Dreya's breathing find its rhythm again.

The syncopation came and went. He noted it. He did not worry about it. Worrying was not useful.

What was useful: the box was warm. They were moving toward something. He didn't know what. He didn't know where. But Mara had known the box would warm, and she had told him *when it gets warm, you're close*, and she had not told him what he was close to.

He would find out. That was what the road was for.

He tapped his thumb against his knee—once, twice—and found the beat still steady, the syncopation gone for now, the rhythm holding. The box was warm. The night was cold. Dreya was asleep.

For now, that was enough.
