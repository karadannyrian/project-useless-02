# Chapter 2 — Toll Roads and Soft Lies

**POV:** Dreya  
**Setting:** The Thornfield Way; waystation inn called The Crooked Axle

---

The inn announced itself by smell before sight: woodsmoke, old hay, the particular sour-sweet of a stable that hadn't been mucked since the last rain. Dreya catalogued these things the way she catalogued everything—a habit from the Academy that had become a habit for survival. *Poor ventilation. Decent hearth. Kitchen functional but not ambitious.* She stepped down from the cart before Six could offer a hand, not because she didn't need one—her leg still pulled where the crossbow bolt had gone through, a dull reminder that sat beneath the surface of every movement—but because she had learned, in two years of exile, that accepting help was a transaction, and she was already running a deficit she couldn't calculate.

Six tied off Provenance at the stable rail with the same unhurried economy he applied to everything. He didn't ask if she wanted to rest. He looked at the inn door, looked at her, and tilted his head approximately two degrees in the direction of *I'll follow your lead on this one.* She was getting better at reading the degrees.

The Crooked Axle was the kind of waystation that survived by being exactly adequate. Low ceiling, heavy beams, a fire that had been burning since autumn and would burn until spring. The common room held a dozen travellers—merchants mostly, a few farmers, one man in the corner with the sun-bleached cloak of a long-distance courier. Conversations hummed at the register Dreya thought of as *transactional murmur*: prices, road conditions, who had been seen where with whom.

She found a table near the fire, not too close, not too far. The position gave her sightlines to both doors and the kitchen entrance. She hadn't always thought in sightlines. That was new. That was Midden.

Six did not sit at the table. He moved to the bar with the quiet drift of someone who had learned to occupy space without claiming it, and he settled onto a stool near the end, his back to the wall, his face to the room. The stool was too small for him. Most things were. He didn't seem to notice, or if he did, he'd decided it wasn't worth the effort of caring. Dreya had watched him make that calculation about seventeen times in the past two days. It was becoming one of her favorite things to observe.

She ordered food—bread, stew, a pot of tea that the innkeeper's daughter brought with an expression of profound disinterest—and let the room's noise settle around her. Two merchants at the next table were deep in an argument about tariffs. Dreya didn't mean to listen. She meant it the way one means not to breathe: it was going to happen regardless.

"—and the authentication levy's gone up again. Three silvers per stone now, and half the stones in the last shipment were below threshold."

"The Duke's fee?"

"Who else? Cerdon's been tightening the screws since solstice. Every sun-stone that passes through his checkpoints pays a tithe, and if you want the Church seal that says it's genuine, you pay double. It's a monopoly dressed up as quality control."

"The Church won't push back?"

"The Church *is* the seal. The Duke controls the distribution chain, the Church controls the blessing, and between them they've got every trader on the Thornfield Way by the throat." The merchant—a heavy-set man with a Valerian accent and the reddened nose of someone who sampled his own wine inventory—shook his head. "I've got a crate of stones in my cart that I can't sell without authentication, and I can't get authentication without paying a fee that eats my margin. So here I sit, drinking beer I can't afford, waiting for a miracle."

Dreya's teacup paused halfway to her mouth.

*There it is,* she thought. *The Duke's reach. Two days south of Midden and he's already in the room.*

She set the cup down and turned in her chair. The motion was smooth, practiced—the particular pivot of someone who had learned to insert herself into conversations without appearing to. "Forgive me," she said. "I couldn't help overhearing. You mentioned a crate of stones awaiting authentication?"

The merchant looked at her. Then at her clothes—wool trousers, quilted jacket, the Valerian cut that marked her as something between a scholar and a minor noble. Then at her face, which she had arranged into an expression of polite, professional curiosity.

"Academic consultant," she said, before he could ask. "Arcane authentication. If you need a seal that isn't the Church's, I can provide one. It won't carry the Duke's weight, but it'll satisfy any buyer who cares more about function than politics."

"You can authenticate sun-stones?"

"I can authenticate anything that stores magical charge. Sun-stones aren't complicated. They're just picky."

The merchant exchanged a glance with his companion—a thinner man, nervier, who had been nursing the same ale for an hour. The thinner man shrugged. The merchant turned back to Dreya.

"How much?"

"Two silvers. Flat fee, regardless of how many stones pass. If any fail, I'll tell you why, and you can decide whether to sell them at a discount or use them yourself."

The merchant considered this. Two silvers was less than the Duke's per-stone tithe on a crate of twenty. The math was not complicated. "Done," he said. "Crate's in the stable. I'll have my man bring it in."

Dreya nodded and turned back to her tea. The negotiation had taken forty seconds. She allowed herself a small, private satisfaction: the kind that came from being useful, from solving a problem that was exactly the size of her skills. It was a good feeling. It was also, she had learned, a dangerous one. Competence attracted attention. Attention attracted questions. Questions were doors she preferred to keep closed.

She was reaching for her teacup when she noticed, across the room, that Six was no longer alone.

The man standing beside Six's stool was a carter—broad-shouldered, red-faced, built like an ale barrel that had learned to walk. He wore a leather apron and the particular expression of someone who had never been told that the space he occupied belonged to anyone else. He was standing next to Six. More precisely, he was standing next to the stool Six was sitting on, and he was standing there the way a man stands when he expects a seat to become available.

Six did not move.

Dreya watched, her teacup forgotten, as the carter cleared his throat. Six took a slow sip of his drink. The carter shifted his weight. Six looked at the wall in front of him with the focused tranquility of a large animal that had decided not to notice something. The carter said, loudly enough to carry across the room, "That's my usual stool."

Six turned his head.

He did not glare. He did not challenge. He simply looked at the carter with amber eyes that were flat and patient and entirely without agreement. A pond. Nothing moving underneath. The carter waited. Six waited. The carter was not, Dreya suspected, a man who was used to waiting.

Twelve seconds passed.

The carter moved to a different stool.

Six looked back at the wall.

Dreya pressed her lips together very hard. The suppressed laugh was a pressure behind her teeth, and she held it there with the discipline of someone who had been taught, at fourteen, that a lady did not laugh at strangers in public houses. The Academy had not covered this exact scenario, but the principle felt applicable.

She was still pressing her lips together when she became aware, with the particular prickle of being observed, that Six was looking at her. He had not turned his head. He was watching her from the corner of his eye, the way he watched everything, and the corner of his mouth had moved approximately one millimeter in the direction of *I see you.*

She looked away. She looked at her tea. The tea was not helpful.

The merchant's crate arrived—a rough-hewn wooden box packed with straw, twenty sun-stones nestled inside like eggs. Dreya unpacked them with the care of someone handling things that might, if mistreated, release several hours of stored daylight into her palms. The authentication process was simple: a small pulse of air magic, calibrated to the stone's resonant frequency, which would either hold and brighten the stone's internal charge, or scatter and leave it dim. She could do it in her sleep. She had done it in her sleep, once, during the Academy's final examination week, and had woken to find her dormitory room lit like midday.

The work took her twenty minutes. Eighteen stones passed. Two failed—micro-fractures in the crystal lattice, invisible to the eye, that would bleed charge over the course of a week. She explained this to the merchant, who nodded as if he understood crystal lattice theory and was simply choosing not to comment on it.

"Your fee," he said, pressing two silver coins into her palm. "And my thanks. You've saved me a week of haggling with the Duke's assessors."

"The Duke's assessors are in Sondell," Dreya said, the words coming out before she'd decided to speak them. "The authentication checkpoint at the border is handled by a Church adjunct. You'll want to route through the eastern pass, not the main gate—the adjunct there is newer, and more willing to accept independent certifications."

The merchant blinked. "You know the border checkpoints."

"I know the bureaucracy." She pocketed the silver. "Safe travels."

She turned back to her table and found her tea had gone cold. The fire had burned lower. The common room was thinning as travellers retired to their rooms or their carts. And Six, at the bar, was still sitting on the same stool, his drink now empty, his expression unchanged.

He caught her eye. He made a gesture: two fingers to his mouth, then a small circular motion.

Dreya blinked.

He did it again, very slightly slower, as if she was having difficulty with a simple concept.

She mouthed across the room: *"Are you hungry?"*

He nodded once, gravely, as though this was important news.

She held up a single finger—*one minute*—and turned to gather her things. The innkeeper's daughter was clearing the merchant's table. Dreya collected her notebook, her pen, the small leather pouch that held the silver coins. She was calculating the cost of two meals against their remaining provisions when she looked up and saw Six making the gesture again.

She held up two fingers. *Two minutes.*

He waited.

He made the gesture a third time.

A very small muscle in Dreya's jaw twitched, which was what she did instead of laughing in professional contexts.

*He is doing that on purpose,* she thought, crossing the room toward the bar. *He is absolutely doing that on purpose. No one is actually hungry three times in twelve minutes.*

She stopped. She considered the thought.

*I'm not certain of that.*

She reached the bar. Six looked at her with the same expression he had worn for the carter, the same expression he wore for garrison riders and hostile mages and the general inconvenience of being alive: patient, still, waiting for the world to catch up to whatever he already knew.

"Hungry," she said. "I gathered."

He made the gesture again, and this time it was definitely, absolutely, without question, deliberate.

"I have two silvers," she said. "I just earned them. You could say thank you."

He considered this. He made a gesture she didn't know: one hand open, a small downward tilt, as if tipping something invisible toward her.

"Is that thank you?"

He tapped twice on the bar.

*Yes.*

"Good. Then you can also wait while I order." She turned to the innkeeper's daughter, who had been watching their exchange with the glazed expression of someone who had stopped trying to understand the world several hours ago. "Two bowls of the stew. Whatever meat's in it. And bread."

The girl nodded and vanished into the kitchen.

Dreya settled onto the stool beside Six. It was too tall for her, and her feet didn't quite reach the floor. She didn't mention this. He didn't mention that she didn't mention it. They sat in the particular silence of two people who had spent enough time together that silence had stopped being awkward and started being a shared resource.

"The Duke's tariff," she said, quietly, not looking at him. "It's a monopoly. He controls the authentication pipeline, the Church provides the seal, and every trader on this road pays a tithe to get their stones blessed. The merchants are scared of him. The Church is in his pocket, or at least the part of the Church that handles sun-stone distribution."

Six tapped once. *Acknowledged.*

"If Mira's in Luminarch, and the Duke controls the sun-stone trade, then—"

She stopped. She didn't finish the thought. She didn't need to. The shape of it was clear enough: if Mira was being held by the Duke, it was about sun-stones. It was always about sun-stones.

Six was looking at her. Not the flat pond-stare he used on garrison riders. Something quieter. She didn't have a word for it yet, and she was still in the habit of naming things. She would find one later.

The stew arrived. Two bowls, thick and brown, with bread that was only slightly stale. Dreya ate. Six ate. The fire crackled. The last of the merchants drifted upstairs.

When she finished, she pushed her bowl away and said, to no one in particular, "We're going to have to go through the Duke's territory. His checkpoints. His assessors. His Lictors."

Six looked at her.

"I know," she said. "We knew that when we left Midden." She touched her silver ring—the one on her left thumb, the one with no visible enchantment. A habit. She stopped herself mid-motion and put her hand flat on the bar. "It's just different, knowing it and seeing his fingers in every pie between here and the border."

Six made a gesture: one hand flat, the other making a slow, deliberate sweep across it.

She was still learning, but this one she knew. *The road is the road.* Or possibly: *What's already in motion stays in motion.* The grammar was still imprecise, but the meaning was clear enough.

"Yes," she said. "Thank you for that deeply philosophical contribution."

He made another gesture—the one she was beginning to recognize as his equivalent of a shrug, a tilt of the head and a small outward turn of both hands.

*You're welcome.*

She made a sound. The one she always tried to catch before it escaped. This time, she didn't quite catch it.

Six looked at the wall. The corner of his mouth had moved again. She pretended not to notice. He pretended not to notice her pretending.

Outside, the wind had picked up, and the sign of The Crooked Axle creaked on its hinges. Somewhere in the stable, Provenance stamped a hoof and settled. The road was still there, waiting for morning, and the Duke's shadow stretched further than Dreya had realized.

But the stew was warm. The silver was in her pocket. And beside her, Six tapped a rhythm on the bar—once, twice, steady—that sounded, against all evidence, like *the day was alright.*

She didn't write that one down. She didn't need to.
