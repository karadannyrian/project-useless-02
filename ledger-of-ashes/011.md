## Chapter 11: Corvin Vell

---

The Eyrie was cold.

White stone. High ceilings. The kind of quiet that came from thick walls and old magic and the knowledge that the ground was a thousand feet below. Sentinel quarters lined the corridor—doors of pale oak, numbered in silver script. Six didn't count them. He was looking for one door. One name.

Corvin Vell.

The Sentinel who'd hired him in a canal-side tavern three days ago. Off-duty. Out of uniform. Coin in hand and a job that should have been simple. *Basement guardian. Old. Should be quiet.* Should be.

Six stopped at the door marked VELL.

He didn't knock.

---

Corvin was younger than Six remembered. Or maybe he just looked younger in the cold light of the Eyrie—pale skin, thin hair, a face that had never seen a battlefield. His quarters were sparse. Neat. The room of a man who owned nothing he couldn't carry.

He looked up when they entered. His eyes found the ledger in Six's hand. His face went grey.

"You found it," he said.

Six dropped the ledger on the desk.

Corvin didn't touch it. Just stared at it. At the Imperial seals. At the pages Dreya had translated, annotated, cross-referenced with everything she knew about Trade Council procedures and Accordance import taxes and the exact dimensions of Lot 7's foundation beams.

"I didn't think you'd get this far," Corvin said. His voice was thin. "The construct should have killed you. Or the hunters. There were hunters. I made sure there were hunters. I tipped the Empire's people before I hired you. They were supposed to intercept you in the Archive. Take the ledger. Leave no witnesses."

Six didn't move.

"Then you survived," Corvin said. He laughed. It wasn't a real laugh. "You survived, and you brought it here. To me. Of course you did. I was the only contact you had."

He reached for the ledger. Opened it. Read.

His face went from grey to white.

"You need to leave," he said. "Now."

---

Dreya stepped forward.

She'd been quiet since the anchor point. Since the duel. Since she'd put her hand on his arm and he hadn't shaken it off. Now she moved with the same calm precision she'd used in the Shaded Market, explaining to an Imperial mage why her deal was a lie.

"You're going to blackmail Councilor Ashford," she said. "That was always the plan. You didn't want to expose the conspiracy. You wanted to own it. Proof of treason gives you leverage. Leverage gives you a seat at the table. A promotion. Maybe a Council posting of your own."

Corvin stared at her.

"The Empire doesn't negotiate with blackmailers," Dreya said. Her voice was calm. Almost gentle. "They don't pay ransoms. They don't honor threats. The moment Ashford realizes you have this ledger, she'll send someone to kill you. If she can't, the Empire will. You're not a player in this game. You're a loose end who thinks he's a player. That's worse."

She paused. Tilted her head.

"You've doomed yourself, Sentinel. And you don't even know it."

She sounded almost pitying.

Six had never heard that tone from her before. Not the lecturer's precision. Not the feverish ramble. Not the brittle deflection. Something quieter. Sadder. The tone of someone who'd watched people destroy themselves and knew what it looked like.

Corvin's hand trembled on the desk.

"You don't understand," he said. "You don't know what it's like in the Order. The politics. The factions. I've been a junior Sentinel for twelve years. Twelve years of watching men with better bloodlines and worse judgment get promoted over me. I needed leverage. I needed—"

"You needed to not betray your own Order to the Empire," Dreya said. "That would have been a start."

Corvin's face twisted.

He grabbed the ledger.

Threw it into the fireplace.

---

Dreya lunged.

Six had never seen her move that fast. Not running from the hunters. Not climbing the shaft in the God-Fall chamber. Not dragging herself through the canal tunnels with a bolt in her leg. One moment she was standing beside him. The next she was at the hearth, her hand outstretched, her fingers shaping a spell he'd never seen her cast.

The air in the room snapped.

The flames guttered. Bowed inward. Died.

The ledger sat in the fireplace, its edges charred, its cover smoking. But the pages were intact. The seals were legible. The proof was still there.

Dreya grabbed it. Her hand came back red—burned, maybe, or just singed. She pressed her other hand to her chest. Her breath caught. A flutter. A wrong-beat. Her hand pressed flat against her chest. She stayed like that a moment, waiting for the rhythm to settle.

Six looked at Corvin.

The Sentinel was backing toward the door. His mouth was open. His hand was reaching for the alarm cord on the wall.

Six crossed the room in three strides. His sword came out. The tip of it found Corvin's throat.

Corvin stopped. His hand froze an inch from the cord. His eyes were white and wide.

Six could kill him.

It would be easy. One thrust. The same thrust he'd used on the man in iron, the same angle, the same clean entry. Corvin wasn't wearing armor. Wasn't a fighter. Wasn't anything but a small man who'd made a large betrayal.

But Corvin wasn't worth it.

Not the blood. Not the weight. Not the cold that came after when the lightning had nowhere to go and the heart had to find its rhythm again.

Six opened his mouth.

"You're not worth the Toll."

His own voice sounded strange. Low. Hoarse. He hadn't used it since the herbalist's den, and it came out rougher than he remembered. Calmer.

Dreya's eyes went wide.

She stared at him. Eyes wide. Mouth slightly open. She'd never heard him speak. Not really. Not words. Not a full sentence delivered in that low, even tone, like a fact that didn't need arguing.

He didn't look at her.

He pulled the sword back. Let Corvin slump against the wall. The Sentinel's legs gave out. He slid to the floor. Alive. Breathing. Not worth the cost.

---

"Guards!" Corvin's voice cracked on the word. "Guards! Intruders! They're—"

Six was already moving. He grabbed the charred ledger from Dreya's hand. Pulled her toward the door. Behind them, Corvin was still shouting, still scrambling, still alive.

The corridor outside was still empty. The alarm hadn't spread yet. They had minutes. Maybe less.

Dreya stumbled beside him. Her hand was still pressed to her chest. Her breath was still catching. But she was moving. She was always moving.

"The stairs," she said. Her voice was thin. "There's a service stairwell at the end of the corridor. It leads to the lower anchor. We can—"

She stopped.

Looked at him.

"You spoke," she said.

Six didn't answer.

"You spoke, and you said—" She shook her head. "You haven't spoken to me. Not once. Three days. Three days and you haven't said a single word to me, and now you just—"

She was rambling. Not the academic ramble. Not the deflection ramble. Something else. Faster. Breathless.

He kept walking.

She limped after him. "You're not worth the Toll. That's what you said. You decided not to kill him because it would cost too much. Not morally. Not ethically. Magically. You weighed his life against the cold in your fingers and you found him wanting. That's—do you know how remarkable that is? Do you have any idea—"

He didn't answer. He never answered.

But she'd heard him now. She knew what his voice sounded like. Low. Calm. Hoarse from underuse.

She didn't stop talking about it until they reached the stairwell.

He almost smiled.

Almost.
