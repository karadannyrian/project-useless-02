# Chapter Ten — The Eyrie Approach

---

Dawn. Stone platform, iron rings set into the edge, the anchor chains running up into the fog that sat above Midden most mornings and wouldn't burn off until midday. The chains disappeared into it. Somewhere above the fog: the Eyrie.

The armored figure was waiting.

He stood at the far end of the platform—no movement, no impatience, just present the way he was always present, as if the fact of him was the point and everything else was detail.

Six looked at him. Looked at the chains. Looked at the door at the base of the anchor tower, which was where he needed to be.

There was no other way to it.

He helped Dreya to the shadow behind a dock bollard—she could stand now, just barely, the fever broken and her legs remembering what they were for. He pressed a hand to her good shoulder, held her eyes, and released.

She understood. She went behind the bollard and stayed.

He walked out onto the platform.

The armored figure turned.

In dawn light the face was the same as in the bunker: the acid burn pulling the left jaw tight, the pale blue eyes that held nothing readable, not anticipation, not reluctance. He was already holding the shock-maul. The capacitor, Six noted from twenty feet, was dark. Uncharged.

The armored figure moved first.

Slow. He was always slow. But slow and large and perfectly committed created a geometry that fast had to work around, and the platform was not large. Six gave ground—step, step, step—working the angle, keeping the arc of the maul from closing off the exits behind him.

He tried the left side of the gorget. Deflected, same as before. The armor took it without comment and the follow-through came wide, and he gave up another step.

Don't let him reset. Keep him committed.

He moved back. The armored figure advanced. The maul coming in long arcs—deliberate, each one placing Six where the next could land. The reduction of available space. Patience as a weapon.

Somewhere in the second minute, Six thought about the current in his chest. The cost of a speed boost here, this much iron around him—how it would ground out, how the jolt would return. His heart's memory of what that had felt like. He thought about it and put it away.

He waited.

The forward arc. The full commitment, the body following the swing through, both feet planted and then the momentum carrying the follow-through too far—

He sidestepped right at the moment of extension. The armored figure's left arm came forward with the swing, his body turning into it, and the armpit opened for the length of a breath.

Six's blade went up and in.

The armpit gap. The only joint the iron didn't line.

Upward angle. Clean.

The armored figure stopped.

He stood. The maul didn't drop—the grip held it, some residual instruction keeping the hand closed. His pale eyes were very still.

"Clean thrust."

Two words. Low. Quiet. Each one doing exactly its work.

Then he fell.

The armor hit the stone platform and the sound went into the anchor chains and up and out of hearing, and what was left was the platform and the chains and the fog above and Six standing over the body breathing harder than the duel accounted for.

He stood there.

Not moving.

He looked at the armored figure's face. The acid burn on the left jaw, the pale blue eyes open and finding nothing. Seven chapters of weight—stone vibrations in tunnel floors, rotten wood collapsing, the sound of the shock-maul moving air, a laugh that had come from somewhere behind iron and meant something he'd filed and never fully examined. *Clean thrust.* Two words at the end of all of it, and then the armor on the stone.

He stood with that for a moment.

He heard her behind him—uneven footsteps, the limp she was managing, the sound of someone moving faster than they should. Her hand found his arm.

He didn't shake it off.

He looked up at the anchor chains going into the fog above Midden—the Eyrie somewhere above it, and Corvin Vell somewhere above that, and the ledger in his coat that had started all of this on a basement floor three days ago—and breathed until his breathing was something other than the last thing in him from the duel.

Alive. She was alive.

That was the count. That was enough.
