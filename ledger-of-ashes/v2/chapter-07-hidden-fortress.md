# Chapter Seven — The Hidden Fortress (Lot 7)

---

The warehouse smelled like wool and indigo dye and the particular staleness of a building shut at night. Six stood at the loading entrance and looked at the upper-floor windows—dark, no light, no movement.

He could leave. Take the medicine, the ledger, Dreya, and find a way out of Midden before the hunters sealed the last exits. The ledger wasn't his problem. It had been his problem since the Archive and hadn't stopped, but it didn't have to be. He could put it down.

He looked at the warehouse.

Beside him, Dreya said: "For science. And also because I want to know if the construction manifests were accurate. The beam dimensions seemed off by at least three inches—"

He moved toward the entrance.

---

The upper floors were ordinary. Legitimate in the way things are legitimate when they've been designed to be examined: looms in rows, dye vats covered for the night, bolts of cloth stacked on high shelving. Workers' aprons hanging on pegs. A shift schedule on the wall near the foreman's station. The smell of fresh wool and chemical color soaked into the floorboards.

He moved through it with one eye on the floor plan and one eye on how deep the storage section at the far end ran. Deeper than the building's exterior suggested.

The shelving unit at the back was pushed too close to the wall. Standard storage left working room. This didn't.

He put his hand on the wall behind it.

There: a seam, running floor to ceiling on either side. Straight. Deliberate. The wall was dressed stone. The seam was the join of a door built to look like wall.

He looked at Dreya.

She was already looking at the seam, one hand half-raised toward it, her mouth open.

He put his hand on her arm.

She closed her mouth.

---

The staircase descended thirty feet into cold that had nothing to do with weather. Iron bands across every step, iron lining the walls—he felt it the familiar way, the buzz behind his teeth, the resistance that meant his lightning had nowhere useful to go. Beside him, Dreya's hand found his sleeve and held it.

Not for balance. He knew the difference.

They went down.

The bunker opened below them—larger than the floor plan above, the walls extending beyond the warehouse's footprint into excavated bedrock. Cold-iron reinforced. High ceilings. The kind of construction that had taken years and a great deal of money and someone in the Accordance government to keep the permits quiet.

He stood in the shadow of the stairwell entrance and looked at what was there.

Siege components. Trebuchet arms bundled and stacked against the far wall. Battering ram housing. An armory: weapons racks full, armor stands occupied, everything organized and labeled in a hand he didn't know. Barracks running the left wall—bunk frames for fifty, maybe more.

Almost finished. Days, not weeks.

He heard the voice before he found the source.

Low. Measured. Each word doing its job and stopping. Coming from the far end of the bunker, near the armory. He moved along the wall until the angle opened and he saw them.

The armored figure. Without the helmet.

Six studied the face: a deep acid burn across the left jaw, pulling the skin tight from cheekbone to chin. Pale blue eyes catching the lamplight. He was looking at the barracks rather than at the woman beside him while he spoke—the way a man looks at the thing being discussed rather than the person discussing it.

Fragments reached Six from across the bunker. *—two weeks at the outside—* and then the echo took the next part, and then *—the council session on the seventeenth—* and then the low voice fell below what the stone would carry, and then *—it will be sufficient.*

He filed it. The seventeenth.

The woman beside the armored figure was in Accordance council robes—deep blue with gold-thread collar, a trade position. Her face was ordinary in the way that made it hard to hold. Not distinctive. Not remarkable. The kind of face that moved through rooms without leaving an impression. She was nodding. She was not afraid. She held her hands clasped at her waist, relaxed, the posture of someone being briefed on an agenda item.

Six committed her face. The jaw. The line of her brow. The clasped hands.

He turned the fragments over. A date. Siege components enough to breach a defended position. Barracks for fifty. The armored figure had said *sufficient*—and whatever *sufficient* meant, it wasn't a raid or an extraction. You didn't build trebuchet arms and barracks for fifty for a targeted killing. He didn't know the politics. He knew the shape. Someone intended to put this city under different management on the seventeenth, and the fortress below the council chambers was how they intended to do it before anyone noticed.

He also noted: the armored figure was briefing her. Not the other way around. She was receiving information she didn't already have, which meant she wasn't at the top of the arrangement—she was a link in it. Above her was something neither of them could see from here.

Beside him, Dreya had gone very still.

Her breath changed—caught, held. Her free hand came up and covered her mouth.

Then, against his ear, barely sound at all:

"That's Councilor Ashford. Trade Attaché. She's on the ledger."

Two things registered at the same time: the name attaching to the face he already had, and the fact that Dreya knew the ledger's contents without him having shown it to her. He filed the second thing the same place he filed most things. Later.

He put his hand on her arm.

She went quiet.

They watched for another minute—the armored figure finishing his report, the councilwoman nodding once more, both of them turning toward the armory and moving away—and then Six applied steady pressure and they started back up the stairs.

---

The warehouse air tasted ordinary after the bunker. Wool and dye. He moved them through it without stopping, back through the loading entrance, into the street.

Dreya limped beside him. Faster than she should have been managing, which told him something about what her body was running on. He didn't slow down.

He turned it over as they walked.

The Accordance government. A council seat. The armored figure speaking about a date. The ledger had suggested what the fortress confirmed, and the fortress confirmed what the ledger had only implied: this wasn't soldiers and spies working in the dark. It had a seat at the table.

He waited for something to arrive with that. Anger, maybe. Or the particular weight of a purpose settling into place.

Cold instead.

He noticed the absence the same way he noticed iron in a room — as a fact about his current state, worth registering, not worth stopping for. He filed it with everything else.

Later.

He kept walking.
