# Chapter Eight — The Chase Underground

---

Running. The sound of stone giving way somewhere behind them—a low, deliberate groan that wasn't the tunnel settling but the tunnel being made to settle—and he changed direction without slowing.

Being herded. He knew it because collapsed exits weren't pursuit—they were walls. The armored one's weight was in the stone floor somewhere above and behind, heavy and measured and building the box one passage at a time. The exits that way were gone. The exits left—he'd checked twice—gave the same result.

The bolt came from the right.

He pulled Dreya sideways and heard it tick off the wall where her head had been. The air beside him shifted—she was already working, hands up, the shape of a cast coming together—and the second bolt hit the shield and stopped. She exhaled through her teeth.

Third bolt. Fourth. The shooter never stayed in one position. Each shot came from a different angle, different depth—not trying to land them. Trying to cost her. Every shield she raised was something she couldn't spend later, and the shooter knew it.

He found the beat. The rhythm underneath all the noise, underneath the stone groaning and the bolts and Dreya's breath. He pulled the current—

Cold hit his chest first. Then his legs. The speed came up and he went forward into the dark toward the last position, the shooter resolving out of nothing as the distance closed—

Gone. Cloth on stone to his right, then silence.

He let the current go.

Cold to the elbows. Lips numbing. And underneath: a brief stutter in his chest, the heart losing the beat for one wrong second, overcorrecting, trying to come back. He pressed two fingers to his sternum and held the count until the rhythm found itself.

Moved again.

Dreya's breathing had changed—shorter, compressed, each exhale doing work it wasn't built for. Her footsteps were placing slightly less carefully. He heard it before he saw it: blood on his coat at the shoulder. Her nosebleed, come down on him while he was moving. She hadn't noticed. He had.

Then the force bolt came from ahead.

Not the shooter's angle. He hadn't found the grey-haired one's position.

He moved—wrong direction, not fast enough—and the bolt went past him.

Hit Dreya.

The sound she made was not any sound she had made before.

He turned. She was still standing—one hand against the tunnel wall, her other arm pulled tight to her body, the shoulder wrongly angled. Her mouth was open. The scream was already in the stone, bouncing back from every surface at once, raw and involuntary and nothing like her other sounds, any of them.

She closed her mouth.

Said nothing.

He crossed to her and got his arm under hers. She took the support without comment, the arm tucked against her chest to hold the shoulder still. She didn't explain. She didn't apologize. She went quiet with a different quality than her usual quiet.

He gave it no word.

He pressed two fingers to his sternum—the count again, the cardiac flutter still receding, cold in his hands, lips pale—and started moving.

Behind them somewhere, a cough. Wet. The grey-haired one had paid for the force bolt. The payment was in her chest. Six filed it without looking back.

The narrow passage was where he remembered it—a low gap in the wall, shoulder-width, the kind that looked like it went nowhere. He'd found it at twelve when someone was chasing him then too.

He went through first. She followed sideways, favoring the shoulder, silent about it.

The passage opened above a channel. Six feet of dark air and then black water below, the smell of canal rot coming up in a wave.

He dropped.

Impact. Canal cold—the still kind, the kind that had nowhere to drain—total and immediate. He came up and found her already surfacing beside him, one arm working, the other held against her body.

Nothing came through the passage above. He held the count.

He got a hand on the canal wall and let the current carry them downstream, under the wide flat hull of a barge, into the shadow of the wood above. Algae. Slime. Good cover.

Her breathing steadied. Short. Controlled. Not getting worse.

He held on. They floated.
