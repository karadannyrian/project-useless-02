# Chapter Five — Into the Dark

---

Dark and footsteps.

His hands found the left wall before his eyes had tried to adjust—there was nothing to adjust to—and he counted the chisel marks against his palm. Still there. Different builders at different times, the marks changing pattern every few dozen feet. He'd learned to read them young. Not reading exactly. More like feeling a route.

Dreya's glow-stone came out of her coat pocket maybe thirty seconds in. Small, alchemical, dim enough to be almost an insult to the dark. She cupped it in one hand and looked at him like she was expecting commentary.

He kept moving.

She fell in beside him. Four steps of silence.

"The original tunnels date from the God-Fall period," she said. Her voice was low—instinctive, like even she understood sound traveled down here—but steady. "Emergency shelters, primarily. The Accordance expanded them during the third century when the river trade routes started choking the upper city. There are load-bearing walls in the lower sections that were never meant to be load-bearing—you can see the stress fractures if you know what to look for." A pause that wasn't a pause. "The acoustics are also deeply problematic from a tactical standpoint. A whisper at one intersection can be heard three tunnels over, depending on the angle. It's a function of the stone curvature. I'd be more specific but I'd need to measure."

He listened to her talk and to the tunnel and to the drip that meant the next left branch was forty feet ahead.

Left. Then right. Then the long straight passage that smelled like rust.

He turned right. His sleeve brushed one of the iron bands set into the wall at intervals—cold under the cloth, rough with old rust. He kept moving.

Something moved above them.

Not footsteps. A weight the stone floor didn't belong to, registering through the soles of his boots as a single, distant press—from the direction of the Docks, heavy, measured, not repeated.

He stopped.

She stopped.

He held the count. Five. Ten. Fifteen.

Nothing.

He moved again. She followed, and after a breath she was talking.

He needed the sound.

There was something else in these tunnels. He'd caught it twice since they'd entered—a footfall, present and then not, too light for the rats that lived in the lower passages. The particular quality of stillness that wasn't emptiness but waiting. Whatever it was, it was moving parallel. Not closing. It wanted to know where they were going before it made a decision.

He kept his pace. Didn't change direction. Let it watch.

She was still talking when the other thing fell back—maintaining its distance, patient. He kept his pace and let her.

Her words had changed. Not the content. The rhythm. A consonant running into its neighbor where it hadn't before.

He noticed before she did.

"—the second-century expansion records are incomplete," she continued, "but the architectural evidence suggests at least three distinct building phases, which means the western passages are structurally different from the eastern ones, which is—from the—"

She stumbled.

His arm caught her before she found the floor. She grabbed his coat sleeve and held on, breathing hard, and he put his free hand to her forehead—

Wrong heat. Not effort. Not cold air. The specific heat of something working against the body from the inside, winning.

She straightened. "I'm fine," she said. Her voice was almost steady. "The floor is uneven."

He adjusted his arm and they kept going.

She talked less after that. Shorter sentences, longer gaps. He filled the gaps with the tunnel—the change in the stone smell that meant the old collapse was coming up on the right, the slight upward grade that preceded the dead-end branch, the particular draft that meant the door was twenty feet ahead—

Still there.

The safehouse door was where it had always been, at the end of a passage that most people wrote off before they reached it. Low door, iron latch, the frame swollen on the left side so it caught unless you lifted and pushed at the same time.

He lifted and pushed. It gave.

Inside: cold, dark, the sealed smell of a room that had been waiting. A cot against the far wall. A table. The iron stove in the corner—small, rusted at its joints, but intact.

He helped her to the cot. She sat and then lay down in the same movement, like sitting had been the compromise and her body had finally voted. The glow-stone was still in her hand. He took it and set it on the table.

He found the tinder box by feel. Old tinder, dry enough. He tried a spark from his fingers—felt the iron of the stove push back immediately, the charge dispersing sideways before it could do anything useful—and tried again. The second one caught.

The flame was small. He fed it carefully, and the iron took its time deciding to warm.

He sat on the floor in front of the stove, close enough to feel the heat beginning at his hands and face. His fingers ached as the warmth reached them—the cold finally having something to argue against. He pressed his palms flat to his knees and waited.

Behind him, Dreya's breathing evened.

He listened for the tunnel. The presence that had moved parallel through the passages hadn't followed them in, or if it had, it was staying far enough back that distance was enough. For now.

The stove ticked. The tunnel dripped. She breathed.

He turned and looked at her.

Stove-light on her face: pale, the dark circles under her eyes deeper than the firelight explained. The fever hadn't broken. The wound in her thigh had been doing its work since before the Archive—he could see where the cloth had gone tight around the bolt, the swelling that hadn't been there this morning—and the river and the warehouse and the market hadn't helped it.

She was quiet. The first time since he'd found her.

He told himself he preferred it. Silence meant nothing had followed them. Sound was a liability.

He looked at her for a while.

He turned back to the stove and watched the fire and waited for morning.
