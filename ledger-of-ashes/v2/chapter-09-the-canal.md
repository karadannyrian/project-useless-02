# Chapter Nine — The Canal

---

The barge above them moved—a slow displacement of water, something large nudged by current—and Six held still against the canal wall.

Dreya was against him, her good shoulder to his chest. Her skin was too hot again. The canal water hadn't helped with that.

She was talking.

Not the usual talking. Words without a leash—incomplete, starting without finishing, going somewhere he couldn't follow. He held on and let them come.

"He wasn't supposed to—" The water lapped at the barge hull. She didn't finish the sentence.

He said nothing.

"I was at the Academy. It was a normal day." She said this clearly, which was worse than when the words were slurred. Clear meant it was close, wherever she was.

The barge drifted. They drifted with it, into the shadow of the next channel.

"Guilt by association." The same precise tone—quoting something, a document, a phrase she'd read once and couldn't release. "That's what they called it."

He found the canal boat two channels east—a flat-bottomed working vessel tied to a private ring, a name on the hull he'd seen before. The padlock on the cabin door was cheap. He broke it with one hand and didn't look at what that said about who wasn't coming back to use it.

Inside: a cot, a stove, a shelf with bandages and a kit he recognized by smell. He carried her in and got her down and lit the stove—iron pushing back, the spark half-eaten, the second one catching—and the warmth began.

She talked through most of it.

"My family." Longer pause. Her breathing had the wrong rhythm of fever, not effort. "Exiled. I fled. I didn't—"

He cleaned the shoulder wound. Wrapped it. She didn't react to his hands, which meant the fever had most of her.

"Two years. I don't know if they're—"

She didn't finish that one either.

"Aunt Mira." A different quality to this—softer, the way names sound when they carry a lot of weight in a small space. "She would have—"

He went still.

"Aunt Mira—"

He knew the name the way he knew names that arrived without a file to put them in: as a sound, as a person, as someone she had loved. He stored it with the rest.

She slept after that. The fragments had run dry, or the fever had taken her deeper, and what remained was just her breathing and the stove and the water moving against the hull.

---

He watched the light change through the gap in the cabin door.

The dark thinned first—the particular grey that was night making a concession. Then the grey warming, slowly, until the canal water outside caught color and the Docks district came back to itself piece by piece. Then light proper, and the sounds of the district waking, and the stove having done most of its work.

She slept through all of it.

He checked her twice: forehead, pulse, the shoulder, the thigh. The thigh was better—the poultice had held, the swelling down from where it had been. The shoulder was holding. The fever was still there but running differently now, not climbing.

The light moved through the afternoon. He didn't sleep. He watched her breathe and refueled the stove when it needed it and listened to the canal outside for anything that moved with intent.

Nothing did.

He could have left her against the barge hull. The temperature, the shoulder, the fever—she'd have been gone before the morning found her.

He didn't.

He didn't examine it. He stored it with everything else he wasn't examining and watched the light go flat outside the door and waited for her to wake.
