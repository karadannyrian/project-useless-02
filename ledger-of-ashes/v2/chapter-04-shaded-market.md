# Chapter Four — The Shaded Market (Night)

---

The market was noise first—voices layered over voices, the sizzle of fat on iron, something burning sweet in a distant brazier, the particular press of bodies that had nowhere else to be at this hour. Six found his rhythm in it with more effort than usual. Too many competing sounds. Too much iron in the stalls, in the railings, in the lamp brackets overhead. He counted three exits in the first thirty seconds and filed them.

Dreya was at his shoulder, moving with him, her right leg setting the pace. The cold from the river was still in his hands, deeper now than it had been on the bridge—settled into the joints, the kind of cold that didn't leave until sleep did. He breathed through it and kept moving.

He could smell the wound through her wet coat. Warm. Sharp. The wrong kind of warm.

She needed medicine. He needed to find it without stopping long enough to be found.

He saw the grey hair from across the market.

Wrong color for the face beneath it—early twenties at most, but the hair was streaked through with silver the way hair goes when something burns through it slowly from the inside. She was sitting on a stool at a food stall, a ceramic plate on the board in front of her, a skewer of grilled meat in one hand. Watching them with the patient quality of someone who had been there long enough to pick the best angle.

She had been waiting since before they'd arrived.

He didn't stop moving. He turned them at an angle—toward the medicine stalls, a man with a destination—but she was already adjusting on her stool, already tracking. He changed direction.

They walked toward her.

She didn't get up. Didn't reach for the iron cylinders on the board beside her plate. Just held the skewer and waited until they were close enough, and then: "Give me the ledger. I'll let you walk."

He said nothing.

Beside him, Dreya drew breath.

"That's not a binding offer under any recognized authority structure." Her voice was level—something, given the lean of her against his arm, the effort she was putting into standing straight. "Imperial mages operating outside Empire territory can't grant immunity from witness procedure. The jurisdiction doesn't extend. Which means if you take the ledger and let us walk, you've created two loose ends instead of one, and the Empire's policy on loose ends is documented and consistent going back to the Veld Accords." A pause. "The legal framework here is extrajudicial execution, not negotiation. You knew that when you sat down."

The grey-haired woman looked at Dreya for a moment.

Then she sighed.

"I was hoping you'd say no. Now I have to cast."

She reached for one of the cylinders.

Six was already moving—sideways, toward the food stall at his right, iron brackets, iron frame, the whole thing heavy with it—

He let the current in just enough to find the iron.

He found the iron in the stall and shoved.

The stall jerked sideways, dragging its tarp with it, and the firebolt hit the canvas instead of his chest. Flame exploded. Canvas burned. The stall collapsed in a shower of sparks and smoke.

His hands went cold. Fingers numb. He ignored it.

The crowd went from loud to screaming. The grey-haired woman was already raising the second cylinder, adjusting for the angle—she had good instincts—

Dreya's hands moved.

He'd seen her cast before. The air bubble in the river: controlled, precise, built under pressure. This was different. Her hands moved faster and her jaw set in a way he hadn't seen, and then the grey-haired woman made a sound.

Not a word. Not a scream. The sound of someone reaching for air and finding none. Her free hand went to her throat. The cylinder dropped. The cast she was attempting died in her fingers before it formed, and then she was stepping backward into the crowd—one step and another and another—and the market swallowed her.

Gone.

He held the count. Three. Four. Five.

Nothing moved that was her.

He turned.

Dreya was where he'd left her, hands in front of her, palms up, like she was still holding the shape of the spell. Blood had started at her upper lip. She wiped it with the back of her wrist and looked at the smear from a distance, like the distance was the only way to look at it. Her eyes weren't quite finding focus.

He got a hand on her arm.

She let him.

He pulled her into the crowd—into the chaos the burning stall had made, bodies moving in every direction, no one watching anything except the fire. He kept her close and kept moving and she came with him, one step at a time, her bad leg finding something close to rhythm.

Then she started talking.

"That was a level three evocation." Too fast. Slightly breathless. "Her form was sloppy—the Medium positioning was off by at least fifteen degrees, you could see the scatter in the—" she wiped her lip again without looking at it— "and I gave her a nosebleed just by watching. I didn't even cast. That's a calibration failure on her part, not—it's not—"

Six said nothing.

He'd seen the sequence. The firebolt, the cast, the blood arriving the way blood does when something has been spent that can't go back. The grey-haired woman's own cost for her own spell.

Not Dreya.

He didn't say so. He kept moving.

She was still talking when they found the alley.
