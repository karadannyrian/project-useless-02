# Chapter Three — Dockside Warehouse

---

Wet boots on the loading dock. The planks were waterlogged, soft underfoot, and they gave a low complaint with each step.

He got her up the dock ladder one-handed—she made it most of the way herself, the bolt catching the rungs twice before she found an angle that worked—and then they were on the dock and the river was behind them and the warehouse door was ten feet ahead.

The smell hit before he opened it.

Fish. Not fresh fish—the other kind, the kind that had been sitting in its own history for longer than the building deserved. Salt and rot and something underneath both of those, sweet in a way that wasn't sweet at all.

He breathed through his mouth and pulled her inside.

She didn't.

"The smell alone constitutes a public health violation under three separate Accordance statutes." Her voice was low, barely above a whisper. "Also the floor—do you hear that? That's load-bearing wood expressing regret. And I want it noted that fish-packing establishments in Valeris are legally required to maintain minimum ventilation standards, which this structure has clearly—"

He put his hand over her mouth.

She went still.

He listened.

Nothing. Just the river against the dock pilings below and the warehouse settling and somewhere in the dark above them the slow drip of water finding its way through a roof that had given up.

Not yet.

He moved them deeper into the warehouse, behind a stack of barrel crates that smelled of salt and abandonment. Dark back here. High ceiling, rotten wooden pillars going up into shadow, the faint glow of Docks lanterns bleeding in through gaps in the wall planking.

He crouched. She crouched beside him, slower, her right leg taking the movement badly.

Her bandage was soaked through—the wet dark of it pressed wrong, flat against the bolt shaft instead of controlling the bleed at the entry point. She had one hand on it. The angle was off. She was doing something, just not the right thing.

He looked away. Not yet.

She was pulling her notebook from her coat—river-soaked, pages stuck together, probably finished. She made a small noise about it that he wasn't going to respond to.

Then: "Dreyara Castellan del Varetta." Quiet. Matter-of-fact, like she was entering something into a ledger. "Dreya. In case the question came up. I realize we haven't—" she looked at the notebook, decided against it, put it back— "formally."

He looked at her.

She looked back, expectant for exactly one second, then: "You already know yours is in circulation. I've heard it from four separate sources. So."

He nodded once. Looked back at the wall.

Dreya.

He filed it.

She started to say something else—

He held up a hand.

Footsteps outside. Not the loose shuffle of Docks nightwork. These were measured. Deliberate. Heavy in a way that had nothing to do with urgency and everything to do with mass.

Then the wall came in.

Not kicked. Not broken. The armored figure simply walked through it, and the rotten wood tore like wet paper and fell away from him without resistance. He stood in the gap he'd made, iron-plated from boots to helm, and the Docks light caught the iron and the room got smaller.

He didn't move immediately. Just stood there. Taking stock.

Six was already moving.

He closed the distance before the armored figure had fully turned—sword up, a thrust at the neck joint where the helmet met the gorget, the gap he could see from across the room, the gap that should have been enough—

The thrust skated off.

Not a miss. The angle was right, the speed was right. The armor just didn't care. His blade went sideways off iron that felt like hitting stone, and there was a half-second where his body had expected a different outcome and was already committed to the wrong next move.

Half a second. He noted it. He wouldn't spend it again.

The shock-maul came around in a wide arc and Six was gone—back and left, inside the swing, not where it landed—but the displaced air from the iron head moved his coat and he felt the weight of it even without contact.

He reset.

The armored figure turned. Unhurried. He moved the way something moves when it knows the room has nowhere to go.

Behind Six, a sharp sound—air drawn tight and fast, a blade-shape forming. He didn't look. He heard her breath catch before the cast, and then the compressed air hit the iron helm.

The magical edge grounded out on contact. He felt it rather than heard it—a flat, absorptive deadness in the room's acoustics, the particular silence of energy going nowhere. But the air itself, already moving, kept moving. Physical matter with nowhere left to be magical.

A scratch. Bright and brief. Metal on metal.

Then the armored figure laughed.

Low. Rumbling. From somewhere behind the helm, through the iron, one sound, unhurried.

Six filed it. First sound he'd made. He'd remember that.

He moved again—no magic, not with that much iron-plate walking toward him, the Toll for nothing was still the Toll—just the sword, just reach and angle. He tested the left side of the gorget. Same result. Tried to drive the blade into the gap at the armpit joint on the forward stroke; the armored figure turned just enough and the angle closed.

He was fast and it didn't matter. Fast wasn't what this needed.

He glanced left.

Dreya was at the bandage again. Both hands, unwinding the soaked cloth—wrong approach, she was losing the pressure she had, the cloth coming away from the entry point entirely—

He knocked the armored figure back with a shoulder into the nearest crate stack—not a real blow, not enough to matter, just enough to buy seconds—and crossed to her. Her hands came up to wave him off. He moved them aside, redirected the cloth, pressed it flat against the entry wound rather than the shaft. She made a sound. He was already back before the displaced crates finished settling.

The armored figure was upright. Still advancing.

Behind him, a small noise from Dreya—not pain, the other kind, the sound of a headache arriving somewhere behind her eyes. She pressed her fingers briefly to her temple and then stopped, like she'd caught herself.

Six circled the armored figure toward the far wall. Kept him turning. Waited for the footfall pattern to give him two steps in the same direction—there, the weight shifting forward, committed—and then went past him on the outside, around to the back of the tallest crate stack.

He put his hands into the base crate and shoved.

The stack went forward—three high, full barrels, brine and salt going everywhere—and came down across the armored figure's back and shoulders. He didn't fall. Didn't go down. But the mass of it took a moment to sort out, iron and brine and broken wood working against each other, and that was enough.

Six crossed back to her, got a hand under her arm, and jerked his chin at the far door.

She was already moving. Her bad leg found something close to speed when it needed to, and they were through the door and out onto the Docks, the river smell replacing the rot smell, and behind them the sound of iron rising out of wreckage with no particular urgency.

"That was a level three abjuration failure." Dreya, between steps, slightly breathless. "His armor should have joints. Every armor has joints." A pause, and then, with genuine bewilderment: "Why doesn't his armor have joints?"

Six didn't answer.

He kept moving.
