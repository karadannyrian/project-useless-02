# Chapter One — The Archive Basement

---

The joint gave. Not cleanly—these things never went cleanly—but it gave, the rust-thick shoulder hinge grinding against his blade like a complaint, and the construct lurched sideways.

Six kept moving. Close the gap. Don't let it reset.

The basement was iron-heavy and he knew it without thinking—the buzz behind his teeth had started the moment he'd come down the stairs. So. Sword. Just the sword.

The construct turned. One arm hung wrong, the shoulder hinge dragging behind it, articulated fingers scraping the stone floor. Still moving. Still oriented on him. Its apertures—two of them, the third dark, dead years before he ever arrived—tracked with a sound like a turning millstone.

It had been something, once. The lines of it—the ribs of reinforced plating, the deliberate geometry of the joints—suggested it had been made with care. Now rust had eaten through the seams. One leg dragged. Half its face was caved in, the armored skull-plating buckled by something it had met a long time before tonight.

Still. Big. Still built to stop a man.

Six found his rhythm.

Not the lightning kind—the iron in the walls would scatter that, turn it sideways, waste what he didn't want to waste tonight. Just the internal kind. The beat that lived under everything else. He tapped his hilt three times with his thumb, found the count, let it settle behind his sternum.

The construct lunged.

He sidestepped left. Late by most standards. Not late for him. Its claws passed close enough that he heard the air part. He let the swing carry it past and brought his blade down hard across the back of its knee—the junction where the lower leg met the upper, where the connecting mechanism sat exposed.

The crunch was wet and wrong and entirely satisfying.

The construct went down on one knee.

He circled. The thing tried to track him, slow now, the damaged leg not responding. One arm came up—the good arm—in a defensive posture that was almost human. Almost like it remembered what defense was supposed to look like.

He drove his blade through the gap between its neck-plating and its upper chest.

The construct groaned. Not a sound from any mouth. Something deeper—the resonance of failing mechanisms, old housing giving way, a pressure releasing all at once from somewhere inside it. It shuddered. The arm dropped.

Then it fell.

It went sideways rather than forward, and the weight of it hit the floor with the sound of someone emptying a forge all at once. Iron on stone. The reverberations came up through his boots.

He held his position. Watched.

Nothing moved.

He kept watching—five, six, seven—before he rolled his shoulder and let his sword arm drop. The rhythm in his chest didn't stop. He kept it going while his ears did the rest.

Water, somewhere. Dripping through old mortar. The faint pull of the river working at the Archive's foundation stones. The cooling tick of the construct's mechanisms losing heat.

He looked at where it had fallen.

The impact had cracked the floor. Not the stone—there was stone and then there was the thing beneath the stone, and the construct had hit the latter. A wooden housing, set flush into the sub-floor, old and painted over so many times it had nearly disappeared. The construct's weight had gone through two of its planks.

He crouched.

The crack ran across the top of a small iron-bound safe. The planks had given but the safe hadn't, not fully—the lid was canted at an angle, the hinge on one side bent past its tolerance. He put his fingers under the edge and lifted.

Inside: a ledger. Small. Dark leather cover. A fold of silk wrapping it, tied with a ribbon.

He picked it up. Turned it over.

The wax seals on the ribbon were Imperial. He knew the sigil—the double-eagle, pressed so precisely it might have come from a stamp rather than a hand. He'd seen it on warrants. On cargo manifests in the Shaded Market, handled carefully by men who handled everything carefully.

He opened to the first page.

Columns. Numbers. Names in a script that wasn't Accordance common, though he could pick out enough to read the shape of it—a format like accounting, or like a list of things owed and things paid. The Imperial double-eagle repeated in the margins. Notes in small, precise handwriting he couldn't follow even when he tried.

He didn't try long.

He folded the silk back around it, tucked it inside his coat, and stood.

And heard footsteps.

Above. The main floor. Two sets, maybe three, moving between the shelving rows with the particular quality of people who didn't want to be heard. Slow. Deliberate. Spaced to avoid each other's sound.

He knew that kind of walking. He'd walked that way himself.

Not Watch. The Watch walked like they owned the floor because they did. These didn't own anything. These were looking for something. Which was worse.

He found the stairs. Took them without the steps creaking, because he knew which boards to miss and he'd checked on the way down.

---

The main floor was a dark place—high shelves, narrow windows near the ceiling that let in light the color of old paper, the smell of dust and binding glue and something beneath both of those, something that had been here longer than the shelves or the books. Stone and damp. The faint presence of water. Close.

He heard her before he saw her.

Breathing—controlled, too controlled, the breathing of someone managing pain. And beneath that, a small sound, almost nothing: the syllables of a spell, mouthed without voice, stopping, starting, stopping.

He looked left.

She was behind a collapsed shelving unit—not a small one, a whole run of it, books spread across the floor in a drift, the wooden frame buckled at an angle. She had her back against it. One hand pressed to her thigh. A crossbow bolt angled up from the muscle, Imperial fletching, the shaft dark.

She was trying to do something with her free hand. Fingers moving in small, careful gestures. The air around her hand shifted almost imperceptibly—and then didn't. Then shifted again, adjusted, controlled, each attempt a small correction rather than a desperate grab. Then went still.

Her eyes found him.

Hazel. Sharp. Even now—bolt in the leg, blood on her hand, three of them somewhere above—she looked at him like she was taking notes.

She mouthed: *There are three of them.*

He assessed.

The bolt wasn't through the artery—she was upright, conscious, still working her fingers. The casting attempts weren't panic. They were methodology. The iron in the walls was getting to her, or the pain was, or both, but she was adjusting for it each time rather than forcing it.

The bolt was Imperial. She'd been reading the fletching on the bolt in her own thigh.

The logical weight of the situation was clear. She couldn't run. The exit was twenty feet ahead. The footsteps above were moving toward the stairs. Taking her along would slow him down, complicate the exit, add a problem he hadn't been paid for.

He stepped over the shelving unit.

Offered his hand.

"Oh, you're the one. The mercenary. The tall one. I've read about you. Well, not read—there aren't files, exactly—but I've heard accounts. The lightning kinetics. Self-taught. Fascinating, really. Most mages with your raw power burn out before they find control, but you've developed a rhythm-based focus, haven't you? I noticed the tapping. It's quite elegant—"

He shrugged.

He turned toward the door.

She grabbed his coat sleeve with the hand that wasn't pressed to her thigh and hauled herself upright against the shelving—somehow—her injured leg taking weight she shouldn't have been putting on it, her free hand catching his arm for balance.

"—which is, yes, fine, we can discuss it later—yes. The exit. Good thinking."

He started moving.

She went with him, still talking.

Above, footsteps found the stairs.
