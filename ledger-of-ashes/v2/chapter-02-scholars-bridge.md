# Chapter Two — The Scholar's Bridge

---

The bridge was old stone, wide enough for two carts to pass, and the night had cleared the foot traffic. Six's footsteps were quiet. Hers were not—she was trying, but her right leg didn't fully cooperate, and every third step had the particular catch of someone managing pain they weren't acknowledging.

He had an arm under her shoulders. She had both hands free and was using one to press against her thigh and the other to gesture at the river below.

"I can walk, I'm just slowly walking."

He kept moving.

The River Vein was black below the railing—twenty feet of dark water, the smell of mud and canal runoff, the sound of current working against the bridge's stone feet. Downstream, the Docks lights flickered. Upstream, nothing. Just the dark of the Archive District and the city carrying on without them.

He heard it before he understood it.

A sound from the railing. Not much—the brief, specific complaint of iron bar under a man's grip, a ring and a creak, and then timber groaning below the deck in a direction he hadn't registered anything moving.

He stopped.

She felt him stop. She stopped talking.

The bridge was quiet.

Then the man came over the railing.

He was fast—faster than men who moved that quietly had any right to be—and he came from the downstream side, one hand releasing the railing as he cleared it, already moving into the space between Six and the far end of the bridge. Small. Precise. A needle between two fingers of his right hand before his feet were fully settled.

The needle caught Six's coat. He felt it—a brief snag, fabric pulling—and then it was gone, bounced, skidded off the stone somewhere behind him.

That close.

Six moved her behind him.

The man was already adjusting. The second needle came from the left hand—Six hadn't tracked the left hand—and it went past his guard. He heard her make a small sound. Not pain. Surprise, then something more careful.

He didn't look at her.

The man didn't press. He was watching. Resetting. The way something watches from cover and waits to see what the wounded thing does next.

Six found his rhythm. The beat was there, steady, and he let the current in—brief, a surge through his legs, a second's worth—

Cold hit his fingers. Both hands. Instant.

He closed the distance.

The man moved. But he had been expecting to be the fastest thing on this bridge and now he wasn't, and the recalculation cost him half a step. Six got his sword hand in and forced him back—two paces, three—toward the upstream railing.

The man went over it.

Not falling. Controlled. One hand catching the railing as his weight swung under the bridge's edge. The iron took his grip with a ring. Timber groaned below. Then the sound moved—downstream along the underside, steady, unhurried—and then nothing.

Six stood at the railing and listened.

Water. Current. His own breathing.

He looked at his fingers. Cold to the second knuckle on both hands. He flexed them, once, twice, until the feeling started coming back.

She was still behind him. He heard her breathing—slower than it should have been, deliberate, the kind of breathing that was doing a job.

He turned.

Her right arm hung differently. The muscle in her forearm had gone rigid—he could see it from here, the particular lock of a limb that was no longer entirely hers. She had her left hand wrapped around it, holding it clear. Her eyes were sharp, but her jaw had the set of someone keeping a count in their head.

He looked at her. Then at the bolt still in her thigh. Then at the far end of the bridge.

The man had retreated to a position Six couldn't reach without leaving the deck, and the underside beams were his ground, not Six's. The next needle wouldn't miss the coat.

One arm out of use. A bolt in her leg. Three hunters who'd had time to move since the Archive.

He couldn't win this with her in it.

The railing was to his left. Twenty feet to the river.

He didn't think about it long.

He got an arm around her waist, lifted—she started to say something, he felt her intake of breath—and put her over the railing.

"You could have warned me!"

He went over after her.

---

The cold was immediate and total. Not the Toll kind—the river kind, the kind that had nothing to do with him and didn't care what he owed. He hit the water badly, no technique, and the current grabbed him before he'd finished the drop.

He couldn't find the bottom.

He kicked. It did something, not enough, and the current was faster than he'd estimated from above. The bridge shadow passed over him—a darker dark against the dark—and then he couldn't tell which way was air.

He didn't panic.

He'd learned early that panic cost more than it bought. He held his breath, found nothing to push against, and waited.

Then the pressure changed.

A small thing—a pocket of still air forming against his face where there shouldn't have been any, like something had cupped its hands around his head. He breathed. It worked. He breathed again.

She was nearby—he could feel the current around her, the resistance of two bodies instead of one, the bolt in her thigh catching the water at a drag angle that pulled her slightly faster downstream. He reached, found her coat, and held on.

The air held.

He let it work.

They moved with the current, under the bridge's shadow and then past it, the sounds above the surface muffling and then gone. He caught one stroke—awkward, enough—and kept them below the waterline until the Docks lights were overhead.

Then the pocket shifted. Her breathing had changed—shallower, effortful. He kicked upward.

They surfaced.

---

She went first, gasping, and then immediately: "—fascinating, actually, the blurring is spiral, not radial, which suggests it's affecting the smooth muscle in the eye rather than—" a cough, river water, more coughing— "—the optic processing, which would mean it's a compound toxin, probably a base alkaloid combined with a muscle relaxant, which is very clever, that's a Khaz-Moran formulation if I—" another cough, "—if I'm remembering my—"

Six got a hand on the stone embankment. Dock-side. The stone was slick with river moss and the smell of fish and iron-heavy runoff.

He hauled himself up. Reached back down.

She was still talking when he pulled her out. Still talking when he set her down on the embankment stones. Still talking—about the toxin, the spiral, the compound formulation, the fascinating specificity of the symptom profile—while water ran off her coat and her breath came back in ragged intervals between sentences.

The bolt in her thigh had shifted in the water. It sat at a worse angle now. She hadn't mentioned it. She was talking about alkaloids.

He looked at the river. Dark, smooth, the current moving on past them without interest.

No sound from the bridge. No weight on any railing.

He flexed his fingers. Still cold, coming back. He found his beat, tapped it twice on his knee.

The corner of his mouth did something. He noticed it and didn't examine it.

He sat down on the embankment stones and let her talk.
