# Chapter Six — The Medicine Run

---

The door closed behind him.

Dark again. Just the tunnel, the drip of water, his own footsteps on stone.

Without her voice the tunnels were a different thing. Not worse exactly. Just quieter than they'd been since the Archive, and the quiet had a weight to it now that it hadn't had before.

He found the left wall and started moving.

The route to the lower market was one he'd walked a hundred times before the mercenary work, before the lightning, before any of it. His hands remembered the texture changes—chisel-marked limestone giving way to older brick at the second intersection, brick to raw stone near the collapsed section he'd always walked around as a kid. He didn't need the glow-stone. He didn't have it.

He'd been moving maybe four minutes when he heard it.

A footfall. Barely. Not a sound so much as a slight wrongness in the air, the way a room changes when something enters it. Then a held breath—deliberate, patient—the specific quality of waiting that meant someone had heard him stop.

He hadn't stopped.

He kept his pace. Same direction. Counted the distance to the next left branch and started closing it.

The presence behind him adjusted. Smooth. No extra footfall, no rush—just a recalibration of angle, weight redistributing across stone floor in increments too small to catch unless you'd spent years learning to catch them.

Better trained. He'd known that from the bridge. He still knew every dead end in this section of the tunnels.

He took the left branch. Then the right. Came around the long loop back toward the Docks side, then doubled east—the presence following, holding its distance, patient. Testing his pattern.

Third intersection. Left.

Dead end.

He stopped at the far wall. The support beam was above his right shoulder where it had always been—old pine, pitted with twenty years of damp rot, the iron bracket half-rusted through. He'd noticed it as a kid and never stopped noticing it. One of those things that kept not falling.

He turned around.

The presence had stopped at the intersection. He could feel it there—hanging back, having read the shape of the passage correctly. Not coming in. Waiting to see what he did.

He put his hands flat against the beam.

Lightning in his chest—not fast, not aimed, just outward, lateral—the charge moving through his palms and into the wood and the wood deciding in one long moment what it had been deciding for years.

The beam cracked.

The crack became a groan. The groan became the tunnel clearing its throat, and then the ceiling came down—stone and old timber and packed earth—between him and the intersection. He pressed himself against the far wall, arms over his head, while the cascade reached its worst and then stopped.

His heart had done something wrong.

He registered it as he registered the beam giving: a fact. Not a beat missed but a beat stuttered—the rhythm stuttering into something irregular, a skip followed by a too-hard correction, the muscle finding the pattern and losing it and scrambling back. Vision pinched inward from the edges. The tunnel got narrower than it was.

He pressed two fingers to his sternum.

Tapped.

Once. Twice. Three times.

Found the count. Held it.

The heart didn't want to cooperate. He gave it the count anyway—two fingers pressing the rhythm into the bone—and after a moment that took longer than he'd have liked, it came back.

Vision opened. The tunnel was the width it had always been.

He breathed.

On the other side of the rubble: silence. No pursuit. No sound of stone being shifted. Just the tunnel settling and then the particular quality of nothing that meant whatever had been following him had made a decision and the decision wasn't to follow.

He moved.

---

The herbalist's lamp was the first real light he'd seen since the stove went out.

Small room, low ceiling, the smell of dried herbs and old tallow and something sharp underneath—the smell he associated with healing and poverty in roughly equal measure. The herbalist looked up when he came in, said nothing, and looked back down at what they were sorting.

He waited.

The herbalist finished sorting. Got up. Went to the shelf without looking at him, without asking where he'd been or how long it had been or what state he was in. Just the efficiency of someone who'd held a lot of debts and knew when one was being cleared.

They set a cloth bundle on the table.

He picked it up.

The herbalist sat back down.

Done.

---

The safehouse door was still closed.

He listened at the latch. Breathing, inside—slow but not sleeping, the particular quality of someone awake and holding still about it. He opened the door.

Cold. The stove had gone out. In the dark: her outline, sitting up on the cot, something laid across her knees.

He recognized the shape.

She held it out when he stepped inside. Both hands, blade pointed away, hilt toward him. Steady. No explanation. Just the sword extended in the dark, waiting to be taken.

He took it.

He crossed to the stove. Found the tinder box by feel, tried a spark—the iron pushed back, the charge dispersing sideways, half of it going nowhere—tried again. Second one caught. He fed the flame until the room started having a temperature.

The glow-stone came on. She must have been holding it.

He sat on the floor with the cloth bundle and opened it. Dried herbs, the right ones. A poultice base in wax cloth. Bandages. He set out what he needed.

Then he looked at the wound.

The bandage had soaked through. He unwrapped it. The bolt was still there—shaft dark, the surrounding tissue swollen and the wrong color in the stove-light, hot to the touch.

He looked at her.

She looked back.

He nodded once. Took hold of the shaft.

She exhaled through her teeth—short, controlled—and didn't make another sound. He pulled the bolt in one movement, put the clean cloth down immediately, pressed until the bleeding slowed to something manageable. Then the poultice: packed in, sealed. Bandage wrapped correctly over the top.

He set the bolt on the floor beside him.

She looked at it. Said nothing.

He moved to the herbs. Boiled water from the canteen over the flame, added the dried leaves in the order that worked—Warrens knowledge, the kind that came from watching and being given nothing except what to do with your hands. He'd used this on himself enough times to know what it tasted like and what it did and roughly how long it took.

"What's in it?" she said.

He didn't answer. He kept working.

She watched him. He could hear her considering something longer—a question or something that wasn't a question—and then setting it aside.

The stove ticked. The medicine steeped.

He filed the sword away. The cold room, the dark, the sitting up and waiting. The steady hands when she gave it back.

He handed her the cup. He looked at the fire.
