# Chapter Thirteen — The Iron Box

---

He heard her before she moved—a change in the quality of her breathing, from the rhythm of deep unconsciousness to something lighter, closer to the surface.

He waited.

She moved. Her head came up. She looked at her hands, at the room, at him.

He was sitting against the far wall with the iron box in his hands. He didn't remember taking it out of the pack. Mara's box. He'd had it out since before she woke.

She looked at it for a moment.

"What's in it?"

The words came out the way they'd come out in Corvin's quarters—slightly wrong in his mouth, the sound of something kept quiet too long. "I don't know."

She didn't push. She lay back and looked at the ceiling. He watched her take stock of herself—the shoulder, the thigh, the accumulated cost of the last few days arriving all at once. Her breathing was steady. The fever was gone from the quality of it. The stove was small but real, and the room was cold except where the heat had reached.

He turned the box over in his hands.

Cold iron. Except it wasn't cold. He'd noticed it when he took it out and he was still noticing it—a warmth in the casing that iron didn't hold, that a room this temperature had no business putting there. He turned it over. The warmth was the same on every face. He set the reasoning down where he set most things and looked at the stove instead.

The ledger was in his coat. Charred edges, Imperial seals smoke-darkened, still legible if you knew what you were looking at. Fifteen days, by his count, until the council session on the seventeenth. Ashford's face was in his memory: the clasped hands, the nod, the posture of someone being briefed on a routine agenda item. She was still behind her council seat. Still with a fortress nearly operational. Still with access to whatever came next.

And no one left to tell.

Corvin would deny everything—and the Order would believe their own over a freelance mercenary and an exiled scholar with a charred ledger and no witnesses. The Eyrie might already have orders out. Empire hunters still active somewhere in the city. The grey-haired one alive on the platform, harmless now, waiting for someone to find her.

Dreya said: "We need to find someone who believes us."

He looked at her. She was watching the ceiling, her voice thin but present. Talking again.

He nodded.

She turned her head. "Who?"

He looked at the box. The warmth was still there.

He shrugged.

That was the problem.

He put the box back in the pack. Not yet. Not today. He didn't know what was inside it. He didn't know why iron that should be cold wasn't. He didn't know who would believe them or what came next.

Those were problems. He'd had problems before. He set them where he set the things that needed time and turned back to the stove.

The fire was low. He fed it one more piece and sat down again.

Dreya's breathing steadied—not sleep, just rest, the particular quality of a body doing the quiet work of putting itself back together. He sat against the wall and let the small warmth reach him and waited for the next thing to show itself.
