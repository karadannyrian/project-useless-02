# Chapter Twelve — The Final Stand

---

The platform had nothing on it. Stone, open air, the iron chain at the center dropping away below, and sky in every direction. Wind. The kind of height that made Midden below look like something drawn rather than something real.

They were waiting.

The grey-haired one was near the chain—one hand braced on it, tremors moving through the arm she wasn't using to hold herself upright. Her skin had gone the color of old ash. Something had dried on her chin and she hadn't wiped it. Her other hand held the last Medium, the iron cylinder dull in the morning light.

She was crying. Not loudly. Just the specific stillness of a face that had been doing it for a while.

The shooter was to the left. Still. Efficient. Unhurried. He'd been bleeding them for days and now he was standing on a platform above the city with nowhere for them to go, and his face had exactly the expression of a man who'd always known how this ended.

Six put himself between them and the chain.

Dreya came up behind him.

The grey-haired one raised the Medium.

He saw the tremor in her hand—the cast requiring more steadiness than she had left. The bolt came out ragged, wide, low, hit the stone at his left and detonated in a wave of heat and pressure that crossed his face and arms.

Too close to dodge fully. He caught the edge of it, took a step sideways, and his footing held.

Dreya stepped forward.

Past him. Into the open center of the platform, into the space between Six and the shooter and the grey-haired one and the edge and everything else. Her chin came up. Her hands came up.

He registered her face.

He registered that he wasn't going to stop her.

The air changed.

Not gradually. All at once—pressure dropping like a door opening in a sealed room, his ears adjusting to a sudden wrongness, the wind around the platform cutting out as the space around her became something else entirely. He felt it in his chest and behind his eyes.

Her hands moved.

The burst came out of her like she'd been holding it for days and the holding was done.

The platform shook. The shooter had been moving—already adjusting, already closing the angle—and the burst caught him mid-step, hit him like a wall of moving nothing, and took him off the edge.

No sound. Not a sound. The canal was dark far below and the air where he'd been standing was just air.

Debris swept the platform—grit, loose stone, the grey-haired one knocked sideways, her Medium skittering across the stone behind her. The chain rang.

Dreya went down.

Not slowly. One moment standing, the next folded—knees first, then sideways, no transition between upright and not. He crossed to her in two steps and got a hand under her shoulder. Her weight was all of it, nothing holding itself up.

He pressed two fingers to her neck. The pulse was there: thin, fast, present. He turned her face. Her eyes were closed.

She was breathing.

He stood.

The grey-haired one was on her hands and knees near the far edge of the platform. The tremors had gotten worse. Her last Medium was somewhere behind her on the stone. She was moving toward him in increments—one palm, one knee, the other palm—and her face was wet.

"I don't want to die here."

He looked at her.

He could see the Medium from where he stood. He crossed to it and picked it up. The charged crystal inside was warm through the iron casing—one spell, still loaded. He put it in his coat.

She'd stopped moving. Watching him.

He looked at her for a moment.

Then he picked up Dreya.

He didn't look at the grey-haired one again. He moved to the chain.

---

Iron. Cold already, before his fingers asked it to be anything else. He adjusted Dreya across his shoulder—weight forward, his arm across the backs of her knees, her head against his back—found the first link and went over the edge.

Don't look down. Next link. Next.

The chain was narrow for this. Both hands would have made it manageable. One hand made it something else. He looped his wrist at each link—grip, release, move, grip—and at one difficult section he pulled the current, brief, just the half-second he needed.

The last warmth left his fingers. Cold to the first knuckle. Then the second.

The grip held.

Below: the Docks, the canal, the city continuing its morning without any knowledge of what had happened on the platform above it.

He counted links.

His heart was steady. After the river and the tunnels and the beam and the duel, after all the times he'd pressed two fingers to his sternum and fed it the count until it agreed—his heart was steady and keeping itself without him.

He filed that under enough.

He kept climbing down.
