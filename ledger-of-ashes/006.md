**Chapter 6: The Medicine Run**

---

He stood. Checked his sword. Checked the door. Checked her—chest rising, falling, too fast, too shallow. Then he was through the door and into the dark before he could change his mind.

The tunnels swallowed him.

---

Alone felt different.

With Dreya, the dark had a shape. Her voice filled it—lectures about limestone and subsidence, words that didn't matter except that they were words. Now there was only water dripping and his own footsteps and the distant, constant hum of iron in the walls. The buzz in his teeth was worse down here. Deeper. The Warrens were built with whatever metal was cheap, and cheap meant iron. Always iron.

He moved fast. Knew the route. Left at the sag, right at the grate, straight through the overflow channel where the water was shallow enough to walk. The herbalist's den was deeper in—past the old market hollow, past the sunken cistern. Twenty minutes if he was alone. Forty if he was carrying someone.

He was alone. He made it in fifteen.

Then he heard it.

A footfall. Soft. Deliberate. The kind of footfall that only happened when someone was trying to be quiet.

Six stopped.

The footfall stopped.

Darkness in both directions. The tunnel was narrow here—old sewer line, curved ceiling, water running down the center channel. No side passages. No doors. Nowhere to hide.

He listened.

Breathing. Not his. Someone else's breath, held and released in a slow rhythm. The rhythm of patience. The rhythm of someone who had all night.

The quiet one.

Six didn't move. Didn't draw. The sound of a blade leaving its sheath would echo for fifty feet in either direction. The quiet one knew exactly where he was. The only question was how close.

Another footfall. Closer. Then nothing.

Waiting.

Six knew this game. He'd played it before—not against the quiet one, but against Watch patrols in the Shaded Market, against rival crews in the Warrens, against anyone who relied on eyes instead of ears. The dark was an equalizer. The quiet one could see in low light, maybe—trained for it, equipped for it—but this wasn't low light. This was absolute. No lanterns. No glow. Couldn't see his own hand in front of his face.

They were both blind. They were both listening.

Six moved.

Not forward. Back. One step. Two. His boots found the stones he'd memorized on the way in—the loose one near the wall, the dry one at the edge of the channel. He walked backward the way he'd come, and he made no sound.

The quiet one followed.

Six could hear him—not his footsteps, but the absence of sound where sound should be. The water drip that stopped when a body passed under it. The air current that changed when someone blocked the tunnel. He was good. Better than good. Trained for this, trained by people who trained shadows.

But Six was raised here.

He knew the dead-end tunnel. Two hundred feet back, off a side passage that looked like it led somewhere. It didn't. It ended in a collapse—old stone, old timber, a support beam that had been holding up nothing for thirty years.

He led the quiet one there.

Not obviously. Not fast. He moved like he was trying to escape—which he was, and which the quiet one expected. The footfalls followed. Steady. Patient. He didn't rush. Didn't need to. He knew Six was leading him somewhere. He was curious. That was his weakness. He wanted to see where this went.

Six stepped into the dead-end tunnel.

The support beam was above him. Thick oak. Rotted through. The collapse beyond it was loose stone and broken mortar. The beam was the only thing holding the ceiling up.

He turned. Faced the dark.

The footfalls stopped.

The quiet one was at the tunnel mouth. Six could feel him—a pressure in the air, a wrongness in the dark. He was waiting. Assessing. He knew this was a trap. He was deciding whether to spring it.

Six didn't give him time.

The rhythm was already there. The drip of water. The beat of his own heart. He tapped it once on his thigh—one, two, three—and the lightning stirred.

Not a blade. Not a bolt. A push. A kinetic shove, aimed at the support beam, and the lightning left his hand in a crack of blue-white light that lit the tunnel for half a breath.

A face. Hooded. Pale eyes. A thin smile.

Then the beam splintered.

The ceiling came down.

Six didn't see it. The light was gone. But he heard it—the groan of stone, the crash of falling rock, the rush of dust and grit that filled his lungs and eyes. He was already backing up, already coughing, already pressing himself against the collapse at the tunnel's end.

The sound went on for a long time.

When it stopped, there was silence.

Then, from the other side of the rockfall: a soft, deliberate footfall. Then nothing.

Still there. Not digging through. Waiting. He knew Six had to come back eventually. The only way out of this tunnel was through the collapse—or back the way he'd come. The quiet one owned that direction now.

Six filed it away.

---

His heart lurched.

Not a beat. A wrong thing. A thud where there should be a pause. His chest seized, and his vision narrowed—edges going dark, then darker, the tunnel walls closing in from the sides. He blinked. The dark stayed dark. The wrong thing in his chest fluttered. Skipped. Fluttered again.

He put a hand on the wall. Waited.

The rhythm was broken. The beat he'd used for the kinetic push scattered when the ceiling came down—too much noise, too much chaos, his focus shattered with the beam. The lightning took what it always took. More, this time. He'd pushed too hard. Used too much.

His heart was trying to find its beat. He let it. Stood still. Breathed. Didn't count.

The flutter stopped. The beat steadied. Not right. But steady.

He moved.

The collapse was a dead end. The quiet one was on the other side. The herbalist was somewhere beyond the market hollow, and the only way there was a maintenance ladder three tunnels back that the quiet one didn't know about.

Six climbed. His hands were cold. The inside cold. The kind that meant he'd pushed too hard and would pay for it later. He ignored it. Climbing was slow. His chest still felt wrong. But he climbed, and the ladder took him to an upper passage, and he kept moving.

---

The herbalist's den smelled like rot and roots.

It was a small room carved into the limestone behind a rusted iron door that looked sealed shut but swung open if you knew where to push. The shelves were old crates stacked sideways. The jars were mismatched glass, some with labels, most without. Bundles of dried herbs hung from the ceiling like dead things.

The herbalist was a woman named Yanna. She was old when Six was young. She was older now. Her hands were gnarled, her eyes were clouded, but her hands were steady when she reached for a jar.

"Tall Mute," she said. Not a question.

Six didn't answer. He didn't need to. She knew what he looked like. She knew what he was. She'd known him since he was a kid running errands for Mara, and she owed him a favor from a winter he didn't think about. Didn't matter. The favor was still good.

"Medicine," he said. His voice was hoarse. Rough from disuse. "Infection. Bolt wound. Fever."

Yanna looked at him for a long moment. Her clouded eyes didn't blink. Then she turned to her shelves and started pulling jars. She didn't ask who the medicine was for. Didn't ask about the bolt wound. Didn't ask about the dust in his hair and the wrong-color pallor of his face and the way he was holding himself like his chest might come apart.

She just filled a cloth bag with jars and packets and a small bottle of something dark.

"Poultice on the wound," she said. "Tea for the fever. Tincture for the pain. Two drops, no more. It's strong."

Six took the bag. Nodded once.

"The favor's paid," she said.

He nodded again. Left.

---

The return was slower. The quiet one was still out there—somewhere between the collapse and the safehouse, waiting. Six took the long way. The maintenance ladders. The overflow ducts. The passages no one used because they were half-flooded and smelled like the inside of a grave. It added an hour. Maybe two. He didn't count.

His heart had settled. Mostly. The flutter came back when he climbed too fast, when he dropped too hard, when he pushed off a wall and his chest reminded him that he hadn't recovered. It would recover. It always recovered.

The safehouse door was closed. He'd left it open. He always left it open—easier to hear what was coming, easier to run. The door was closed now.

He stopped.

Listened.

Breathing inside. One person. Shallow. Fast. Awake.

He pushed the door open.

Dreya was sitting up. The rags were pooled around her. Her face was still flushed, her eyes still too bright, but she was conscious. Alert. Watching him.

She was holding his sword.

It was across her lap—not raised, not aimed, not ready to swing. Just resting there. Her hands were wrapped around the hilt. Her knuckles were white.

Six looked at the sword.

Dreya looked at him.

She held it out.

He took it.

No words. No explanation. No apology.

He leaned the sword against the wall. Knelt beside the stove. Started pulling jars from the bag.

"The herbalist," Dreya said. Her voice was cracked. Dry. "I didn't know there were herbalists in the Warrens. It makes sense, of course. Underground communities require underground medical infrastructure. I read a paper once about informal healing networks in Midden's lower districts. The author argued that—"

She stopped.

Six was watching her.

She looked at her hands. At the sword against the wall. At him.

"Is it bad?" she asked. "The medicine, I mean. Does it taste bad? Some of these tinctures are remarkably unpleasant. I had a roommate at the Academy who took valerian for sleep and the whole room smelled like—"

She was talking about medicine. Not the sword. Not the closed door. Not the three hours alone.

Six didn't answer. He poured water from his canteen into a tin cup. Added the herbs for the fever tea. Set it on the stove to steep.

Dreya watched his hands. The way he measured the tincture—two drops, no more. The way he crushed the poultice herbs into a paste. The way his fingers were still cold, still a little slow, still not quite right. She didn't ask about that either.

"Thank you," she said.

It was the quietest thing she'd ever said.

Six glanced at her. Nodded once. Turned back to the medicine.

The fire crackled. The tea steeped. Neither of them spoke.
