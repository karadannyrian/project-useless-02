**Chapter 5: Into the Dark**
---

The tunnel entrance is a wound in the canal wall.

Six finds it by feel—a gap in the stone where the mortar crumbled before he was born, where the iron support beams rusted through and left a darkness deeper than the night above. The smell is wet stone and old sewage and something else. Something familiar.

He breathes it in. Doesn't name it.

Dreya hesitates at the entrance. Her good leg holds her weight. The bad leg drags. The fever has flushed her cheeks and made her eyes too bright. She stares at the dark. Doesn't step forward. Her mouth opens. Words come out. Fast.

"The Warrens," she says. "Originally constructed during Midden's Third Expansion as worker housing for the canal dredging crews. The geological substrate is primarily limestone, which is highly susceptible to water erosion—hence the constant damp. Most of the original structures collapsed within fifty years of completion, which speaks to either criminal negligence in the architectural planning or a deliberate acceptance of temporary housing conditions for the labor class. Given what I've read about the Accordance's labor policies during that period, I'd lean toward deliberate—"

She's not looking at the tunnel. She's looking at her hands. At the canal wall. At anything but the dark.

Six steps into the dark.

She follows.

---

His feet knew the way. Hadn't forgotten. The body doesn't forget stairs. Left at the junction where the ceiling sags. Right where the old sewer grate is bent inward—someone's been through since, but not recently. Straight where the floor slopes down to the canal overflow. The water is ankle-deep here, cold and black. His boots splash. Dreya's boots splash behind him.

She's still talking.

"—the structural weaknesses of underground dwellings are well-documented. Subsidence, primarily, but also flooding, vermin infestation, and inadequate ventilation leading to respiratory illness. The mortality rate in the Warrens during the Fourth Winter was thirty-seven percent, which is appalling, but when you factor in the population density and the lack of medical infrastructure—"

Her voice is getting thinner.

Six listens. Not to the words. To the voice. To the spaces between the words. The pauses are getting longer. The sentences are trailing off before they finish.

He doesn't look back. He doesn't need to. Her footfalls tell him everything. Slower now. Heavier on the bad leg.

"—and the ventilation shafts were retrofitted after the Fifth Plague, but the iron grates they installed rusted through within a decade, which is ironic, really, because the whole point of using iron was—"

She stops.

Not talking. Walking.

Six turns. She's leaning against the tunnel wall, one hand pressed to the stone, the other clutching her thigh. The bandage is dark with seepage. Her face is the color of old wax.

"I'm fine," she says. "I just needed to—the wall was—it's very dark in here. Has anyone ever mentioned that? That it's dark? Because it is. Remarkably dark. The absence of light is nearly complete. I've been in dark rooms before—the Academy has several for sensory deprivation experiments—but this is different. This is—"

She slides.

Six catches her before she hits the water. His arm hooks under hers, and he pulls her upright, and her skin is hot. Burning. The fever has climbed past fever into something worse. Infection. Blood poisoning, maybe. The kind of sick that kills people who don't see a medic.

She's shivering. Her teeth are chattering. But her eyes are still open. Still looking at him.

"The architecture," she says. "I was talking about the architecture. The limestone. The subsidence. Where was I?"

He doesn't answer. He shifts her weight onto his shoulder and keeps moving.

She keeps talking.

---

Above them, something heavy moves.

Six stops.

Not close. Not in the tunnel. Above. On the surface. The sound comes through the stone like a heartbeat felt through a wall—muffled, rhythmic, enormous. Footsteps. Deliberate. Pacing.

The man in iron. Sealing exits. Six can hear it in the pattern—the footsteps stop, pause for the space of a breath, then resume. He's blocking something. A door, a grate, a ventilation shaft. Each pause is an exit closing.

Dreya doesn't notice. She's talking about foundation stresses and load-bearing walls and the tensile strength of limestone versus granite, and her voice is so quiet now that he can barely hear it over the drip of water.

He counts the pauses.

Three exits sealed. Maybe four. There are other ways out—ways the man in iron won't find, ways that aren't on any map—but they're deep. Deeper than Dreya can walk in her current state.

He keeps moving.

---

Another sound. Closer.

Not above. Behind. Somewhere in the tunnels, several passages back, soft-soled shoes on wet stone. Then nothing. Then the shoes again, closer this time.

The quiet one. Inside. Hunting. He doesn't move like the man in iron—no weight, no rhythm, no pattern. He moves like a held breath. Like a blade sliding out of a sheath. He wants them to know he's here. That's the point.

Six doesn't speed up. Doesn't look back. The tunnel ahead branches twice before it reaches the safehouse, and the quiet one won't know which branch unless Six shows him. Silence is the answer. Silence and direction.

Dreya is still talking.

"—the limestone dissolves in acidic water, you know. That's why the canals are so alkaline—they treat the water to prevent erosion, but the treatment only reaches the main channels. Down here, the water is pure runoff. The stalactites in the lower caverns are technically not stalactites at all but a form of—"

He turns left. The soft-soled footsteps go right.

Good.

---

The safehouse is exactly where he left it.

Eleven years ago. Maybe twelve. He was fourteen when he found it—a room carved into the limestone behind a collapsed sewer grate, invisible from the main passage, too small for anyone to bother claiming. He slept here the winter after Mara died. He hasn't been back since he was seventeen. He didn't expect to come back at all.

The door is a slab of old oak, warped by damp but still solid. The hinges are rusted. They scream when he pushes.

Inside: a single room. Stone floor. Stone walls. A stove in the corner, its pipe venting through a crack in the ceiling. A pile of rags in the corner that used to be a bed. A lantern with no oil. A shelf with nothing on it.

Dreya is barely standing. Her talking has stopped. Not because she's chosen to be quiet—because she can't. Her lips are moving, but no sound comes out. Her eyes are glassy. Her skin is hot enough that he can feel the heat through his coat.

He lowers her onto the rags. She doesn't protest. Doesn't lecture. Just curls onto her side and closes her eyes.

The stove.

There's wood stacked beside it. Old. Dry. He doesn't remember leaving it. Doesn't matter. He pulls a handful of kindling, arranges it in the stove, strikes his flint. It takes three tries. Four. The kindling catches. The wood catches. The fire is small and hungry, but it's fire.

Heat fills the room.

Six sits against the wall. Between the door and Dreya. His sword is across his knees. His eyes are on the door.

Behind him, Dreya breathes.

---

She's quiet.

For the first time since the Archive basement—since he watched her try to cast through a crossbow wound, since she introduced herself while bleeding on a bridge, since she complained about fish oil and analyzed neurotoxins and quoted anatomy texts while he redid her bandage—she's quiet.

He looks at her.

She's curled on the rags, one hand tucked under her cheek, the other still resting on her wounded thigh. The fever flush has spread down her neck. Her breathing is shallow and fast, but steady. The firelight flickers across her face. She doesn't stir.

She's useful when she's conscious. That's what he tells himself. She can cast. She can talk her way past things he can't. She can explain what they're up against.

She's also just a person. A person who's quiet right now, and he doesn't like it.

He looks away. Listens to the tunnels. Water dripping. Wood burning. The quiet one, somewhere far off, still searching. The man in iron, somewhere above, still sealing.

She's useful when she's conscious.

He knows that's not entirely true.

The fire burns. Dreya breathes. Six watches the door.

She's still breathing. That's enough. For now.
