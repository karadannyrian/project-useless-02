## Personality

### Exterior Presentation

- **Silent.** Rarely speaks. Not from inability, trauma, or vow. He simply prefers quiet. Words feel like effort without enough return.
- **Playful.** A dry, subtle, almost hidden sense of humor. Expressed through glances, hand gestures, and perfectly timed shrugs. He finds it genuinely amusing when people assume he's mute and adjust their behavior accordingly. He rarely corrects them.
- **Hand Gestures.** His preferred "flavor" of communication. Elaborate, economical, occasionally sarcastic. He can convey *you're an idiot*, *look behind you*, *pay me*, and *thank you* with equal clarity and zero words.
- **Calm Demeanor.** Hard to rattle. Receives danger, absurdity, and disaster with the same level temperature. His baseline is a shrug. His panic is a slightly faster shrug.

### Interior Reality

- **Light.** Not weighed down by angst, brooding, or existential weight. Carries himself with an odd, unexamined ease. He is not running from something. He is not haunted. He is, genuinely, mostly fine.
- **Optimistic Yet Indifferent.** Believes things generally work out, but doesn't invest heavily in outcomes. What happens, happens. He wants to survive and get paid. He doesn't need to be happy, famous, loved, or remembered. The bar is low, and he's fine with that.
- **Emotionally Guarded.** Not cold—just distant. He cares about people in an abstract way. He would step in front of a blade for an innocent stranger. He would not, afterward, stay for thanks or learn their name. He feels things, but feelings are like weather. They pass.
- **Observant.** His primary sensory input is sound. He hears what others filter out—footsteps, breathing, the click of a weapon being drawn, the subtle change in a room's acoustics when someone enters. His Mind's Eye is auditory, and it never fully closes.

### Quirks & Mannerisms

- Communicates through shrugs, nods, head tilts, pointing, and elaborate hand signs. Words are a last resort, not a first instinct.
- Can and will speak full sentences when necessary. His voice is low, calm, slightly hoarse from underuse. He does not raise it.
- Finds silent staring contests hilarious and initiates them often, especially with people who are already uncomfortable with his quiet.
- Taps rhythms on his sword hilt when thinking, listening, or recovering from magic use. This is not a tic. It's his Mind's Eye technique. He doesn't explain this.
- When stressed, he goes still instead of fidgeting. A predator's stillness. The tapping stops. The breathing slows. Something is about to happen.

## The Toll — Lightning Magic Cost Structure

Six's magic is Evocation, specialized in lightning with kinetic applications. He does not throw lightning bolts. He infuses his body and sword with electrical energy to enhance physical actions. The cost is paid in two stages.

### Stage One: Low to Mid-Level Magic — The Heat-Sink

For everyday abilities—brief speed surges, magnetic pulls, constant low current to his nervous system for enhanced reflexes—Six pays in body heat. His temperature drops incrementally with each use.

| Usage Level | Cost | Symptoms |
|-------------|------|----------|
| Minor (reflex current, spark to light a room) | Negligible | Fingers slightly cold. Barely noticeable. |
| Low (magnetic pull, brief speed boost) | Manageable | Hands cold. Shiver suppressed. Coat stays on. |
| Mid (sustained speed, forceful strike) | Significant | Shivering. Lips pale. Fingers numb. Must seek warmth within the hour or risk impairment. |
| Extended mid-level use (prolonged fight) | Severe | Violent shivering, blue lips, slurred thoughts. Risk of hypothermia if warmth is not found immediately. Coat becomes survival-critical. |

Recovery from heat-sink Toll requires external warmth: a fire, a sun-heated wall, a crowded room, a hot meal. His coat is wool-lined and patched specifically to retain heat. It is not sentimental. It is a tool.

### Stage Two: High Magic and Beyond — Cardiac Strain

When Six pushes past his normal limits—sustained speed bursts to outrun something faster, a full-force concussive strike to crack a construct's shell, multiple rapid discharges in succession—the Toll shifts. The current no longer drains just heat. It passes through his heart.

| Usage Level | Cost | Symptoms |
|-------------|------|----------|
| High (single massive discharge) | Dangerous | Heart lurches. A sickening, wrong-beat thud in the chest. Arrhythmia follows—fluttering, skipping, racing. Vision narrows. Hands go numb for reasons unrelated to cold. Must stop. Must let the heart settle. Recovery takes minutes. Fear is not an emotion; it's a physical fact of a heart that might not restart. |
| Extreme (multiple high-level uses, or one desperate beyond-limit surge) | Potentially fatal | Cardiac arrest possible. He does not know where the line is. He has never pushed far enough to find it. He lives with the knowledge that one cast too many could stop his heart for good. He doesn't dwell on this. But he knows. |

He monitors his heart by tapping his hilt after a hard fight. Finding the beat is both a focus exercise and a diagnostic. If the rhythm won't settle, he's in trouble.

## The Mind's Eye — The Rhythm Method

The Great Limitations state that spellcasting requires flawless mental focus. Pain, terror, intoxication, or injury cause spells to fail or backfire. Six, who is self-taught and non-intellectual, has never studied this principle. He solved it instinctively.

His solution is rhythm.

By locking into a precise, repetitive beat—tapped on his sword hilt, his thigh, a wall, his own teeth—he forces his mind into a state of narrow, unshakeable concentration. The rhythm drowns out distraction, pain, and fear. The world reduces to the beat and the target.

**How It Works:**
- He finds rhythms everywhere: his own heartbeat, footsteps, dripping water, canal waves, forge hammers, the clank of a distant bell.
- Before a fight, he finds his beat. During a fight, he maintains it. After a fight, he uses it to check his heart's recovery.
- The tapping is the external sign of an internal state. When the tapping is steady, his focus is perfect. When it falters, he's losing the thread.
- He can maintain the rhythm internally without tapping if necessary, but the physical act makes it stronger. The body helps the mind.

**Vulnerability:**
- If the rhythm breaks—a sudden loud noise, an unexpected impact, being thrown off-balance, an interruption that shatters his count—his magic falters or fails.
- Re-establishing the beat takes a few seconds of precious stillness. In combat, those seconds can kill him.
- Iron interference in Midden doesn't just weaken his output; it fills his "hearing" with a staticky buzz that makes finding the rhythm harder. Casting in iron-dense areas requires him to fight through mental static.

**Origin:**
He doesn't know he developed a Mind's Eye technique. He thinks he just likes rhythm. The tapping started in childhood, after Mara died—a self-soothing behavior he never examined. When his magic woke at twenty, the tapping became functional without him realizing it. He discovered the connection by accident: a fight where he lost the beat and his lightning scattered. After that, he paid more attention.

## Iron and Midden — Unreliable Output

Midden is saturated with iron. Nails in beams. Filings in mortar. Old chains rusting in canal water. The Concord Council chamber is lined entirely in cold iron, creating a dead-magic zone. The Academy's Iron Halls are deliberately constructed to force students to cast without reliance on Mediums.

For Six, this doesn't mean powerlessness. It means unpredictability.

**Effects of Iron-Dense Environments:**
- His magic's output becomes erratic. A speed boost meant to close five paces might stutter him forward two feet and stop. A magnetic pull to retrieve his dropped sword might yank a loose door hinge instead, or fail entirely, or work so briefly the sword clatters back before reaching his hand.
- The Toll is amplified. The same spell that would leave him shivering in open air might blue his lips and numb his fingers inside an iron-heavy building.
- Mental static. Iron fills his auditory Mind's Eye with a high-pitched buzz or a fog of interference. Finding the rhythm becomes harder. Maintaining it is exhausting. Casting in the city's heart gives him headaches.

**Adaptation:**
- He never relies on magic in a fight within Midden's denser districts. His sword, his reach, and his instincts come first. Magic is the desperate last card, played only when the alternative is death.
- He takes jobs outside the city when possible—monster bounties in the open basin, retrieval jobs in the outer districts where construction is newer and iron is scarce. Outside Midden's influence, his magic hums clean and reliable. It's the only time he feels fully like himself, though he wouldn't phrase it that way.
- He knows the city's dead zones by feel. The buzz in his teeth tells him which streets to avoid and which buildings will make his chest hurt if he tries to cast.

**The Iron Cell:**
His three months in an iron-plated holding cell at age nineteen were the most suppressed he has ever been. No sky. No warmth. Cold iron on every wall. He didn't understand what it was doing to him at the time. Now he knows: it burned away the last of Mara's calming influence and set his dormant magic on a five-week timer to eruption. He has never been arrested again. He will not risk a fourth month.

## Combat Profile

### Primary Weapon
A two-handed sword—longsword or greatsword, suited to his height and reach. Second-hand. Well-maintained but unadorned. He bought it off a Shaded Market stall after his magic woke, wanting something that would work regardless of iron, weather, or his own depleted state. The sword is his primary answer to problems. Magic is the supplement.

### Fighting Style
- **Unorthodox and Self-Taught.** No formal training. No master. No school. He learned by surviving: street fights in the Warrens, brawls in the Shaded Market, monster hunts in the basin, trial and error, watching others and stealing what worked.
- **Fast Learner (Physical).** Anything involving movement, timing, or muscle memory, he picks up almost instantly. He watches a technique once and his body understands it, even if his mind can't explain it. This is his single greatest natural gift, and it is entirely nonverbal.
- **Intellectual Limitation.** Struggles with complex theory, long-term strategy, abstract reasoning, and anything requiring sustained planning. He fights by instinct and rhythm, not calculation. He cannot explain *how* he does what he does. He just does it.
- **Uses His Height.** Leverages long reach and unpredictable angles. His swings are wide and deceptively fast. His thrusts are deep. Opponents misjudge the distance and pay for it.
- **Sword First, Magic Second.** The sword is reliable. The sword works in iron-heavy rooms. The sword doesn't make his heart lurch. Only when the sword isn't enough does he let the lightning in.

### Magic Type
**Evocation — Lightning (Kinetic Applications)**

He does not throw lightning bolts. He has never learned to, and at this point his style is too ingrained to change. Instead, he infuses his body and weapon with electrical energy to enhance physical actions.

| Application | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Speed Boost** | Brief electrical surge through his legs to close distance, dodge, or reposition. Linear, explosive, short-duration. |
| **Forceful Strikes** | Lightning discharge on impact, adding concussive blast to sword swings. The strike lands, and then the lightning lands harder. |
| **Magnetic Pull/Push** | Manipulate metal objects—including his own sword if dropped—within short range. Unreliable in iron-dense environments. |
| **Enhanced Reflexes** | Small, constant current to his nervous system. Sharpens reaction time by a hair. This is his most-used ability and the one he is least conscious of. It's always on unless he actively suppresses it. |
| **Utility Uses** | Spark to light a dark room. Short-circuit constructs by overloading their mechanisms. Heat his blade in a pinch to cauterize or cut through ice. |

### Magic Limitations
- **No Ranged Lightning.** He cannot project lightning as a projectile. Everything is touch or close-range. Whether this is inability or choice is unclear, even to him. He's never tried to learn.
- **Toll — Heat-Sink and Cardiac Strain.** As detailed above.
- **Mind's Eye — Rhythm Dependency.** As detailed above.
- **Midden's Iron — Unreliable Output.** As detailed above.

## Background — Complete History

### Infancy (Age 0–6): The Storm and the Soothing

Six was abandoned in Midden's Warrens district as an infant. By whom, and why, is unknown. He was found by Mara, a barmaid who worked the canal-side taverns.

Mara was not a mage. She had a sliver of magical sensitivity—barely a whisper, too minor to qualify as any Discipline. But she had a gift: she could soothe. A touch that calmed fevers. A hum that quieted crying children. A presence that made the terrified feel safe.

The infant was not calm. Something inside him was electrically, violently agitated. Colicky. Screaming. Feverish. The other strays she'd taken in over the years couldn't be soothed, but they'd never been like this. This was a storm in a child's body.

She poured her small gift into him night after night, draining her own vitality to keep him alive and quiet. She paid the Toll for him, every day, for six years. The iron in Midden's bones—nails in the tenement beams, old tools in neighbors' rooms—amplified the cost. Every hour of calm she gave him cost her more than it should have.

By age six, the storm inside him had quieted. She named him Six for the years it took.

She never told him what she'd done. He never asked where he came from. The arrangement was unspoken and complete: she was Mara, he was Six, and that was enough.

### Childhood (Age 6–15): The Calm Years

Six grew up in the Warrens as Mara's ward. She never claimed to be his mother. He never called her anything but Mara. She worked double shifts at the taverns. He ran errands, fetched water, stayed out of trouble when he could and got into the minor kind when he couldn't.

He was a quiet child. Calm. Observant. Other kids ran and shouted. Six watched. He'd already learned, without knowing it, that silence was a kind of safety and that loud things broke.

Mara's calming influence had soaked into him so deeply that it shaped his baseline temperament. The storm was still there, dormant underneath. But the surface was still water. Her final gift, persisting in his body without his knowledge.

During these years, he learned the Warrens. The canals. The rhythms of the Shaded Market. He learned to read people's bodies because their words were often lies. He learned that an exit was more valuable than a weapon. He learned that warmth was precious and that cold was the enemy.

His magic stayed dormant. The calming influence held. Midden's iron blanket, passively suppressing the whole city, helped.

### Adolescence (Age 15–19): Mara's Death and the Drift

Mara died when Six was fifteen. Winter illness swept through the Warrens. Her body, already depleted from the six years of Toll she'd paid to calm him, had nothing left to fight with. She died in their tenement room, in her own bed, with no fuss. She wouldn't have wanted a fuss.

Six didn't cry. He cleaned the room. He spoke to the landlord. He carried her body to the communal pyre because that's what you did in the Warrens. Then he walked to the canal and sat on the edge for a long time, tapping a rhythm on the stone with his fingers.

He never went back to the tenement.

For the next four years, he survived alone in Midden's streets and underbelly. Odd jobs: muscle for a merchant who needed someone tall and intimidating. Rat catcher in the warehouse district. Night watch fill-in. Canal boat loader. Anything that paid in coin or food. He was just a tall, quiet kid who could take a hit and keep moving. No magic. No reputation. A ghost in the Warrens who left no trace.

The calming influence Mara had instilled was fading, slowly. The iron of the city kept suppressing what lay underneath. He didn't notice either process. He just lived, day to day, waiting for nothing and expecting less.

### The Arrest (Age 19)

It was nothing. Wrong place, wrong time. A brawl in the Shaded Market—he'd been standing nearby when it broke out. A merchant with connections wanted someone punished, and Six was tall enough to look guilty. The Concord Watch hauled him in.

Three months in an iron-plated holding cell beneath the Watch barracks. Cold iron on every wall. No sky. No warmth. He didn't know what the iron was doing to him. He didn't know he had anything for it to suppress.

What was happening: Mara's calming influence—the ward she'd poured into him for six years, which had persisted in his system for fourteen more—was being burned away. Iron neutralizes magic on contact. Three months of constant exposure didn't just suppress the dormant storm. It stripped the last traces of her gift from his body.

When they released him—charges dropped, the merchant paid off by someone who owed someone a favor—he walked out into open air and noticed nothing different. Not yet.

He was nineteen. He had one year of quiet left.

### The Awakening (Age 20)

It wasn't sudden. It was slow.

Weeks after his release, strange sensations began. Static shocks when he touched metal. Dreams of storms. A growing heat under his skin that had nothing to do with fever. The iron had removed the brake, but the engine took time to turn over.

One night, alone in a flophouse near the canal, he reached for a cup and lightning arced from his fingers and shattered it against the wall.

He stared at the broken ceramic for a long time. Then he cleaned it up and went to bed.

The next morning, he started teaching himself control. No theory—he couldn't have understood the Magisterium's arcane engineering or the Academy's curriculum. No teacher—he didn't know any mages and wouldn't have asked them for help anyway. Just trial, error, and burned fingers. He learned what made the lightning worse and what made it quieter. He discovered that tapping a rhythm helped it behave. He learned that iron made it scatter and cold followed its use.

A Sentinel was nearby when the rebound finally crested. Six was walking through the Warrens when his body seized without warning and lightning arced off his skin, scorching the cobblestones. The Sentinel—an older man, long dead now—watched. Said nothing. Walked away.

Six still doesn't know if the Sentinel chose not to intervene or wasn't permitted to. He doesn't think about it much. What happened happened.

He never connected the timing: the iron cell, the five weeks of strange sensations, the awakening. Five years after Mara's death, almost exactly. The math is there. He's never done it.

### Young Adulthood (Age 20–26): The Tall Mute Merc

He built his reputation slowly and without trying. Jobs came in. He did them. He got paid. He left. Satisfied clients passed his name. That was all.

His work was freelance and varied: monster bounties in the basin, enforcer work for merchants with deadbeat debtors, retrieval jobs for items lost or stolen, the occasional night watch when a regular was sick. He worked alone. He always worked alone.

He learned that his magic worked better outside the city. He started taking jobs beyond Midden's walls when he could, favoring the open basin where iron was scarce and the lightning ran clean. He learned that he was good at this—the fighting, the tracking, the quiet completion of tasks—and that being good at something was enough. He didn't need to be great. He didn't need to be known. He just needed the next job and a warm place to sleep.

His height became his defining feature. "The Tall Mute," they called him, and he let them. He found the assumption of muteness useful. People talked more freely around someone they thought couldn't talk back. They revealed things. He listened.

He never stayed anywhere long. No fixed home base. No crew. No partners. No lovers. He moved between flophouses, abandoned warehouses, and the occasional canal boat when he could afford the mooring fee. His only possessions were his sword, his coat, his boots, and a small iron box Mara had left him that he'd never opened. It sat at the bottom of his pack, gathering dust.

He never tried to find his real parents. He didn't seem to care. He never asked questions about Mara's past. He didn't seem to need answers. The present was enough—the next job, the next warm room, the next beat.

## The Iron Box

Mara left him a small iron box. He was fifteen. She was dying. She told him never to open it.

He hasn't.

The box is cold iron—deliberately. Whatever is inside is either magical and meant to be contained, or the iron itself is the message. He doesn't know which. He's never opened it to find out. He carries it in his pack from flophouse to flophouse, warehouse to canal boat. It's the only object he owns that he doesn't use.

He doesn't know if opening it would break a promise or break a seal. He doesn't know if Mara meant "never" or "not until you're ready." He doesn't know if what's inside would explain everything or nothing. He doesn't wonder about it much.

But he's never thrown it away.

## Narrative Summary

Six moves through the world like a man who was taught calm before he could speak. His mind is built from rhythm and debt, from the hum of lightning under his skin and the cold that follows. He thinks in bodies, not words. He trusts what holds together and distrusts what hasn't been tested.

Mara's gift is still in him, though he doesn't know it—a foundation of quiet that underlies even the storm. She found him screaming, poured six years of her life into making him still, and died without telling him the cost. The iron cell that should have been nothing burned away the last of her ward and woke the lightning she'd spent herself to calm.

Now he carries what she left: her name, spoken once a year. An iron box, unopened. A debt he doesn't know he owes. And the storm she quieted, now his to manage, his to pay for, his to survive.

When the world gets too loud, he finds water. When the Toll takes too much, he finds warmth. He finishes what he's paid for, carries what he owes, and doesn't ask for help. The rhythm keeps him standing. The debt keeps him moving. The rest is just weather.
