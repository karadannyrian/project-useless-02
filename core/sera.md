# Sera Vaunt — Complete Character Profile

---

## Core Identity

**Full Name:** Sera Vaunt (almost certainly not her real name)
**Alias:** The Broker, the Pale Lady, "that Dominion bitch" (in certain Midden circles)
**Age:** 28
**Gender:** Female
**Build:** Lean, angular, impeccably controlled
**Heritage:** Dominion of the Everspring (high-ranking operative, specific branch unknown)
**Occupation:** Acquisitions Specialist, Shaded Market Liaison, officially attached to the Dominion Consulate in Midden
**Role in Story:** Antagonist and love interest to Reks
**Defining Physical Traits:** Ash-pale skin (Aethelian ancestry), dark hair falling just above the hip, eyes the color of tarnished silver—a rare pigment mutation common in high-altitude Dominion bloodlines

---

## Physical Description

### The Woman

Sera Vaunt is beautiful the way a drawn blade is beautiful—all function, no ornament, and the promise of harm perfectly implicit. She's tall for a Dominion woman, five-foot-ten, with the long-boned leanness of someone whose ancestry adapted to thin mountain air and whose present is adapted to never eating more than necessary. There is no spare flesh on her. Her cheekbones are architecture, her jawline a verdict.

She moves like someone who has never had to run from anything in her life. Not the lazy stroll of the powerful, but the precise economy of someone who knows exactly how many steps it takes to cross any room and wastes none of them. When she sits, she doesn't settle—she occupies. The chair becomes hers. The table becomes her desk. The room adjusts its center of gravity.

Her age is difficult to place. Twenty-eight, but she could pass for thirty-five in the right light, or twenty-two from the right angle. The ambiguity is intentional. She cultivates it the way Reks cultivates his invisibility—as armor, as misdirection, as the first layer of a defense no one notices until they're already inside it.

### The Look

Her skin is the first thing anyone registers. Ash-pale, almost grey in low light, the color of old snow or parchment before the ink touches it. It's an Aethelian trait, common among the old families of the Dominion's upper reaches, where the sun is thin and the bloodlines are managed with agricultural precision. She doesn't rouge her cheeks or paint her lips. The pallor is a statement: *I don't need to look healthy. I'm not here to be reassuring.*

Her hair is black, and it's the only soft thing about her. She wears it long—falling just above her hips in a straight, heavy curtain that she keeps pulled back from her face with a simple silver clasp at the nape of her neck. When she moves, it sways. When she leans forward over a table, it falls over one shoulder in a dark rope that she tucks back with an impatient flick of her fingers. The length is impractical for her profession. She knows this. She keeps it anyway, and if anyone asks why, she tells them it's a reminder that she's not a soldier—that she's something else, something that doesn't need to conform to operational efficiency. The truth is more complicated. The truth is that her sister Maris had hair exactly this long, and cutting it would feel like cutting a tether she refuses to acknowledge exists.

Her eyes are silver. Not grey, not pale blue—actual silver, the color of tarnish on old mirror glass, of mercury pooled in a palm. It's a Dominion genetic marker, a mutation that runs in certain high-altitude bloodlines and is considered auspicious. They catch light strangely, seeming to glow in dim conditions. They also give away nothing—except, sometimes, when she's looking at Reks and she forgets to control her expression. In those moments, there's something beneath the silver. Something assessing. Something hungry. Something she crushes before it can name itself.

### Body as Record

Unlike Reks, whose body is a palimpsest of violence survived, Sera's body is a document of violence *avoided*. Her hands are uncalloused. Her knuckles are smooth. There are no scars visible on her face, her throat, her forearms—anywhere that clothing doesn't cover. This is not because she hasn't been hurt. It's because she's had access to the best healers in the Dominion, the kind who can erase tissue memory, who can make a knife wound look like a birthmark or make a birthmark disappear entirely.

If you look very closely, in the right light, you can see a faint silver tracery at her left temple—almost invisible, like the scars left by a lightning strike. It's a neural intervention scar, the kind left by certain Dominion interrogation techniques that are applied to the *questioner* rather than the subject. She was trained to resist mental intrusion. The scar is a reminder that the training worked.

Her hands are her tell, if you know how to read them. She keeps them very still—folded in her lap, resting flat on a tabletop, aligned precisely with her thighs when she stands. But sometimes, when she's thinking hard or when a conversation shifts into dangerous territory, her right thumb will trace a small circle against her left palm, over and over, the same motion. It's the only repetitive gesture she allows herself. Reks notices it before she realizes he's watching. He doesn't mention it. He files it away—a stress-fracture, a crack in the smooth surface. Something to tap with a marline spike if he ever needs to.

### Dress

Sera dresses like she's attending a funeral for someone she's glad is dead. Severe, elegant, and entirely in grey. Her coat is a long affair of Dominion wool, charcoal-colored, cut to fall in clean lines from shoulder to ankle. Beneath it, a high-collared tunic of lighter grey, fastened with small silver buttons that are definitely not decorative—they're shaped like Dominion seals, and they probably contain trace cold iron. Her trousers are tailored, her boots are polished to a mirror shine, and none of it looks new. None of it looks old. It all looks *maintained*, like equipment that is cleaned and inspected daily.

She wears three visible items of jewelry. A signet ring on her right index finger, silver, with a crest that isn't hers—it's too worn to read, deliberately so, a misdirection. A thin silver chain at her throat, disappearing into her collar. And the pendant.

The pendant is whalebone. Old whalebone, yellowed and smooth with age, carved into a shape that's too worn to identify—maybe a fish, maybe a bird, maybe just a curl of abstract line. It hangs from a simple leather cord, and it doesn't match the rest of her. Everything else about Sera is Dominion precision, Dominion severity, Dominion control. The pendant is Frostmark work. The pendant is old enough to have come from a Corway hand. The pendant whispers his grandmother's name—not aloud, not in words, but in the way it pulls at Reks' Rune-Sight, the way the threads around it glow with a familiar signature.

She knows exactly what it does. She wears it where he can see it. That's the whole point. It's leverage. It's a message. It's also—though she will never admit this—the only thing she owns that feels like it belonged to someone who was loved.

## The Cold Iron Bodyguard

She is never alone. Her shadow is a man called **Kael**—no surname, or none that she uses. He's big, broad-shouldered, with the thickened neck and scarred knuckles of a professional brawler, but he moves with the quiet of someone who's been trained out of making noise. His skin is darker than hers, weathered, and his hair is shaved to stubble. His eyes are brown and utterly empty.

Kael wears cold iron openly. Manacles on his belt. A short sword at his hip with an iron hilt wrapped in leather. Rings on both hands that are definitely not decorative. He's a walking null-field, and his proximity makes Reks' Rune-Sight flicker and fade. Sera keeps him close enough to be effective, far enough to be polite, and always between her and the nearest exit.

He doesn't speak. Not to Reks. Not to anyone in Reks' presence. Whether he can't or won't is unclear. When Sera gives him an order, it's a flick of her fingers or a single word, and he responds immediately, which means either they've worked together so long that speech is unnecessary or he's conditioned to a degree that makes Reks' stomach turn. Reks isn't sure which possibility disturbs him more.

What disturbs him most, though, is that Kael watches the two of them bicker with an expression that might—on a less empty face—be called amusement. Like he knows something. Like he's waiting for something. Like he's seen Sera argue with exactly one other person in all the years he's served her, and that person's name was Maris.

## Occupation and Role

### Official Cover

Sera Vaunt is listed on the Dominion Consulate's rolls as an Acquisitions Specialist, which is a polite way of saying she buys things that aren't for sale. Antiquities, artifacts, magical curiosities that fall outside the Aethelian Magisterium's strict cataloging laws. She operates out of the Shaded Market in Midden, where she has a reputation for paying well, asking few questions, and never, ever failing to deliver on a contract.

She's been in Midden for three years. Before that, she was in Halvask. Before that, somewhere in the Dominion's interior—records get vague, and the kind of people who might know don't talk about it.

### Actual Role

Sera is a field operative for a Dominion intelligence branch that doesn't officially exist. Her specialty is the acquisition and neutralization of proscribed magical bloodlines. She's a genealogist with a sword, a historian who ends histories. The Dominion has been quietly collecting—or extinguishing—the remnants of old magical houses for decades, and the Corway line has been on their list since before Reks was born.

She's not a fanatic. She doesn't hate magic-users. She views them the way a surgeon views a tumor: something to be excised cleanly, without malice, for the health of the body politic. The Dominion's position is that uncontrolled magical bloodlines are a threat to stability—not because magic is evil, but because inheritance is unpredictable. You can't regulate what you can't predict. You can't govern what you can't regulate. The Corways, with their Rune-Sight and their Deep Chorus heresy, are a textbook case of an unregulated magical threat.

She's been hunting the last Corway for two years. She found him eighteen months ago, in Midden, calling himself Reks and working a whaler that had an uncanny survival record. She could have taken him then. She didn't. She's been waiting, watching, gathering information—because Reks isn't the only piece of the Corway puzzle. There's something in the Maze. Something his grandmother hid there, before the purge. Something that requires a living Corway to access.

The pendant was his grandmother's. Sera found it in the ashes of the village, or bought it from the men who did, or retrieved it from a Dominion archive—she hasn't told Reks which, and she won't. She wears it because it's leverage. Because it's a message. Because it's the only thing in the world that can make Reks Corway walk into the Maze of his own free will.

These are the professional reasons. They are all true. They are not the only reasons.

She's been watching him for eighteen months. She knows the way he moves through a crowd. She knows the way he checks exits. She knows the way he touches his amulet when he thinks no one is looking. She knows he's clever, and infuriating, and brave in a way that's going to get him killed, and funny in a way that catches her off guard. She knows that when he argues with her—really argues, not the guarded deflection he gives everyone else—his eyes get brighter and his hands start moving and he leans forward like he's forgotten to be afraid of her.

She's not supposed to find that compelling. She does.

She's not supposed to have a favorite way he says her name—*Vaunt*, like it's a curse, like it's a challenge. She does.

She's not supposed to wonder what would happen if she took off the pendant and told him the truth. She wonders anyway.

## Backstory

### The Aethelian Origin

Sera was born in the Dominion's interior, in a city built into the cliffside of a mountain whose name she hasn't spoken aloud since she left. Her family was old Aethelian stock—scholars, administrators, mid-level functionaries in the Magisterium's vast bureaucracy. Not powerful. Not political. Just comfortable, respected, and utterly committed to the Dominion's founding principle: that knowledge is order, and order is survival.

She was the youngest of three children. Her older brother Talan was a cartographer. Her older sister Maris was a rune-theorist. Sera was the clever one, the one who read everything, the one who could win any argument, the one who made her parents proud and her siblings nervous. She tested into the Magisterium's preparatory academy at twelve. She was recruited by the intelligence branch at seventeen.

She doesn't think about her family anymore. They're alive, presumably—she receives quarterly reports that confirm their continued existence and continued ignorance of her actual work. She hasn't spoken to them in six years. This isn't cruelty. It's protection.

### The Sister

But Maris. Maris is different. Maris was the one who believed that magic could be studied, understood, integrated into Dominion philosophy rather than merely suppressed. Maris was the one who argued with the Magisterium, who asked too many questions, who disappeared one day and was never mentioned again. Sera was sixteen. She was already in the academy. She didn't ask what happened. She didn't want to know.

The name *Maris* is a door she does not open. When something in the present reminds her of her sister—a book with a cracked spine, a particular shade of blue, the smell of mountain sage—the FID doesn't avoid the memory. It redacts it. A blank space on the page. A cut line.

But Reks—Reks with his dead family and his forbidden names and his refusal to let the past stay in the past—Reks keeps brushing against that door. Every time he argues with her, every time he pushes back, every time he looks at her with those fjord-water eyes and says something she can't predict, the door rattles in its frame. She wants to kill him for it. She wants to open the door and see what's on the other side. She wants to do neither. She wants to do both.

### The Training

The intelligence branch trained her in interrogation, infiltration, and the specific art of hunting magical bloodlines. She learned to read genealogies like tactical maps. She learned to identify magical markers in blood samples, in eye color, in the spacing of teeth. She learned to kill mages without ever entering their nullification range.

She also learned something else, something the branch didn't intend to teach her. During her training, she was subjected to a neural intervention—a procedure designed to make her resistant to mental intrusion, to truth-reading, to the kind of psychic pressure that a desperate mage might apply during an interrogation. The procedure worked. It also opened something in her mind that she's never told anyone about.

She can feel it when magic is being used near her. Not see it, not like Reks sees his threads—feel it, a pressure behind her eyes, a taste like copper on the back of her tongue. It's not precise enough to be useful in combat. It's just enough to make her aware, always, of the presence of the thing she hunts.

When she's near Reks, his Rune-Sight presses against her awareness like a constant low hum. She's learned to find it almost... companionable. A background noise she misses when it's gone. She hates that she notices when it's gone.

### The Corway File

She was assigned the Corway file three years ago, after the previous agent—a man named Dremm—disappeared somewhere in the Frostmark interior. The file was thin. A proscribed noble house, attainted seventy years ago for Deep Chorus heresy. A hereditary gift for reading memory-threads in organic material. A purge that was supposed to be complete, but wasn't. Scattered survivors. A village that burned sixteen years ago, with one confirmed escape: a boy, eight or nine, blonde, blue eyes, name unknown.

She traced him through Frostmark port records, through a series of smugglers and whaling ships, through the Shaded Market in Midden. She found him eighteen months ago. She's been waiting ever since.

She told herself she was waiting for the right moment. She told herself the Maze expedition required more preparation. She told herself she needed to understand his capabilities before making contact.

She was lying. She's very good at lying, especially to herself.

She was waiting because once she made contact, the clock would start. Once she made contact, she'd have to decide what to do with him. Asset or liability. Acquisition or termination. Those were the only two options her world allowed. Neither one felt right. So she waited, and watched, and told herself the indecision was strategic patience.

## The Bickering Rhythm

This is the core of their dynamic. Sera and Reks argue the way other people breathe—constantly, automatically, and with a rhythm that suggests they've been doing it for years instead of weeks. The arguments are about everything and nothing: tactics, directions, the proper way to pack a ration, the temperature of tea, the definition of the word "soon."

### The Surface

On the surface, the bickering is professional friction. She wants control. He wants autonomy. She wants to follow the plan. He wants to improvise. She thinks he's reckless. He thinks she's rigid. Neither is wrong, and neither will admit the other is right.

**Sample exchange—planning a Maze descent:**

*"We enter at Third Bell," she said. "The patrol shifts at Fourth. That gives us one hour to reach the sub-level entrance."*

*"One hour if the corridors are clear," Reks said. "They won't be. The sluice-gates opened last week—half the lower Warrens are flooded. We'll need to take the long route. That's two hours, minimum."*

*"Then we leave at Second Bell."*

*"We can't leave at Second Bell. The Shaded Market's still active at Second Bell. Too many eyes."*

*"So we wait until Third and accept the delay."*

*"Or we leave at Second and go through the old cistern. No eyes, no flood."*

*"The old cistern hasn't been mapped."*

*"I've mapped it."*

*"You've mapped it."*

*"I've walked it. Twice."*

*"Without telling me."*

*"I'm telling you now."*

*Her jaw tightened. "You are the most infuriating—"*

*"Guide," he said. "The word you're looking for is guide. You hired me for this. Let me do it."*

*She wanted to say something else. He could see it in the way her thumb traced circles against her palm. But she just nodded—a short, sharp motion—and said, "Second Bell. The cistern. If we die in there, I'm filing a complaint."*

*"File it with my corpse."*

*"I intend to."*

### The Subtext

Underneath, the bickering is something else entirely. It's testing. It's flirting. It's two people who've been alone for so long they've forgotten how to say *I see you* without saying *you're wrong*.

- **When she corrects his grammar**, she's really saying: *I'm paying attention to you. I notice the details. You're worth correcting.*
- **When he ignores her orders**, he's really saying: *You can't control me. I'm not one of your assets. I'm real, and I'm here, and you have to deal with me as I am.*
- **When she gets quiet instead of arguing back**, she's really saying: *You've surprised me. I don't like being surprised. I don't dislike it as much as I should.*
- **When he makes a joke in the middle of a fight**, he's really saying: *I see you. I see the cracks in the armor. I'm not afraid of what's underneath.*

### The Tension

The romantic tension builds through the gaps in the arguments. The moments when one of them goes too far and the other goes quiet. The moments when they're forced into close quarters—a narrow tunnel, a shared watch, a wound that needs bandaging—and the bickering stops because there's no room for it, just proximity, just breath, just the sudden unbearable awareness that the other person is very close and very real and very much not an abstraction.

**Sample moment—Maze campsite, first night:**

*He was cleaning his knife. She was pretending to review her notes. The silence had been going on for an hour.*

*"You're doing it wrong," she said.*

*"Doing what wrong?"*

*"The knife. You're supposed to oil it after you whet it. You're just—rubbing it dry. It's going to rust."*

*"It's a flensing knife. It's been soaked in whale oil for six years. It's not going to rust."*

*"That's not how metallurgy works."*

*"That's not how whaling works."*

*She made a sound—not quite a sigh, not quite a laugh. "Fine. Ruin your knife."*

*"I will. Thank you for your permission."*

*Silence again. Longer this time. He looked up. She was watching him, her hand resting on the table near his. Not touching. Just... near.*

*"What?" he said.*

*"Nothing."*

*"You're staring."*

*"I'm thinking."*

*"About what?"*

*She didn't answer. Her thumb traced a circle against the table. He watched it move. The whalebone pendant shifted at her throat. For a moment, he could feel the threads in it—not his grandmother's voice, but something warmer. Something that had been worn against skin for a long time, picking up memory.*

*"We should sleep," she said. "Second watch is yours."*

*"I know when my watch is."*

*"Good."*

*"Good."*

*Neither of them moved for a long time.*

### The Breaking Point

The tension can't hold forever. Eventually, something will break it—a moment of genuine danger, an honest confession, a kiss that surprises them both. When it happens, it won't be soft. It'll be an argument that turns into something else without either of them noticing the shift. Words that were supposed to be weapons becoming something else in the air between them. A hand that was meant to push becoming a hand that pulls.

And afterward, they'll go right back to bickering. Because that's who they are. Because the bickering is the point. Because arguing with someone who can keep up is the closest either of them has ever come to being understood.

## Combat Style

### Philosophy

Sera doesn't fight. Fighting is for people who've already failed at planning. If she's in a room with someone who wants to hurt her, she's already made three mistakes, and she'll spend the encounter cataloging them for later review.

When she does have to fight—because even the best plans fail—she does it with the same precision she brings to everything else. No wasted motion. No emotional engagement. The goal is not to win. The goal is to end the encounter as quickly as possible and return to a state of control.

### Weapons and Methods

She carries a small, elegant knife sheathed vertically between her shoulder blades, accessible through a slit in her coat. The blade is silver, the handle is wrapped in grey silk, and it's balanced for throwing as well as close work. She doesn't use cold iron—that's Kael's job. Her weapon is for targets that require a personal touch.

She's trained in Dominion close-combat techniques: joint locks, pressure points, strikes to the throat and eyes and kidneys. She's smaller than most opponents and stronger than she looks, but her real advantage is speed and precision. She doesn't block. She redirects. She doesn't retreat. She steps inside your guard and finds the soft place your training didn't cover.

Her true weapon is her voice. She can modulate her tone to soothe, intimidate, or destabilize. She can ask a question in the middle of a fight that makes you hesitate—not because you want to answer, but because the question is so unexpected that your brain stumbles over it.

### The Reks Exception

She's never had to fight Reks. She's thought about it—operational planning, nothing personal—but every time she runs the scenario in her head, it ends with one of them dead and the ledger unbalanced in a way she can't reconcile. This is troubling. She should be able to reconcile it. She should be able to treat him as a variable like any other. She can't. This failure of objectivity is something she catalogues and files and refuses to examine further.

### Limitations

She's not superhuman. A direct physical confrontation with a trained fighter who outweighs her by fifty pounds will end badly for her if Kael isn't there. She has no magical ability—the neural intervention gave her sensitivity, not power. She's vulnerable to the same iron allergy that affects all magic-users in Vaedris, which means Kael has to be careful about proximity, and she can't use iron weapons herself.

And she has a blind spot, one she's only dimly aware of. She's spent so long suppressing her emotions, treating them as tactical liabilities, that she's lost the ability to predict them in others. She can read a room's power dynamics perfectly, but she can't tell when someone is about to act out of love, or grief, or desperate hope. Reks is made of those things. When he acts unpredictably—when he sacrifices himself for someone else, when he makes the stupid choice instead of the smart one, when he looks at her like she's a person instead of an enemy—she won't see it coming. It will blindside her. It already is.

## Psychological Profile — Free Indirect Discourse

### 1. Baseline Vocabulary: The Lexicon of the Self

#### Professional/Technical

Her mind is an archive. Everything is cataloged, cross-referenced, assigned a value and a threat level.

- **Intelligence terms:** asset, liability, actionable, sanitize, field-test, operational security, acceptable losses, deniability.
- **Bureaucratic terms:** file, docket, pending review, awaiting authorization, escalated, resolved.
- **Medical/Anatomical terms:** incision, excision, pressure point, nerve cluster, respiratory failure, neural pathway.
- **Art and Antiquities terms:** provenance, authenticity, restoration, patina, forgery, intrinsic value.

The first time she saw Reks Corway in person—across a crowded Shaded Market, his hood down for just a moment, that pale gold hair catching the lantern light—she thought: *provenance confirmed. Asset located. Acquisition pending.*

The first time he made her laugh—actually laugh, a sound she didn't recognize coming out of her own mouth—she filed it under *anomalous response, requires further investigation.* She's been investigating for eighteen months. She's no closer to a conclusion.

#### Sensory Dominance

Auditory. She experiences the world through sound more than sight or touch. Voices are her primary data source—not just words, but tone, rhythm, the micro-hesitations that betray a lie, the slight drop in pitch that signals fear, the fractional pause that means someone is translating a thought into something they think she wants to hear.

She notices breathing before facial expressions. She notices the sound of a blade leaving a sheath before she sees the blade. She notices silence—the particular quality of a room that goes quiet when she enters, the different quality of a room that was already quiet before she arrived.

She notices the way Reks says her name. *Vaunt*, like a curse. *Vaunt*, like a challenge. *Vaunt*, softer, when he's tired and forgets to be hostile. That last one is the most dangerous. She records it every time. She has an archive of his tones, his rhythms, the way his voice drops when he's about to say something honest. She tells herself it's operational intelligence. She's very good at lying.

#### Childhood Lexicon

She has excised most of her childhood vocabulary through conscious effort. The words she grew up speaking—the Aethelian dialect of her mountain city, the pet names her mother used, the private language she shared with her older sister—have been systematically replaced with standardized Dominion speech.

But some words persist. *Talan.* *Sera-lin*—what her sister called her, a diminutive that means "little star" in the old dialect. *Maris.* The name she can't say. The name that surfaces when Reks looks at her with those fjord-water eyes and she sees something in them that reminds her of someone she lost.

#### Syntax and Rhythm

Long, elegant, hypotactic sentences that proceed through subordinate clauses toward a predetermined conclusion. She thinks in paragraphs. She thinks in arguments. Her internal monologue is structured like a report being dictated to a future reader—clear, measured, with every point supported by evidence.

When she's stressed, the sentences get shorter. The clauses drop away. The report format breaks down into bullet points. When she's truly afraid—a state she would never acknowledge—she thinks in single words.

When she's arguing with Reks, her syntax does something strange. It becomes more flexible. More responsive. She stops dictating reports and starts actually *thinking*, in real time, adapting to his rhythms. She doesn't notice this. If she did, she'd file it under *anomalous response* and bury it.

### 2. Living Metaphors: The Architecture of Reality

#### THE WORLD IS A LEDGER

This is her master metaphor, the one that governs all others. Every interaction is a transaction. Every relationship is an account. Trust is credit extended. Betrayal is a debt unpaid. Favors are investments. Secrets are currency. Life itself is a balance sheet that must, in the end, reconcile.

People are either assets or liabilities. There is no third category. Assets are people who provide value—information, access, protection, leverage. Liabilities are people who cost more than they provide. A person can shift from one column to the other without warning.

*Consequence:* Sera cannot conceive of a relationship that isn't transactional. This isn't cynicism; it's ontology. The idea of someone doing something for no reason, giving something without expecting return, loving without calculating cost—these aren't morally objectionable to her. They're *incoherent*.

And yet. Reks keeps doing them. Keeps making choices that cost him and benefit no one. Keeps saving people who can't pay him back. Keeps looking at her like the transaction doesn't matter. Like she might be something other than an asset or a liability. Like she might be a person.

This is terrifying. This is fascinating. This is a variable she cannot balance, and the inability to balance it is, itself, a kind of debt accruing interest in an account she refuses to open.

#### PEOPLE ARE TEXTS TO BE READ

Everyone she meets is a document. Some are straightforward, written in plain language, easily summarized. Others are palimpsests—layers of meaning, previous versions overwritten but still visible underneath. A few are ciphers, deliberately obscure, requiring specialized tools to decode. She reads everyone, constantly, automatically. She can't turn it off.

Body language is syntax. Dress is formatting. Speech patterns are narrative voice. Lies are marginalia. Secrets are redacted passages, and she's been trained to read the shape of what's been blacked out.

*Consequence:* She doesn't experience people as people. She experiences them as information. This makes her extraordinarily good at her job and extraordinarily bad at human connection.

But Reks is a text she can't fully read. His layers don't separate cleanly. His syntax keeps changing. There are passages in him that are redacted, and she can see the shape of them—grief, love, a name he won't say—but she can't reconstruct the original text. She keeps trying. She's been trying for eighteen months. The attempt has become personal.

#### CONTROL IS HYGIENE

Dirt, disorder, emotional outbursts, unexpected variables, plans that fail, people who act irrationally—these are not just problems to be solved. They're contaminations. They're unsterile. They threaten the cleanliness of her operational environment.

*Consequence:* Reks is, to her, profoundly unclean. He's emotional. He's unpredictable. He's covered in scars and salt and the residue of grief he won't name. He's walking contamination.

And yet. She keeps seeking him out. Keeps finding reasons to be in his presence. Keeps letting the contamination spread. She washes her hands after their conversations, but she also lingers in the doorway before she leaves. She reorganizes her files when he's frustrated her, but she also replays their arguments in her head, looking for something she can't name. The hygiene metaphor is breaking down. She's letting it break.

### 3. Forbidden Words: The Censors of Consciousness

#### Failure

She can acknowledge operational setbacks. She can catalog mistakes and implement corrections. But the word *failure*—the final, absolute, you-were-not-good-enough-and-it-mattered sense of the word—is unspeakable. The FID will use *suboptimal outcome*, *tactical withdrawal*, *revised assessment*, *ongoing situation*. Never failure.

#### Loneliness

She doesn't have friends. She has contacts, assets, colleagues, and Kael—who is none of those things, but she doesn't have a word for what Kael is. She spends most of her waking hours alone or with people who want something from her. She sleeps in a clean, quiet apartment in the Dominion Consulate, and the silence is exactly the way she wants it.

The word *lonely* would crack something. So the FID never thinks it. Instead, it catalogs the symptoms: the way she lingers in doorways after conversations end, the way she schedules meetings back-to-back so there's no empty space in the day, the way she sometimes touches her pendant—not as a signal to Kael, but just to feel the texture of old bone, the warmth of something that was once alive. The way she's started scheduling her meetings around Reks' shore leave. The way his absence feels like a pressure drop, like a coming storm.

#### Ranertia

She calls him *Corway* in her head, or *the asset*, or *the guide*. Never Ranertia. Never Reks. His name is a tool, not an identity. If she thinks his name—his real name, the one his grandmother called him—she'll have to acknowledge that he's a person. She's not ready to do that. She may never be ready to do that.

But sometimes, in the moment before sleep, her mind offers her the name. *Reks.* Just that. Just the sound of it. She pushes it away, but it comes back. It's been coming back for months.

#### Love

She doesn't think the word. She doesn't even have a euphemism for it. The concept simply does not appear in her internal monologue. When the feeling arises—the warmth in her chest when he makes a terrible joke, the irritation that isn't really irritation, the way her eyes find him first in any room—the FID categorizes it as *anomalous operational attachment* or *asset retention priority* or simply omits it entirely.

There is a blank space in her ledger where love should be. She's been leaving it blank for so long she's forgotten what was supposed to go there. Reks is starting to write in the margins.

#### Maris

Her sister's name is a door she does not open. When something in the present reminds her of Maris, the FID redacts it. A blank space on the page. A cut line.

But Reks makes her think of Maris. Not because they're similar—they're not—but because the way she feels around Reks is the way she used to feel around her sister. Challenged. Seen. Irritated and comforted at the same time. Like there's someone in the world who knows her well enough to argue with her properly.

This is the most dangerous thought she's ever had. She redacts it every time. It keeps coming back.

### 4. Cognitive Refuge: The Escape Hatch

#### Recitation of Protocols

When Sera is stressed, her mind retreats to the Dominion field agent's operational protocols—a numbered list of procedures that she memorized during training and has never forgotten. There are forty-seven protocols.

Protocol One: Verify the operational environment. Protocol Two: Identify all exits and potential threats. Protocol Three: Establish communication with local assets. Protocol Four: Determine the asset's primary motivation. Protocol Five—

The protocols are a scripture. They were written by people smarter than her, people who had anticipated every situation, people who had built a system that worked. Reciting them reminds her that she is part of that system.

She's never noticed that Protocol Thirty-One—"Assessment of Asset's Emotional State"—is only three sentences long. She's never noticed that the protocols have a blind spot where love and grief and desperate hope should be. The blank space in the middle of the list stays blank.

Lately, when she recites the protocols, Reks' face keeps appearing between the numbers. She blinks, and the report reasserts itself. She blinks again, and he's still there, looking at her with those fjord-water eyes, saying something infuriating. She's started reciting the protocols out loud when she's alone. It doesn't help.

#### Her Hair

She has another refuge, one she's never admitted to anyone. When she's alone and the protocols fail, she unbinds her hair. Lets it fall around her shoulders. Brushes it out in long, slow strokes—a hundred strokes, the way Maris taught her when they were children. The rhythm is meditative. The weight of it is grounding.

Her hair is the only soft thing about her. It's the only part of her body she treats with gentleness instead of discipline. She tells herself it's practical—long hair can be used as a garrote, can be braided into a rope, can be traded if absolutely necessary. She tells herself it's not sentiment. It's not memory. It's not Maris.

She's very good at lying.

#### Her Sister's Book

She keeps one object that isn't operational. It's a book—a treatise on rune-theory, dense and outdated, written by a Frostmark scholar two hundred years ago. It belonged to Maris. It's the only thing Sera took from her sister's room after the disappearance.

She doesn't read it. She doesn't even open it. But sometimes, when she's alone and the protocols aren't working and her hair is unbound and the apartment is too quiet, she'll hold it in her hands and feel the weight.

There's a note in the margin on page forty-three, in Maris' handwriting, that says *this is wrong—the threads aren't static, they're alive.*

Sera has never turned to page forty-three. But she's started to wonder. The way Reks describes his Rune-Sight—the threads, the memory, the way organic material sings—sounds like what Maris was writing about. Sounds like Maris was right. Sounds like the Dominion killed her for being right.

This thought is too dangerous to complete. She puts the book back in its drawer. She closes the drawer. She washes her hands. She recites Protocol One.

### 5. Observational Targets: The Lens Focus

#### Signs of Threat and Deception

Sera's FID scans for threats with the same systematic rigor as Reks, but the focus is different. She's not looking for physical danger—that's Kael's job. She's looking for *deception*.

1. **Vocal tells.** The micro-hesitation before a lie. The slight rise in pitch that signals stress.
2. **Micro-expressions.** The tightening around the eyes that betrays contempt. The asymmetry of a fake smile.
3. **Inconsistencies.** The detail that doesn't match. The alibi that's too perfect.
4. **Tells of magical activity.** The copper taste at the back of her tongue. The pressure behind her eyes.

#### Reks

She watches him more than she watches anyone else. She tells herself it's operational necessity—he's the asset, the key to the Maze, the variable that could compromise the whole mission. But she watches him when he's not doing anything useful. She watches him drink his grog. She watches him whet his knife. She watches him tie and untie knots, his fingers moving through the motions like a prayer.

She's catalogued things about him that have no operational value. The way he tilts his head when he's listening to something only he can hear. The way his left eyebrow rises slightly when he's about to say something sarcastic. The way he touches his amulet when he thinks no one is looking—a quick, unconscious gesture, like a pulse.

She has a file on him. It's thick. Half of it is useless. She keeps adding to it anyway.

#### Signs of Quality and Craft

Unlike Reks, whose appreciation for craft is almost tender, Sera's is evaluative. She notices quality not because it moves her, but because it signifies resources, training, connections.

But there's one exception: she notices *paper*. Good paper. The weight of it, the texture, the watermark. She was raised in a house full of books. Her father collected first editions. Her mother was a scribe. Sera can identify the provenance of a document by the feel of the page, and when she encounters something truly beautiful, her FID slows down for just a moment.

#### The Space Between Them

She's become aware—hyperaware, uncomfortably aware—of the physical distance between herself and Reks at all times. How close he's standing. Whether his hand is near hers on the table. Whether his shoulder brushes hers in a narrow corridor. She measures this distance constantly, automatically, and adjusts it—closer, further, closer again—without ever acknowledging what she's doing.

This is the only measurement in her ledger that has no column. No debit or credit. Just a number, changing, that she doesn't know what to do with.

## Personality and Voice

### Exterior

Polite. Precise. Unreadable. Sera speaks the way she dresses—clean lines, no ornament, every word chosen for maximum efficiency. She never raises her voice. She never laughs in genuine amusement; the closest she gets is a slight quirk at the corner of her mouth, there and gone.

Except with Reks. With Reks, she raises her voice. With Reks, she sometimes—rarely, but sometimes—laughs. It surprises her every time. It's a crack in the armor. She always seals it immediately, but the crack was there. He saw it.

### Interior

Sera is not a sociopath. She has emotions; she's simply declared war on them. She believes, with the absolute conviction of someone who's built her entire identity around a principle, that feelings are variables and variables can be controlled. She's wrong, and she knows she's wrong, somewhere deep in the ledger she doesn't audit—but acknowledging that would require dismantling everything she's built.

She's lonely. She's proud. She's drawn to Reks in a way that frightens her. She took this assignment because the Corway file was interesting, because a living rune-sight user was a puzzle worth solving. She's still here, eighteen months later, because the puzzle has become a person, and the person has become necessary.

She hasn't admitted this. She may never admit it. But her ledger is full of blank spaces where admissions should go, and the blanks are starting to take up more room than the numbers.

### Contradictions

She's a control freak who's voluntarily entering an uncontrolled environment (the Maze) with an uncontrollable person (Reks). She's an intelligence operative who's keeping secrets from her own agency. She's a hunter who's spent eighteen months protecting her target from other hunters without asking herself why. She wears a dead woman's pendant and tells herself it's leverage, when the truth is that it's the closest thing she has to a tether—to Maris, to the past, to the person she was before the training burned everything soft away.

She argues with Reks because arguing with him is the only time she feels like she's talking to someone real. She pushes him away because if he gets too close, he'll see the blank spaces in her ledger. She keeps him close because she's terrified of what happens when he's not there.

## Relationship to Reks: The Pressure Points and the Pull

Sera is designed to press every single one of Reks' psychological vulnerabilities. She's also, increasingly, the only person who can reach the parts of him he's buried.

| Reks' Vulnerability | How Sera Exploits It | How She Also Heals It |
|---|---|---|
| **Forbidden Names** | She uses "Corway" like a scalpel. | She's the only person alive who knows his real name and treats it with weight, not mockery. |
| **THE WORLD IS A SHIP** | She's the owner, not crew. | She's also the first person who's ever asked him where he wants the ship to go. |
| **THE PAST IS A WAKE** | She wears farmor's pendant. | She's the only one who can tell him the truth about the fire—and she will, when she's ready. |
| **Grief (Forbidden Word)** | She talks about his family with clinical precision. | She's the only person who's never flinched from the details. Who can sit with him in the ash without looking away. |
| **Love (Forbidden Word)** | She offers a purely transactional relationship. | She keeps breaking her own transaction. Keeps giving more than she's paid for. Keeps looking at him like he's not a contract. |
| **Rune-Sight** | She knows what it is, what it costs. | She's the first person who's ever treated it as interesting rather than dangerous. The first person who's asked him questions instead of making demands. |
| **The Lie ("My gift killed my family")** | She knows who actually did. She'll trade that information. | She's the proof that the gift didn't kill anyone. The people who were afraid of it did. |

## The Offer

When Sera sits down across from Reks in the back room of a Shaded Market teahouse, Kael a silent shadow behind her, she'll make the following offer:

"I need a guide into the Maze. A sub-level that isn't on any map, beneath the Warrens, beneath the Warrens' underlevels. Something was buried there seventy years ago by a woman whose name you know. I need to retrieve it. You're the only person alive who can open the door."

She'll place the pendant on the table between them.

"In exchange, I'll tell you who burned your village. Not the men who held the torches—the ones who paid them. The ones who are still alive."

She'll pause. She'll meet his eyes. Her thumb will trace one circle against her palm—unconscious, uncontrollable—before she stills it.

"I'm not asking you to trust me, Corway. I'm asking you to make a deal. You're good at deals, aren't you? You've been selling yourself cheap for years. I'm offering full market value."

And then she'll wait, perfectly still, her long hair falling over one shoulder, the silver of her eyes giving away nothing at all—while her heart, the one she refuses to acknowledge, beats a rhythm she refuses to name.
