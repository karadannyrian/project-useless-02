# Ranertia "Reks" Corway — Complete Character Profile

## Physical Description

### The Man

Wiry isn't just a build—it's a lifestyle carved by years of bad food, hard labor, and the constant low-grade starvation of a sailor's pay. At twenty-three, Reks looks both younger and older than he is: the boyish angularity of his jaw is undercut by the permanent hollows beneath his cheekbones, the fine lines already etched around his eyes from squinting into salt-spray.

He stands just shy of six feet, but the way he carries himself—shoulders curled slightly inward, weight always on the balls of his feet—makes him seem smaller until he moves. Then the illusion shatters. When Reks climbs, fights, or runs, his body unfolds into something else entirely: all tensile strength and improbable reach, the coiled efficiency of rigging pulled taut.

His hands are his most honest feature. The knuckles are enlarged from cold-weather labor, the fingers long and clever but cross-hatched with old rope burns and the pale crescents of flensing accidents. A marline spike callus thickens the heel of his right palm. These are hands that have hauled frozen sailcloth at midnight and held a dying crewmate's face steady while the sea took him.

### The Corway Look

The hair is inconvenient. Pale gold, almost white in direct sun, the color of sea-foam or old ivory. It falls past his ears in unkempt hanks, usually tied back with tarred twine, and he keeps meaning to cut it short or dye it dark but never has the coin or the courage. In Midden, he wears a waxed canvas hood. The hair is a beacon. The hair has nearly gotten him killed twice.

The eyes are worse. Deep blue—not the cheerful blue of a summer sky, but the color of fjord water where it drops into the abyss, a blue that swallows light. The Corway eyes. They're set deep under a brow that's seen too much weather, and they don't match the rest of his face. The rest of his face is a dock-rat's mask: sardonic, watchful, empty. The eyes are old. When his Rune-Sight activates, the pupils contract to pinpricks and the irises seem to darken, pulling light in. Sailors on the *Grey-Eye* have learned not to meet his gaze when the hunt is on.

### Body as Record

His torso is a palimpsest of survival. The oldest scar is a burn across his left shoulder blade—a childhood lesson, his grandmother's oil lamp knocked over when the soldiers came. A puckered star beneath his right ribs where a whalebone splinter punched through during a bad hunt. The rope-burn bracelets at both wrists, some from his work, some from his own hand pressing cold iron to the skin when the Sight wouldn't stop. A fresh cut on his forearm, still scabbed, from a Maze-job gone sideways.

He moves with a sailor's permanent compensation—a slight sway, as if the ground might pitch at any moment. Ashore, he walks too quietly, feet finding the deck-planks that aren't there. Aloft, in the rigging, he's the only place he's ever felt like his body makes sense.

### Dress

Practical and forgettable. A salt-stained wool tunic, faded to the color of overcast sky. Leather trousers patched at the knees with sailcloth. A short oilskin coat, waxed to blackness, with deep interior pockets that hold his knives, his marline spike, and a whalebone amulet he never shows. Boots are knee-high and cracked, the left one re-soled with a piece of old harness leather that squeaks when it's wet. Around his neck, a strip of grey wool that was once his mother's scarf—he winds it over his mouth and nose in a fight, a habit he doesn't examine.

His only visible weapon is the flensing knife sheathed horizontally across the small of his back, its handle worn to the shape of his grip. Everything else is hidden. A man who looks unarmed is underestimated. A man who is underestimated survives.

## Lineage: House Corway

### The Forgotten House

The Corways were a minor noble house of Frostmark, sworn to the crown in Halvask but never seated on the Jarlmoot. Their domain was a stretch of ironwood forest and a deep-water anchorage on the northeastern coast, a place where the fjords narrowed and the wind never stopped howling. They were not jarls; they were something older and stranger.

Unlike most Frostmark rune-carvers, who laboriously prepare whalebone and driftwood mediums through ritual, the Corways possessed a rare hereditary talent: Rune-Sight. Certain members of the bloodline could perceive the latent memory within organic materials—the pattern of a storm that once battered a piece of driftwood, the echo of a whale's death-struggle in its bone. They didn't carve runes so much as reveal what was already there.

This made their mediums exceptionally potent with minimal preparation, and their ship-wards were legendary. A Corway-carved keel could navigate pack ice blind. A Corway ward-stone could hold a storm at bay for hours. The house's motto was "The Sea Remembers"—the same words Frostmark itself claims, but for the Corways, it was literal.

### The Fall

Seventy years ago, the house was attainted and struck from the rolls. The official charge was heresy: Lord Corway was accused of attempting to read the "Deep Chorus" itself—the ancestral voices of Frostmark's collective ritual—as though it were a medium to be exploited. Whether this was true, or a pretext by rival houses who coveted Corway ironwood and anchorage, is a matter of whispered debate. What is certain is that the house was purged: its holdings seized, its rune-libraries burned, its surviving members scattered or slain. The name Corway is now spoken only in old sailors' songs and cautionary tales told to apprentice rune-carvers.

### Physical Legacy

The Corway look—striking blonde hair, pale as sea-foam, and deep blue eyes, the color of fjord water in winter—is common among old Frostmark bloodlines, but in the Corways it bred exceptionally true. For Ranertia, it's a walking brand. In the wrong company, that hair and those eyes mark him as a descendant of a proscribed house, a potential heretic, and a loose end.

### Rune-Sight in Practice

Ranertia's Rune-Sight manifests as faint, shimmering lines visible only to him—the memory-threads within objects. A splinter of ship-timber might show him the impact that broke it. A whalebone charm might whisper the beast's final dive. In combat, he can read the stress-points in a weapon or the imminent arc of a blow.

But the sight is not free; it requires the Mind's Eye focus that all Vaedrisian magic demands. Pain, exhaustion, or intoxication blinds him to the threads. Worse, using Rune-Sight on anything touched by the Deep Chorus is both seductive and potentially catastrophic—the ancestral voices do not like being read.

Using Rune-Sight is casting; it is subject to the Law of Conservation (the Toll drains his stamina and body heat), Iron Allergy (contact with cold iron blinds him entirely), and the Mind's Eye (he must maintain absolute focus, or the threads dissolve).

## Occupation

### The Cover: Whale-Runner

Reks crews aboard the *Grey-Eye*, a twin-masted whaling schooner out of Midden that hunts the deepwater frost-whales north of the Helspine. The pay is terrible, the mortality rate is worse, and no one asks questions about your past.

The work is rigging, sail-work, spotting whale-spouts from the crow's nest, and harpoon retrieval. In a hunt, he's the man in the bow of the chase-boat, rope coiled around his ankle—if the whale dives, he's meant to cut himself free before the line drags him under. He's gotten very fast with a knife.

The organic materials aboard a whaler are ideal for his Rune-Sight: whalebone, ambergris, spermaceti, driftwood harpoon shafts—all of it sings with deep-sea memory. He can read a whale's path before the hunt, sense a storm in a piece of baleen, or feel the stress-point in a mast about to crack. The *Grey-Eye* has an uncanny survival record; the captain suspects why, but keeps his mouth shut and his harpooner paid.

### The Real Occupation: Maze-Runner

When the *Grey-Eye* docks in Midden for the off-season, Reks has a second trade: guide and recovery agent for the Warrens. The Maze is the ancient undercity beneath Midden, and it swallows fools daily. Reks will take coin to guide smugglers, scholars, or the terminally curious through its safer routes, and to retrieve things—or people—that have gotten lost.

It's a grey-market profession, and it puts him in contact with the Shaded Market, the Sentinels, and the sort of people who recognize Frostmark rune-lore when they see it. He hates the work but needs the coin.

## Combat Style

Reks is not a warrior. He's a survivor who fights like the deck of a pitching ship: no footing, no rules, everything wet and moving and trying to kill you.

### Core Principles

- **Motion Over Position:** Reks never plants his feet. He fights off rigging, railings, overturned tables, and his opponent's momentum. He's always climbing, swinging, or diving—verticality is his advantage.
- **Small Blades, Close Quarters:** His weapons are a flensing knife (curved, razor-sharp, designed to part blubber from meat), a rope-cutter's gut-hook, and whatever's at hand (marline spike, broken bottle, fish-gutting blade). These are short-range, brutal tools. He has to get inside your guard to kill you.
- **Harpoon and Line:** For distance, he carries a small throwing harpoon—more a heavy javelin—with a coiled retrieval line. He uses it not just to impale, but to hook limbs, snare weapons, and yank opponents off-balance. Think grappling hook meets spear.
- **Death by Environment:** In the Maze, he doesn't just stab you. He slashes a salt-bag to blind you, kicks a lantern to set the floor ablaze, cuts a rope to drop a cargo net on your head. Every fight ends up a disaster zone.

### Rune-Sight in Combat

When his Mind's Eye is clear, the world becomes a web of threat-lines. A red thread arcs from a sword's tip to his ribs: the killing path. He twists before the blow commits. The haft of an enemy's axe shows a hairline glow: the stress-fracture from a bad temper. He strikes it with his knife-butt and the weapon shatters. A guard's heartbeat pulses visibly through the floorboards—he can feel the rhythm and move between beats.

But the Toll is brutal. Mid-fight, Rune-Sight eats his body heat; steam rises from his skin even in cold air. Extended use drops him into hypothermic shock if he's not careful. His iron allergy means a single manacle, a cheap iron blade pressed to his skin, can blind him completely. He fights with the constant calculation of a man who is one mistake from helpless.

## Backstory

### The Wound

Ranertia Corway was born in hiding, in a nameless fishing village clinging to the rocks of what was once Corway land. His grandmother, the last rune-carver of the old house, taught him to read the memory in driftwood before she died. His father forbade it, calling the gift a curse. His mother said nothing, but hid cold iron charms in his bedding.

When he was sixteen, the last Corway hideout was raided. He survived because he'd rowed out alone to check lobster pots, drunk on stolen mead. He returned to ash and silence. The raiders wore no colors, but they carried cold iron nets—anti-mage tools standard in Frostmark. He doesn't know if they were bounty hunters, rivals, or agents of the Jarlmoot itself.

### The Flight

He's been running ever since—through the port cities of Frostmark, aboard smugglers' ships, into the neutral ground of Midden where he survives as a dock-rat, petty thief, and occasional guide for those who need to navigate the Warrens. He goes by "Reks" because "Ranertia Corway" is a death sentence.

### The Drive (External)

Find the people who ordered the purge and make them answer for it. Or, if he's past revenge, protect the last few who might carry Corway blood from being hunted as heretics.

### The Drive (Internal)

Prove that the Corway name is not a curse. That Rune-Sight is a gift, not a heresy. That "Reks" can be his own man, not just the last echo of a dead house.

### The Lie He Believes

*"My gift killed my family."* Or, in his darker moments: *"I am the last, and when I die, the Corway name dies with me."*

## Inciting Incident

When the *Grey-Eye* docks in Midden for the final time this season, Reks plans to take his pay and disappear inland, away from the sea and its memories. But a woman from the Shaded Market—a Dominion agent with too much knowledge about proscribed Frostmark bloodlines—offers him a job: guide her deep into the Maze, into a sub-level that shouldn't exist, where something carved with Corway runes has been unearthed. The pay is enough to buy passage to Valenwood and vanish forever.

The catch: her cold iron bodyguard never blinks, and she's wearing a pendant of whalebone that whispers his grandmother's name.

## Daily Life and Habits

- **Ritual:** Every morning, Reks runs a whetstone over his flensing knife. It's meditative, a holdover from his grandmother's rune-preparation rituals. He doesn't sing the old songs aloud anymore, but he mouths the words.
- **Drink:** He nurses a single cup of grog for hours, never drunk, always aware. If he drinks to excess, the Rune-Sight bleeds uncontrollably, and he sees things in the dark water he'd rather forget.
- **Iron Scars:** His forearms are cross-hatched with pale scars, many from his own doing—pressing cold iron to his skin to blind his Sight before it overwhelms him. A Sentinel who sees those will know what they mean.
- **Tattoos:** He has a faded, self-inked rune on his inner wrist, the Corway ship-ward. It's mostly for remembrance. It stopped working the night his family died.

## Psychological Profile — Free Indirect Discourse

### 1. Baseline Vocabulary: The Lexicon of the Self

#### Professional/Technical

His mind is rigged with ship-language. The world is a vessel, and everything in it is either seaworthy or not.

- **Rigging terms:** tension, slack, chafe, fouled, belay, overhaul, splice, bitter end.
- **Whaling terms:** flense, sound, breach, spout, flukes, the Nantucket sleigh-ride.
- **Weather terms:** the glass is falling, wind-shift, white horses, the calm before the gale.
- **Navigation terms:** dead reckoning, bearing, lee shore, soundings, the set of the current.

The first time he kissed a girl, he thought: *her mouth gave way like a rope through a well-greased block.*

#### Sensory Dominance

Kinesthetic. He experiences the world through tension and motion, through the pull of gravity and the resistance of materials. A room isn't "dark," it's *the air thick as bilge-water, pressing in from all sides.* A threat isn't "dangerous," it's *a line about to part, the twang before the snap.* Even his emotions register physically: fear is a cold weight in the stomach, anger is a line hauled too tight, grief is *the long sag after the cable cuts.*

#### Childhood Lexicon

A handful of Frostmark words, spoken only in his head, never aloud. *Far* (father, but also "shelter"), *mor* (mother), *farmor* (grandmother), *liten* (little one—what farmor called him). The word *hearth-ember* for the feeling of being safe and warm. The phrase *the sea remembers* as both promise and threat. These words surface when he's exhausted, wounded, or alone in the dark of the forecastle. He crushes them down like a spent lamp-wick.

#### Syntax and Rhythm

Short, declarative fragments punctuated by longer, looping observations when his Rune-Sight engages. His thoughts stutter when he's anxious and flow like current when he's focused. He does not think in complex sentences. He thinks in conditions.

Wind's shifting. The glass is falling. She's going to ask about the amulet. She's been working up to it since the Spire-lock. Don't look at her hands. Look at the space between her shoulder and the wall—if she moves, that space closes first. The amulet's warm against his sternum. Farmor's work. The sea remembers. Shut up. The line's fouled in the block. No it isn't. There is no line. There is no block. There's just a woman in a dark room and she's going to ask and he's going to lie and the lie is going to taste like cold iron.

### 2. Living Metaphors: The Architecture of Reality

#### THE WORLD IS A SHIP

This is the master metaphor from which all others hang. A ship is a closed system, a fragile thing suspended over an abyss that wants to kill you. Everything must be maintained. Everything must be lashed down. People are either crew (useful, trusted, temporary) or passengers (dead weight, dangerous to rely on). Love is mutiny—a loss of command. Hope is a lee shore: pretty from a distance, but it'll break your keel if you drift too close.

*Consequence:* Reks cannot relax in a social situation because, on a ship, there is no off-watch for survival. Even in a tavern, his back finds a wall. Even in sleep, a part of him is listening for the change in the wind. He evaluates relationships by whether someone will cut your line when the whale dives or whether they'll try to save you and drown you both.

#### THE PAST IS A WAKE

The wake is the only proof of where a ship has been, but it's also just disturbed water, fading. You can't steer by it. You can't sail back through it. The Corway name, the burning village, his grandmother's songs—all wake. Sometimes beautiful in moonlight. Sometimes full of sharks. Always behind him. The metaphor lets him justify never looking back, but it also means he believes, somewhere deep, that he is unmoored—that without a wake worth following, he has no real course.

*Consequence:* He mistrusts nostalgia and anyone who asks about his past. Sentiment is barnacle-weight. Memory is a luxury for men with keels beneath them. When his Rune-Sight forces him to perceive the past (in whalebone, in driftwood, in the ash of his village), it sickens him not just physically but spiritually—he is violating his own core metaphor. He is facing the wake.

#### TRUTH IS A STRESS-FRACTURE

This one comes from Rune-Sight. Lies and evasions are smooth surfaces; the truth is the hairline crack that runs through them, the point where pressure will cause failure. He doesn't look for the truth in what people say. He looks for the flaw in their story, the one place where if you tap it with a marline spike, the whole thing shatters.

*Consequence:* He is constitutionally incapable of taking anything at face value, even kindness. A gift is a test. A compliment is a distraction. A declaration of loyalty is the moment right before the fracture. This makes him exhausting to care about and almost impossible to love.

### 3. Forbidden Words: The Censors of Consciousness

#### His Family's Names

He will not think *Ranertia.* The name belongs to a boy who died in the village, a boy farmor called *liten.* He thinks of himself as *Reks* even in the privacy of his own head, and when a memory surfaces—his mother's face, his father's voice—the FID will give the sensory detail but never the name. His father is *the man who taught him to splice rope.* His grandmother is *the hands that showed him the first rune-thread.* His mother is *the grey wool scarf.* To name them is to summon them, and to summon them is to admit they're dead.

#### Grief

The word does not exist in his lexicon. Instead, the FID will describe a sudden stillness, a long pause between thoughts, the taste of salt that isn't from the sea, a hand moving unconsciously to touch the amulet beneath his shirt. The closest he gets is the ship-term *hove to*—a ship that has stopped making way, sails backed, waiting out the storm. But he never completes the metaphor. He never names the storm.

#### Love

Equally forbidden, but handled differently. He'll use the metaphor of *fouled lines*—two ropes tangled so badly you have to cut one free. The idea of love as something good, something sustaining, is not just foreign but heresy against his architecture of survival. When he feels affection, the FID translates it into tactical terms: *she's useful, she's reliable, she's crew.* When the feeling deepens, the FID simply stops. A blank space. A cut line.

#### The Deep Chorus

He knows what the old Frostmark rituals call it. He knows his great-grandfather was accused of trying to read it. The phrase *Deep Chorus* only appears in his thoughts when he's half-asleep or bleeding out, and even then, it's immediately smothered: *Don't. Don't listen. It's just whale-song. It's just the cables groaning. Shut up shut up shut up.*

### 4. Cognitive Refuge: The Escape Hatch

#### Compulsive Rigging Inventory

When reality overwhelms—when the Sight won't stop, when memories break through, when someone is kind to him and he doesn't know what to do with it—Reks retreats into an obsessive catalog of the *Grey-Eye's* rigging. He mentally checks every shroud, every stay, every block and halyard, in order from bow to stern. The mainmast shrouds: port three, starboard three, all sound, no chafe. The forestay: tension good, the turnbuckle greased. The jib halyard: the sheave needs replacing, tell the bosun, you've told him twice, he won't listen.

This is not a grounding exercise; it's a dissociative one. The ship becomes more real than the room, the memory, the person touching his face. It's a seaworthy place. It has rules. It doesn't ask anything of him except maintenance.

When he's ashore and can't use the *Grey-Eye,* he defaults to knots. He mentally ties a bowline, a sheet bend, a clove hitch, a cat's-paw, a rolling hitch. His fingers twitch through the motions. If it's very bad, he'll pull out a length of tarred twine and do it for real, over and over, until the world stops screaming.

---

### 5. Observational Targets: The Lens Focus

#### Signs of Threat and Weakness

Reks' FID scans every room for tactical information before it registers anything else. The order of operations is rigid:

1. **Exits and ingress points.** Doors, windows, hatches, ladders, sewer grates. How many and can he reach them before anyone reaches him.
2. **Weapons and weapon-analogues.** Who's armed, who's carrying something that could be a weapon (a bottle, a chair leg, a heavy belt buckle), who's standing near something that could be a weapon.
3. **Power dynamics.** Who's afraid of whom. Who's in charge. Who thinks they're in charge. Who's about to find out they're wrong.
4. **Micro-expressions of displeasure.** The tightening around a mouth, the slight flare of nostrils, the way a hand curls toward a fist and then relaxes. He sees irritation before it becomes anger, fear before it becomes flight.
5. **Structural flaws.** The crack in the rafter, the rot in the floorboard, the hinge that'll pop if you kick it from the left. A lifetime of knowing exactly where to strike.

What he does not notice: the color of the curtains, the quality of the wine, the beauty of the fresco on the ceiling, the way someone's hair catches the light. Unless any of those things can be used as a weapon, an exit, or a warning, they do not exist to him.

#### Signs of Craft

The one exception. When Reks encounters a well-made thing—a perfectly spliced eye, a cleanly carven rune, a knife with a balance so true it feels like an extension of the hand—his FID becomes almost tender. The vocabulary sharpens, slows down, pays attention. He will spend three sentences describing the way a cooper fitted a barrel stave when he spent none on the woman who smiled at him.

The splice was beautiful. Seven tucks, each one lying flat against the lay of the rope, the bitter end trimmed so clean it vanished into the braid. A man who could splice like that didn't need to talk. The rope would tell you everything. The rope was still talking, if you knew how to listen.

The same tenderness extends, unbidden, to whales. He will not call it love. But when the *Grey-Eye* makes its kill and the water turns red, his FID goes quiet for a long time, and when it returns, it's describing the vastness of the eye, the barnacled architecture of the flukes, the way the final exhalation fountains into the sky like a soul departing. He has no framework for this feeling. So the FID just records, and the recording is grief whether he will name it or not.

#### Entities of Empathy

Whales. The *Grey-Eye* herself, which he thinks of as *she* and not *it.* And, buried so deep he rarely notices, the memory of a one-eared tomcat that lived in the rafters of the village longhouse. The cat's name was Mote, and it's the only name from the old life he can think without wanting to put cold iron to his skin. Mote survived the fire. Mote walked out of the ash with his whiskers singed and demanded to be fed. Reks doesn't know what happened to him after. Sometimes, during the rigging inventory, the thought of Mote slips through the gaps: *Probably dead by now. Old even then. Good cat. Don't.*

## Personality and Voice

### Exterior

Sardonic, guarded, speaks in clipped sentences. Uses humor like a shield. Deflects personal questions with a quip or a glare. He's the guy in the corner of the tavern with a good view of the door, nursing one drink all night.

### Interior

Deeply loyal to the few he trusts, but expects betrayal. Carries guilt—the Corway name got people killed, and he failed to protect them. Terrified of becoming the heretic his house was accused of being. Longs for anonymity and a quiet life but is constitutionally incapable of walking away from someone in chains.

### Contradictions

He's a meticulous planner who thrives in chaos. He'll pick a lock with surgical precision, then hurl himself off a rooftop on a half-formed gamble. His body is a mess of precision and reckless scar tissue.

---

## Skillset

### Primary Skills

Coastal survival (sailing, fishing, navigation by stars), parkour through rigging and rooftops, knife-fighting (small, fast, dirty), stealth and infiltration, an almost preternatural spatial awareness. He can read a room instantly—exits, threats, the biggest ego to flatter or prick.

### Unique Ability: Rune-Sight

The ability to perceive memory-threads in organic materials. In moments of focus, Reks sees faint, shimmering lines connecting stress-points, imminent strikes, or the history of an object. A lock's mechanism reveals its internal pattern as a ghostly sketch. A guard's loyalty threads to his captain. A killing blow reads as a red line before it lands. He can act on these glimpses, but the vision fades fast and leaves him mentally frayed.

### Limitations

No formal combat training. Gets demolished by a trained swordswoman in a fair fight. Largely self-taught in rune-lore; his knowledge is fragmentary and intuitive rather than scholarly. His Rune-Sight is useless if he's emotionally compromised, exhausted, or in contact with iron. Sometimes it shows him truths he absolutely doesn't want to see—like the memory of his family's last moments, recorded in the ash of their pyre.

## Key Relationships

### The Grey-Eye Crew

- **The Captain:** Knows more than he says. Keeps Reks employed because the ship has never lost a man to weather or whale on his watch. Doesn't ask questions.
- **The Bosun:** Old, scarred, missing two fingers on his left hand. Taught Reks to splice. The closest thing to a father figure still breathing.

### The Dominion Agent

A woman from the Shaded Market with too much knowledge about proscribed Frostmark bloodlines. Wears a whalebone pendant that whispers farmor's name. Her cold iron bodyguard never blinks. She's offering a job that might be a trap or might be the key to everything.

### Mote the Cat

One-eared tomcat from the village longhouse. Survived the fire. Current status unknown. Only name from the old life Reks can think without pain.
