# Chapter One: The Hunt

The drum was a second heartbeat.

Not the ship's bell—that was for watches, for meals, for the measured rhythm of a vessel under sail. The drum was for blood. A skin stretched over whalebone, beaten with a marline spike wrapped in leather, and the beat went straight from the oar-lock to the spine.

Reks pulled.

The oar was smooth where ten thousand strokes had worn it, rough where salt had pitted the grain. His hands found their place without looking—left above right, the marline-spike callus on his right palm pressing into the wood. The rope around his ankle was a live thing, coiled and waiting. He'd tied the knot himself. A bowline, easy to spill if the line ran. In theory.

The air was so cold it had teeth. Every breath scraped something raw at the back of his throat, and when he exhaled, the fog of it mixed with the spray from the oar-blades until he couldn't tell what was sea and what was lung. The water was grey-green, the color of a day-old bruise, and chunks of ice knocked against the chase-boat's hull with a sound like someone tapping a spoon against a clay cup. Polite. Patient. Waiting for the hull to crack.

"Steady."

Tamas Kelm's voice came from the stern, low and even, the way he'd talked Reks through his first flensing when the boy was fifteen and shaking so hard he'd nearly dropped the knife. The bosun was missing two fingers on his left hand—a whale-line accident, twenty years ago—and the stumps rested against the tiller like they'd been carved to fit it. He wasn't looking at the crew. He was looking past them, into the mist, where the whales were.

The drum kept beating.

Boat One was ahead of them, twenty yards to starboard. Reks could see Drenn Olsk in the bow, his harpoon already half-raised, the iron head catching the thin northern light. Drenn was built like a barrel and moved like one—all momentum, no grace—but he could throw. Reks had seen him bury a harpoon in a cow's heart-lung from thirty feet in a following sea. The man was an artist with iron in his hand and a bully with nothing in it.

Behind Drenn, two oarsmen Reks didn't know well—replacements picked up in Midden after the last hunt cost them a pair of hands to frostbite—and the boat-header, a grizzled Frostmarker named Orlen who'd been hunting whales since before Reks was born. Boat One was the lead boat, the strike boat. Boat Two was the back-up. The safety. The ones who pulled survivors from the water when things went wrong.

The drum beat faster.

"There," Tamas said.

Not a shout. The bosun never shouted. He said the word the way a man might note a change in the weather, and everyone in the boat looked where he was looking.

The bull breached.

It rose out of the sea like a continent being born. Water sheeted off its flanks in curtains, and the sound of it—tons of seawater falling back into the ocean—was a roar that swallowed the drum, the oars, the beat of Reks' own blood. The bull was old. You could see it in the scarring: white lines cross-hatched across the head, the healed-over punctures of old harpoon strikes that hadn't gone deep enough, the barnacles crusted along the jaw like a beard of stone. Its baleen was a cathedral of grey-white fronds, each one longer than a man was tall, and its eye—the left one, the one that rolled to fix on the chase-boats—was the size of a dinner plate.

It saw them.

Reks knew the difference between a whale that was running and a whale that was deciding. A running whale sounded the moment it sensed the boats—tail up, back arched, gone. This bull hung there, half-breached, its flukes still under the surface, and the eye moved from Boat One to Boat Two with something that wasn't fear.

It was old, and it was tired, and it was looking at them like it had done this before.

"Boat One's closing," someone said. Maybe Pip. The cabin boy was on the Grey-Eye's rail, too young for the chase-boats, too young for the hunt, but no one could keep him off the deck when the drum started. Reks didn't turn to look. His attention was on the bull, and the bull's attention was on Boat One, and something was wrong.

The flukes moved.

Not the casual sweep of a whale sounding. This was a gathering, a coiling, the way a rope bunched before the snap. The bull's body rolled, and the great tail came up out of the water, and Reks saw the arc before it happened—not with the Sight, not yet, just with the part of him that had spent seven years watching whales die and knowing which ones would die hard.

"Back," he said. "Tamas, tell them to back—"

The flukes came down.

They hit Boat One amidships. The sound was a clap of thunder and a wet crunch and a scream cut short all at once. The boat folded like a book closing, and the men inside it spilled into the water—Drenn, Orlen, the two Midden replacements—and the sea swallowed them. The water where Boat One had been was suddenly full of splinters and bubbles and the dark shapes of bodies.

The cold would kill them in minutes. Less, if the shock took them first.

The bull sounded. The flukes went up—a wall of grey flesh and white scarring—and slid under, and the line that was still attached to the harpoon in Boat One's bow ran out with a hiss that turned into a scream.

Reks felt the rope around his own ankle.

Boat Two was too close. The bull was going under, and the line was running, and if the whale dragged Boat One's wreckage down and turned—if it surfaced under them—they'd join the others in the water. The rule was simple: when the whale sounded, you cut the line. You saved the boat. You lived to hunt again.

But the men in the water.

Drenn was face-up, his beard a tangle of ice, his mouth open in a sound Reks couldn't hear over the drum that was still beating—someone had kept the drum going, stupid, stop the drum—and his eyes were wide and white and fixed on the sky. The others were darker shapes, arms flailing, heads barely above the surface.

The line was still running.

Reks let go of the oar.

He didn't think about it. Thinking would have meant choosing, and choosing would have meant weighing Drenn's life against his own, and there was no weight to assign. Drenn hated him. Drenn had called him a Corway bastard in front of half the crew last season, drunk on Midden rye, and the captain had pretended not to hear. Drenn was a bully and a coward and a man who hit the cabin boy when he thought no one was looking.

Drenn was crew.

Reks reached for the Sight.

It wasn't a decision. It was a door he'd learned to open without touching, a muscle he'd trained until the flexing of it was almost automatic. The world didn't change—that was the lie the old stories told. The world stayed exactly the same. The grey-green water, the ice-chunks, the splinters of Boat One, the bodies. What changed was Reks.

The threads came.

They were faint, shimmering, the color of light on water. The bull's path was a red arc cutting through the grey—not where it was, but where it would be. The stress-point at the base of its skull pulsed like a heartbeat, a node of brighter red where the vertebrae met, where the spine was thickest and most vulnerable. The dark lines converged around the men in the water: Drenn's thread, fraying, going dim at the edges; Orlen's, steadier, moving toward the nearest ice-floe; the others', tangled and fading.

Reks read the whale like a text.

The bull would surface in eight seconds, forty yards to port. It would come up under Boat Two if they didn't move. But if he threw now—if he put the harpoon in the node that pulsed at the base of the skull—the shock would turn it. The flukes would come up instead of down. The killing strike would be a deflection, not an attack.

A suicide shot. The kind no harpooneer attempted, because the arc was wrong and the distance was changing and if you missed, you'd thrown away your only weapon and the whale would kill you.

Reks stood.

The boat pitched under him, and his body adjusted without thought—knees bent, weight low, the sailor's compensation that never left him. The harpoon was in his hand. He didn't remember picking it up. The iron head was cold, and the cold bit through his glove, and the cold was the Toll starting to take, but he didn't feel it yet.

The throw happened before he was ready.

His arm went back, and his shoulder rotated, and the harpoon left his hand in a long flat arc that shouldn't have worked. The line sang. The iron head caught the grey light and held it, and for a moment the harpoon seemed to hang in the air—a black line against the white sky—and then it dropped, and the bull breached into it.

The iron buried itself at the base of the skull.

The bull shuddered.

It was a full-body thing, a convulsion that started at the head and ran down the sixty feet of its length like a wave hitting a shore. The great tail rose out of the water and hung there, dripping, and then it fell—not on the boat, not on the men in the water, but sideways, a glancing blow that sent spray over all of them. The bull rolled. The eye came up one last time, and Reks saw it.

Not fear. Recognition.

The whale knew him. Not as Reks, not as Ranertia Corway, but as something else—something that had been reading the sea's memory since before the ship-wards were carved, since before the Corway name was spoken in Halvask, since before the house was attainted and burned. The sea remembered. The whale was part of the sea. The whale remembered something.

Then the eye went dark.

The Toll hit.

Reks felt it in his chest first—a clench, a fist closing around something that wasn't an organ. Then the cold. Not the cold of the northern water or the ice-chunks or the spray. A cold that came from inside, from the space where the Sight had been, spreading outward through his ribs and into his arms and legs until his fingers went numb around the harpoon line. Steam rose from his skin in the freezing air. The sweat on his forearms was evaporating faster than it should, and the heat was going with it—leaving him hollow, leaving him shaking, leaving his vision grey at the edges.

He stayed upright through sheer stubbornness.

No one noticed the steam. They were too busy staring at the dead whale.

"Pull them out," Tamas said. The bosun's voice was still even. Still calm. But his scarred hand—the one with the missing fingers—was white-knuckled on the tiller. "Pull them out now."

The oarsmen were already moving. Boat Two cut through the chop toward the floating wreckage, and hands reached over the side, and bodies came up—Drenn first, coughing water and bile, his lips blue; then Orlen, who'd somehow kept hold of a boat-hook and was using it to drag one of the replacements toward the boat; then the other replacement, a young man whose name Reks had never learned, whose leg was bent at an angle that meant it was broken.

Four men in the water. Four men pulled out.

The drum had stopped.

---

Tamas pulled Reks back into the boat.

Reks didn't remember sitting down. He was in the stern now, the rope still coiled around his ankle, the harpoon line slack in his hands. The bull's body floated in the red water, vast and still, and the gulls were already gathering—white specks against the grey sky, their cries sharp and eager.

Tamas's hand was on his shoulder. The scarred one, the missing fingers, the knuckles swollen from a lifetime of cold-weather labor. The bosun didn't say anything. His face was weathered teak, the lines around his eyes deep enough to hold shadow, and he looked at Reks the way he'd looked at him for seven years—like he was reading something written in a language he half-remembered.

Nothing needed to be said.

On the Grey-Eye's deck, Pip was cheering. The boy was fourteen, all elbows and Adam's apple, and his voice cracked on the second syllable. He'd seen the throw—everyone had seen the throw—and he was too young to understand how close they'd come. Too young to know that the men in Boat One would have died if Reks had been a second slower, or a foot to the left, or if the bull had turned the other way. He just saw the dead whale and the full hold and the pay that was coming.

Reks didn't wave back.

Drenn was in the bow of Boat Two now, wrapped in someone's oilskin, his beard already freezing into a mask. He was looking at Reks. His eyes were the color of shallow water over mud, and they were fixed on Reks with an expression that wasn't gratitude. It wasn't hatred, either. It was the look of a man who'd just learned something he didn't want to know, and was trying to figure out how to unknow it.

He said nothing.

The silence was almost gratitude.

---

The flensing took hours.

They hauled the bull alongside the Grey-Eye with block and tackle, and the crew fell to work with flensing knives and hooks, stripping the blubber in long white strips that steamed in the cold air. The baleen was cut out in sections—each frond worth more than a month's pay—and the meat was hauled up in dripping slabs to be salted and packed in the hold. The gulls fought over the offal, and the sea around the ship turned red, then pink, then red again as the carcass slowly disassembled.

Reks worked with the others. His hands knew the rhythm—cut, pull, strip, repeat—and his body was grateful for the motion. The Toll had left him hollow, a cold knot in his chest that wouldn't quite loosen, and the physical labor was easier than standing still. No one spoke to him. No one asked about the throw. The crew had seen him do strange things before—find whales in fog that should have hidden them, predict storms that weren't in the sky yet—and they'd learned not to ask. The captain paid them to hunt, not to wonder.

The captain.

Orris Vane was on the quarterdeck, wintermint frozen on her lip where she'd been chewing it for the last hour. She was fifty-four, copper-eyed, built like a spar—tall and thin and unbreakable. She'd been captain of the Grey-Eye for twenty years, and she'd lied to the Dominion for Reks twice. Once when they came asking about Corway refugees in Midden. Once when they came back.

She was watching him now.

Not the whale. Not the flensing. Him. Her copper eyes tracked him across the deck, and her face was unreadable, and when Reks met her gaze for half a second, she looked away first. Not because she was afraid. Because she'd seen something she didn't want to confirm.

Reks put his head down and kept cutting.

---

The secret moment came after.

Alone at the rail, with the flensing done and the crew below and the whale reduced to a skeleton that hung in the water like a ghost of itself. The gulls were still there, wheeling and crying, and the sea was still red, and the sun was going down behind the ice-pack in a smear of pale gold that reminded Reks of his own hair.

He touched the amulet.

It was under his tunic, against his sternum, warm from his skin. Whalebone, old and smooth, carved into a shape that was too worn to identify. His farmor's work. She'd made it for him when he was six years old, before the soldiers came, before the fire, before he learned that the Corway name was a death sentence and the Corway look was a brand. She'd pressed it into his hands and told him something—he couldn't remember the words, but he remembered the shape of them, the weight of them, the way her eyes had been the same fjord-blue as his and just as old.

The sea remembers.

He didn't say it aloud. He never said it aloud. But the words were there, in the warmth of the whalebone, in the red water, in the memory of the bull's eye meeting his. The whale had known him. Not as Reks, not as Ranertia, not as the last son of a proscribed house. As something else. Something that had been reading the sea's memory since before the Corway name was spoken. Something that the Deep Chorus might recognize, if he ever got close enough to listen.

He didn't want to know what.

The cold was still in him—the Toll's hangover, the price of the Sight. His hands were steady now, but the knot in his chest hadn't loosened, and his vision still greyed at the edges when he moved too fast. He'd sleep for ten hours tonight, if the dreams let him. He'd wake up hollow and fill himself with coffee and salt pork and the rhythm of the ship. He'd go back to work.

That was what you did. You paid the Toll and you kept moving.

The whale was dead. The crew was alive. He'd saved them with a gift that had gotten his family killed. The irony didn't escape him—it sat in his chest next to the cold, a second weight, just as heavy.

He didn't name it grief.

He just stood there, salt on his face, until Pip called him for dinner.

The amulet was warm against his sternum. Farmor's work. The sea remembered.

He went below.

---

The hold smelled of blubber and blood and the particular sweetness of whale meat beginning to cool. The crew was packed into the mess—twenty-three men and one cabin boy, wedged onto benches that had been built for eighteen, eating salt pork and hardtack and thin stew made from the previous hunt's leavings. The bull's meat would feed them for weeks, but tonight they were still eating the last whale. There was a ritual to it, a continuity. The old whale feeding the men who had killed the new one.

Pip was talking too fast, his hands describing the arc of Reks' throw, his voice cracking with excitement. The men around him were nodding, grunting, not quite looking at Reks. Drenn was in the corner, his back against the hull, eating his stew in silence. The bandages on his hands were white against his reddened skin. He didn't look up when Reks came in.

Tamas made room on the bench. The bosun's bowl was half-empty, and he pushed a hunk of bread across the table without being asked. Reks took it. The bread was stale and hard and exactly what he needed.

"Three days to Midden," Tamas said.

Not a question.

"Three days," Reks agreed.

"Captain's already got the manifest drawn up. The baleen alone—" Tamas shook his head. "Good season."

"Good season."

They ate in silence after that. Not the uncomfortable silence of men with nothing to say, but the companionable silence of men who'd said everything that mattered years ago and were still here anyway.

When Reks went up for the night watch, the sky was clear. The stars were out, hard and bright, the kind of stars you only got in the northern waters where the cold burned the haze out of the air. The Grey-Eye's mast creaked in the swell, and the ice-chunks knocked against the hull with their patient rhythm, and somewhere below, in the hold, the bull's bones were settling into the dark.

Reks looked south.

Toward Midden, where the season ended and something else began. He didn't know what yet. But the whale's eye, before it died, had looked at him with something that wasn't fear. Recognition, maybe. The sea remembered. The whale remembered something.

He stood at the rail until his watch ended, and the cold seeped into his bones, and the stars wheeled overhead. Then he went below, and the dreams were waiting, and the amulet was warm against his chest.

The sea remembered.

He didn't want to know what.
