Chapter Two: The Final Berth

The Grey-Eye came into Midden on a dying wind.

Reks was aloft when the river-mouth opened before them, reefing the last of the topsail with fingers that moved without asking. Sixty feet of air between his body and the deck, the mast describing slow circles against the grey sky, and he was thinking about a sheave that needed replacing—the jib halyard, the block was starting to score the line where the bearing had worn flat—when the ship rounded the final headland and the city spread itself before him like a net pulled up from the sea.

He stopped. One hand on the spar, the other still holding the reef-line. The canvas flapped once and then settled. His body adjusted to the mast's sway without thought—knees loose, hips following the roll—and he let himself look.

Midden sprawled in the basin below the low mountain ring, terraced districts stepping down toward the river confluence where the Vein and the Silvervein met and tangled like two ropes from different ships. The water caught the dying light and threw it back in sheets of copper and lead. West, the Wyrm-Teeth were a dark serration against the pale sky; east, the Spine rose in a grey wall that said this far and no further. The Accordance's embassy towers stood white and clean above the lower city, each one flying its faction's colors in the offshore breeze. Frostmark blue. Valeris gold. Aethelian black. Khaz-Moran crimson. All of them pretending at peace in the only city that made them.

The Warrens were invisible from here, buried under the oldest part of the city, but Reks knew where they were. He could feel them the way he felt a storm in a piece of baleen—a pressure, a waiting.

He didn't want to go back down. The masthead was the only place his body made sense, and Midden was the only place it didn't. Neutral ground meant everyone was here. Frostmark exiles drinking in the dock taverns, comparing scars and grievances. Dominion agents with silver eyes and too many questions. Valeris pilgrims in their sun-bleached robes. Khaz-Moran traders who'd slit your throat for a handful of Arcanum crystals. Anyone could be looking for a blonde, blue-eyed Corway. Anyone could be carrying cold iron and a writ of attainder with his family's name on it.

The sailcloth snapped again. Reks realized he'd been holding the reef-line too tight. He loosened his grip. Finished the tie. Climbed down.

---

The deck was organized chaos when his boots hit the planks. Dock crews were already hauling lines across the narrowing gap of grey water, and the crew was moving through the thousand small tasks of putting a ship to bed. Coiling rope. Battening hatches. Lashing the chase-boats to their davits.

Pip was on the rail, chattering at Tamas about shore leave. The boy's voice cracked on every third word, and his hands were describing something large and improbable. A tavern, maybe. A girl. The same thing, to Pip.

"—and then we'll find a place with real food, not salt pork, actual meat with gravy—"

"Steady," Tamas said.

"—and I heard there's a market in the Warrens where they sell these little fried things, like dumplings but sweet—"

"Pip."

"—and I'm going to eat thirty of them, I don't care what it costs, I've got pay coming—"

The bosun's scarred hand came down on the boy's shoulder with the gentleness of a man who'd learned to be careful with the fingers he had left. "Help Drenn with the port davit."

"But I'm—"

"Now."

Pip went. His shoulders were still bouncing with undischarged excitement, and he was still talking, aiming the words at whoever was in earshot. Drenn, on the port side, was sharpening his harpoon with long slow strokes of a whetstone. He didn't look up when Pip approached. But something had shifted in him since the hunt—the silence was less a blade and more a door left slightly open. Not friendly. Just less.

The Captain was on the quarterdeck, wintermint frozen on her lip, watching the dock crews with her copper-coin eyes. She'd been watching the dock crews since they entered the river-mouth. Reks had seen her do it every season for seven years, and he still didn't know what she was looking for. The habit of command, maybe. The certainty that someone was always about to cheat you.

He climbed down from the rigging and went to work.

---

The last lines. The last knots.

Reks secured the Grey-Eye the way he'd done it a hundred times, moving from bow to stern in the order his body remembered. The bowline to the forward bollard: two round turns, a clove hitch, the bitter end tucked under. The stern line: the same, but with a double hitch because the river current tugged harder aft. The spring lines fore and aft, to keep her from ranging in the night. The fenders between hull and dock, old canvas bags stuffed with cork, fraying at the seams.

His mind ran the rigging inventory without permission.

The mainmast shrouds: port three, starboard three, all sound, no chafe. The forestay: tension good, the turnbuckle greased. The jib halyard: the sheave needs replacing, tell the bosun, you've told him twice, he won't listen. The topping lift: there's a strand parting three feet from the splice, you saw it this morning, you should—

He pulled the last knot tight. The ship was secure. The season was over.

The crew was already dispersing—down the gangplank in twos and threes, pay in their pockets, shore leave in their eyes. Reks stayed at the rail. He wasn't ready. The city was too large, too loud, too full of eyes. On the ship, everyone knew what he could do and no one asked. On shore, the questions started before he'd finished his first drink.

A hand on his arm.

The Captain. She was shorter than him by half a head, but she stood like she was taller—weight back, chin up, the copper eyes steady and unblinking. Her grey hair was damp with river mist. Her missing fingers were tucked into her coat pocket. She held out a leather pouch.

"Your pay."

He took it. The weight was right—the familiar heft of a full season's labor, minus deductions for the flensing knife he'd lost overboard in the spring and the advance he'd taken for new boots. He tucked it inside his coat.

She held out a second pouch. Smaller. Heavier.

"And this."

He didn't take it. "Captain."

"Don't." She pressed the pouch into his hand. Her fingers were cold and dry, the knuckles swollen with the same cold-weather arthritis that was waiting for him in twenty years. "You saved four of my crew. Drenn's an ass, but he's my ass. Orlen's got grandchildren. The two Midden boys you don't even know the names of." She paused. The wintermint crackled between her teeth. "Take the coin."

He took it.

"There's been people asking."

The words landed in his chest like a stone in still water. He didn't move. His face didn't change. But the rigging inventory stopped. The ropes went slack.

"Dominion," the Captain said. "A woman. Pale, silver eyes, long dark hair. She knows what you are. I told her nothing."

The river lapped against the hull. The dock crews were shouting in the distance. Somewhere on deck, Pip was still talking—the boy's voice a bright thread in the grey afternoon.

"Boy." The Captain's voice was lower now, rough at the edges. The wintermint had stopped crackling. "Whatever she offers you—it's not worth it."

Reks looked at her. The copper eyes were unreadable, but her jaw was set, and the hand in her coat pocket was clenched around something. Her missing fingers. Or a weapon. Or both.

She knew. She'd always known. The Corway look, the uncanny hunts, the way he found whales in fog and predicted storms that weren't in the sky yet. She'd lied to the Dominion for him twice—once when they came asking about proscribed bloodlines in Midden, once when they came back with a description and a warrant. She'd never asked for an explanation. She'd never asked for anything.

Some debts were too old to call in. Some questions were too dangerous to ask.

"Thank you, Captain."

She snorted. A short, unimpressed sound, the kind she made when a green sailor claimed the wind was shifting and he'd been wrong about the last three shifts. She let go of his arm. She stepped back.

But she watched him walk down the gangplank. She watched until he reached the dock, until the crowd swallowed him, until there was nothing left to watch but the space where he'd been. Tamas came to stand beside her, the bosun's scarred hand resting on the rail, his silence a question.

Neither of them said anything.

Both of them knew exactly what they were watching for.

---

Pip caught him at the foot of the gangplank.

The boy materialized out of the dock-crowd like a splinter working its way to the surface—suddenly there, all elbows and determination, his hair sticking up in twelve directions. He'd been running. His chest was heaving.

"I'm coming with you."

"No."

"I'm not staying on the ship all off-season. Tamas snores. Drenn's an ass. I can help—"

"Pip."

"—I've been in the Warrens before, I know the east tunnels, I won't get in the way—"

Reks knelt.

The motion surprised them both. He was eye-level with the boy now—those brown eyes that were too old for fourteen, too sharp, the eyes of a Midden pickpocket who'd learned to read faces before he learned to read words. Three years ago, Reks had caught that boy trying to lift his purse in a dock tavern. He'd grabbed the wrist, felt the bird-bones under the skin, and bought him a meal instead of breaking his fingers. Three years of looking after him. Three years of teaching him knots and knife-work and the hundred small cruelties of surviving on a ship.

Three years of pretending he wasn't responsible for another life.

"Stay with the ship," Reks said. His voice came out rougher than he'd meant. "Listen to Tamas. Don't steal anything. Don't follow me. Don't look for me. I'll be back before the season starts. That's an order."

Pip's face did something complicated. The brown eyes went bright and then hard, and his jaw set in a way Reks recognized—the way of a boy who'd been left behind before, by parents or circumstances or the indifferent arithmetic of Midden's streets.

"You're not my captain."

"No. I'm the man who caught you trying to lift his purse and decided you were worth more than the coin in it." The words came out flat and fast, a harpoon thrown without the full wind-up. "Stay. On. The ship."

Pip stared at him. The dock-crowd moved around them—sailors and merchants and grey-market traders, the endless circulation of the Accordance's neutral ground. A cart rumbled past, its wheels grinding on the cobbles. Gulls screamed.

Then Pip said, very quietly: "You're going to do something stupid. I can tell. You've got that look."

"What look."

"The one you had right before you threw the harpoon."

Reks didn't have an answer. The look. The one where the threads were already showing him the arc of the thing before it happened, where his body was already moving, where the decision had been made before his mind caught up. Pip had seen it from the Grey-Eye's rail, three days ago, when the bull turned and the boats were too close and everything was about to go wrong.

He'd seen it. He'd remembered.

Reks stood. He put a hand on Pip's shoulder—just for a moment, just enough pressure to say what he couldn't say—and then he walked away.

Mote wound between his ankles as he went. The ship's cat, grey and scarred and missing one ear, survivor of a dozen seasons and a dozen docks. The cat meowed. The cat didn't understand. The cat was probably better off.

Reks didn't say goodbye to the cat either.

---

The city swallowed him.

Midden in the off-season was a tide-pool of everything the Accordance pretended to regulate. The main thoroughfares were clean and orderly—embassy guards in faction colors, merchant stalls with stamped licenses, the occasional Sentinel patrol in their grey cloaks. But the side streets were different. The side streets belonged to the grey market, to the smugglers and information-brokers and unlicensed mages who paid the right bribes to the right officials. The Warrens were beneath even that, a labyrinth of pre-Accordance tunnels and catacombs where the rules got thin and the knives came out.

Reks walked with his hood up and his head down.

The street narrowed. The buildings leaned toward each other like old sailors sharing a joke, their upper stories almost touching. The smell changed—salt and fish and tar giving way to the sharper reek of crowded humanity, cooking fires, the particular sweetness of something rotting in an alley. The light went grey and then grey-blue, the river's reflection dying as the sun dropped behind the Wyrm-Teeth.

He was heading for the Warrens. He had contacts there. A room he rented by the week, paid through a Shaded Market factor who didn't ask questions. A plan: disappear, wait out the off-season, rejoin the Grey-Eye in the spring. The plan was simple. The plan had worked for three years.

The plan was already failing.

He could feel it in the threads—not the Sight, not yet, just the sailor's instinct for a coming storm. The tension in the air. The pull, like a line going taut. Something was waiting for him in Midden. Something that knew his name. The Captain's warning was a cold weight in his stomach. A woman. Pale, silver eyes, long dark hair. She knows what you are.

The same description the crew had whispered about in Midden last season. A Dominion agent asking questions about proscribed bloodlines. Asking about rune-lore. Asking about a family that was supposed to be dead.

Reks pulled his hood lower. His hand went to the amulet beneath his tunic—whalebone, old and smooth, warm from his skin. Farmor's work. The sea remembered.

The river confluence gleamed silver between the buildings ahead. The Spine was a dark wall on the eastern horizon, the last light catching on its peaks. The air smelled like rain, or snow, or something else—something cold that had nothing to do with the weather.

He walked into the Warrens as the light failed.

---

The entrance was a sewer grate behind a fishmonger's stall, rusted at the hinges and slick with offal. Reks paid the fishmonger a copper piece for the use of his back room, climbed down the maintenance ladder, and dropped into the dark.

The tunnels were the same as they'd always been. Damp stone, the drip of water somewhere in the distance, the smell of mold and old magic and things that had died in corners. The walls were pre-Accordance—rough-hewn, marked with the faded remnants of ward-sigils that hadn't worked in centuries. The floor was uneven, worn into channels by water and feet and time.

Reks walked without a light. He knew this route. Third tunnel on the left, past the collapsed archway, through the chamber with the mosaic floor—don't step on the black tiles, they're trapped, or they were last season—and then the long slope down to the Shaded Market's eastern entrance.

His footsteps echoed. The rigging inventory was still running in the back of his mind, a quiet litany. Mainmast shrouds: all sound. Forestay: tension good. Jib halyard: the sheave needs—

He stopped.

The tunnel ahead was dark, but not the right kind of dark. There was a quality to the blackness that his eyes didn't like—a texture, a density. The threads were stirring. Not the Sight, not yet, but the precursor, the prickle at the back of his skull that meant something was about to open.

Something was waiting for him in Midden.

He put his hand on the flensing knife at the small of his back. The handle was worn to the shape of his grip. The blade was sharp. He'd sharpened it this morning, the ritual his grandmother had taught him, the whetstone moving in long steady strokes while he mouthed the old words under his breath.

He walked into the dark.

The tunnel swallowed the sound of his footsteps. The drip of water stopped. The rigging inventory went quiet.

Reks kept walking.
