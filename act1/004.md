Chapter Four: The Broker

The tea was still hot. Reks hadn't touched it.

Sera Vaunt sat across from him with her coat off and her hands folded on the table, the whalebone pendant resting at her throat. She'd given him her name. She'd given him her division—Dominion Intelligence, Special Acquisitions, the kind of title that sounded clean and meant dirty. She hadn't given him the price.

The bodyguard stood behind her. Kael, she'd called him. A name that fit—short, hard, the kind of syllable you could carve into stone. The cold iron was everywhere on him. Sword across the back. Studs in the vambraces. Knife at the belt. The static-pressure of his presence was a low buzz at the edge of Reks' perception, the way the rigging hummed before a squall. It made the threads harder to see. It made everything harder to see.

Reks let the silence stretch. The brazier in the corner was still burning, the same coals, the same orange pulse under the ash. The server hadn't come to the back room since he'd sat down. Either Sera had paid for privacy or Kael's silhouette was deterrent enough.

"You said you had a job," he said. "You didn't say what kind."

"A retrieval." She lifted her cup—the same pale green tea, the same pot—and took a sip. "There's a sub-level of the Maze that doesn't appear on any Accordance survey. The entrance is warded. Bloodline-specific. Designed by your great-grandfather seventy years ago. It requires a living Corway to open."

"I'm not a rune-carver."

"You don't need to be. You just need to be you." She said it without flattery, the way she might have noted a key's shape or a lock's mechanism. "The door responds to Corway blood. Or Corway sight. My researchers haven't been able to determine which."

"Your researchers."

"The Dominion has an interest in pre-Accordance Frostmark artifacts. Your house produced some of the finest work in the northern tradition before its attainder. Whatever your great-grandfather sealed in that sub-level, he considered it worth protecting from everyone except his own blood. That suggests it's valuable." She set the cup down. "That suggests it's worth finding."

Reks looked at the pendant. Farmor's work. The ship-ward pattern was unmistakable—the same as the faded tattoo on his inner wrist, the same as the amulet beneath his tunic. The threads were faint at this distance but present, a shimmer that came and went with the pulse of the bodyguard's iron.

"The pendant," he said. "Let me see it."

A pause. She could refuse. She was holding most of the leverage.

She reached up, unclasped the chain, and held it out to him in her palm. Her fingers didn't brush his when he took it. She was careful that way.

The contact was immediate.

Farmor's voice wasn't in the pendant—that would have been too clean. What was there was older and stranger. The memory of a storm warded off the summer he turned seven, the pressure-drop in his sinuses, the particular off-key hum she used for the big wards. The smell of whale-oil and old wool. The way she'd tuck the pendant into his shirt when he was afraid, her knuckles bony against his chest.

Under that: Sera's imprint. She'd been wearing it for months. The memories were layered and fragmentary—cold rooms, a book with a cracked spine, a man's voice saying report in a tone that expected obedience. A name that glowed warmer than everything around it: Maris. A wound dressed but never healed.

He pulled back. The static from Kael's iron made the reading harder than it should have—the edges of the images were frayed, the sound distorted. The Toll was a cold ache settling into his molars, familiar and unwelcome. He'd been clenching his jaw without noticing.

Sera was watching him. Her expression hadn't changed. But she'd stopped breathing—not a gasp, not a held breath of surprise, just a stillness in the chest. She knew what he'd just done. She was waiting to see what he found.

He set the pendant on the table between them.

"Where did you get this?"

"I acquired it." Her voice was steady. "From a collector in Ferrax who didn't know what he had."

"You knew."

"I suspected. Now I know." A fractional pause. "You read it."

It wasn't a question. He didn't confirm or deny.

"Why do you need a Corway? Specifically."

"The door is bloodline-warded. My researchers have tried every bypass in the Dominion archives. The wards were designed to exclude everyone except a living Corway. Not a key. Not a password. A person. Your great-grandfather was thorough."

"What's in the vault?"

"I don't know."

The admission cost her. The micro-tightening around her mouth, the way her thumb started to move and then stopped before it completed the circle. She wasn't used to saying those words. She wasn't used to being questioned by someone who wasn't afraid of her.

Reks leaned back. "You're asking me to walk into unmapped territory, open a door my great-grandfather sealed, and retrieve something you can't identify. For the Dominion. Which has been hunting my bloodline for seventy years." He didn't raise his voice. "Why should I believe you'll let me walk out again?"

"I'm not asking you to believe me. I'm asking you to make a deal." She leaned forward. The strand of hair fell across her cheek again. She didn't tuck it back. "You've been selling yourself cheap for years, Corway. A whaler's pay. A Maze-runner's fee. You're worth more than that. I'm offering full market value."

"The names of the men who killed my family aren't market value. They're leverage."

"Yes. And you want them. And I have them." She held his gaze. "And you'll take the job because the alternative is spending the rest of your life wondering if you could have known. If you could have found them. If you could have done something about it."

The cold in his molars spread to his jaw. Not the Toll. Something else.

She was right. She knew she was right. Something shifted in her eyes—not satisfaction, exactly. Something quieter. She'd made her case and she knew it was good.

"What's the pay?" he asked.

She told him. It was enough to buy passage to Valenwood and vanish forever. Enough to buy a new name, new papers, a life somewhere the Corway look wouldn't get him killed. The Captain's voice was a dull echo in the back of his skull: Whatever she offers you—it's not worth it.

He reached for the Sight again.

The static from Kael's iron pushed back—a pressure at the temples now, the buzz sharpening to a whine at the upper edge of hearing. The threads around Sera were faint but legible. She wasn't lying about knowing who ordered the purge. The thread that connected her to that knowledge was clean and bright—she had the information, she was prepared to trade it. She wasn't lying about the door. The thread there was confident, supported by the kind of certainty that came from research, not speculation. She wasn't lying about the Dominion's interest. The thread was official—she had backing, resources, a mandate.

But there was a gap. A shape missing from the weave, a blank space where a motive should be. The artifact—whatever it was—wasn't just a Dominion objective. It was personal. The thread that should have connected her to the mission brief frayed at the edges and disappeared into something warmer. The same warmth as the name Maris in the pendant. The same wound.

He could follow that thread. He could push deeper, past the surface of her words, into the place where the secret lived.

He didn't.

Partly because Kael's iron was making his vision swim. Partly because going that deep into someone without their knowledge felt too close to what his great-grandfather was accused of—reading what shouldn't be read, crossing the line into heresy. Partly because he didn't want to know. He had enough secrets of his own. He didn't need to carry hers.

He pulled back. The threads dissolved. The cold in his molars settled into something that would ache all night. He added it to the tally.

Sera was still watching him. Her hands were folded on the table now, the thumb still. She knew he'd read something. She didn't know what.

"What aren't you telling me?" he asked.

The pause was smaller this time. She'd been expecting the question. "Several things. None of them relevant to the job."

"That's not reassuring."

"I'm not trying to reassure you. I'm trying to hire you." She picked up her cup, found it empty, set it down again. "The relevant facts are these: the entrance exists, it requires you, the Dominion is willing to pay, and I have the names you want. Everything else is my business."

"Everything else is what gets me killed."

"If I wanted you dead, Corway, you'd be dead. Kael is very efficient." She said it without emphasis, the way she might have noted the time or the weather. "I don't want you dead. I want you employed. The distinction matters."

Reks looked at the bodyguard. The bodyguard looked back. The man hadn't blinked since Reks sat down. Maybe he didn't need to. Maybe the cold iron had replaced the parts of him that did ordinary human things.

"How long?" Reks asked.

"Three days. Maybe four. Supplies are provided. Kael handles the heavy work. You handle the navigation and the door." She paused. "And anything else that requires a Corway's particular talents."

"My particular talents."

"Don't be coy. You read the pendant. You read me. You've been doing it since you sat down." Her gaze was steady. "I'm not offended. It's what I would do in your position. But I'd prefer you didn't go fishing in my head without permission."

"I wasn't in your head. I was in the pendant."

"The pendant has been against my skin for four months. There's very little difference at this point." She reached across the table and picked it up, her fingers closing around the whalebone. For a moment, she held it in her palm, looking at it. Then she clasped it back around her throat. "I'm keeping this. You'll get it back when the job is done."

"I didn't ask for it."

"You didn't have to." She smoothed the pendant against her collar, centering it. "It was your grandmother's. You want it. That's not a criticism. It's an observation."

Reks said nothing. The pendant was back at her throat, the ship-ward catching the lamplight, and the threads had gone quiet again—muffled by distance, by Kael's iron, by the wall Sera had rebuilt between them.

"Second Bell tomorrow," he said. "Where?"

She gave him the location: an old sluice gate at the edge of the Warrens, where the canal went underground. Supplies for three days. Kael handled the heavy work. She stood, and Kael moved to open the door, and she paused at the threshold without turning around.

"Don't be late, Corway. I don't like waiting."

"You said that already."

"I meant it both times."

She was gone. The paper screen slid shut. The lamplight flickered in the draft, then steadied.

Reks sat alone in the back room. The tea had gone cold. He hadn't taken a single sip. The brazier was still pulsing in the corner, the coals shifting with a soft click, and the server was still absent, and the pendant was gone—back around Sera's throat, where it had been for four months, where it would stay until the job was done.

He stood. His jaw ached from the Toll. His temples were still buzzing with the afterimage of Kael's iron. He'd used the Sight twice in an hour, and the cold was settling into his bones the way it did after a bad hunt—a slow freeze, a debt not yet called in.

He walked out into the night.

The Market had quieted. The fish-oil lamps were guttering in their brackets, and the cold-glow crystals were the only steady light—Dominion light, pale and even, spilling across the cobblestones. The Khaz-Moran spice trader was gone. Gerrit's booth was dark. The money-changer had packed up his stall and left nothing but a scattering of Valeris sun-stone chips that glittered in the gutter like dropped coins.

Reks didn't go to his rented room.

He walked toward the Warrens instead. The entrance behind the fishmonger's stall was still unlocked—the same rusted grate, the same maintenance ladder, the same smell of mold and old magic. He climbed down into the dark and let the tunnels swallow him.

The pendant was in his pocket.

Not Sera's pendant. His own. The amulet farmor had pressed into his hands when he was six years old, before the soldiers came, before the fire. He'd touched it without thinking when Sera clasped hers back around her throat. A mirroring. A reflex. The whalebone was smooth and cool, and the threads in it were quiet—his own memory, his own past, no one else's layered on top.

He walked through the dark with his hand around the amulet and his jaw still aching and Sera's voice in his head.

You'll take the job because the alternative is spending the rest of your life wondering.

She was right. He hated that she was right. He hated the way she'd leaned forward and the hair had fallen across her cheek and she hadn't tucked it back, like she'd forgotten it was there, like she had more important things to worry about than a strand of hair. He hated that he'd noticed. He hated that he was still noticing.

The tunnel sloped down. The air grew colder. Somewhere ahead, water dripped on stone with a sound like a clock that had lost its rhythm.

He was doing the job. He'd told himself it was for the names—the men who burned the village, the ones who were still alive, the ones he could find and make answer for what they'd done. That was true. It was also not the whole truth.

The whole truth was more complicated. The pendant, farmor's voice, the missing shape in Sera's motives, the way she'd argued with him like he was an equal instead of an asset. The way she'd said I'm keeping this and he'd believed her—believed she'd give it back when the job was done, believed she was the kind of person who kept her word even when it cost her.

The whole truth was that he was curious. About the vault. About the door. About the woman with the silver eyes and the cold iron bodyguard and the wound she carried under her ribs like a second heart.

The whole truth was that he was standing on the deck of a ship that wasn't his, sailing toward a destination he couldn't see, and the woman at the helm had his grandmother's pendant and secrets she was keeping even from herself.

He didn't name it. He didn't have a word for it. The word would have been mutiny—a loss of command, a course change he hadn't ordered—and he wasn't ready to think it yet.

The tunnel opened into a junction. Three passages: east to the Shaded Market, south to the sluice gates, west to his rented room. He took the south passage. He wanted to see the gate before tomorrow. He wanted to know the exits.

He told himself that was all.
