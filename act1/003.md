Chapter Three: The Shaded Market

The fish-oil lamps were guttering by the time Reks found Gerrit's booth.

Not the first booth. Not the second. The fourth—tucked behind a Khaz-Moran spice trader whose veiled face tracked him across the market aisle, whose hand stayed inside the robe too long for comfort. Reks catalogued the trader and moved on. Gerrit's stall was a sagging canvas lean-to wedged between a canal-side tenement and a stack of crates that smelled of river rot. A single cold-glow crystal hung from the awning, Dominion-made, its light the color of old ice.

Reks shouldered through the flap.

Gerrit looked up from a tray of dubious antiquities—tarnished amulets, a Valeris sun-stone chip that was probably glass, a whalebone comb with a crack running through the spine. His mustache twitched. His hands, which had been sorting the tray with the nervous precision of a man who expected every transaction to be his last, went still.

"Reks." The mustache twitched again. "You're back."

"Gerrit."

"Season's over, then. The ship—the ship docked today? I heard—someone said the Grey-Eye made port. Good season? Full hold?"

Reks didn't answer. He let the silence stretch. The booth was too small for three people, barely large enough for two, and the canvas walls did nothing to stop the Market noise from bleeding through—haggling, footsteps, the splash of an oar in the canal. A pair of Valeris pilgrims argued with a money-changer two stalls over, their sun-stone chips clinking on the counter. The money-changer's voice was flat with repetition. The exchange rate is what it is. Take it or pray for a miracle.

Gerrit's left eye had developed a tic. It jumped every time the money-changer said no.

"You owe me," Reks said.

"I—yes. The Maze. Last year. The tunnel collapse." Gerrit swallowed. His fingers found the whalebone comb and began turning it over and over, the cracked spine catching the cold-glow light. "You pulled me out. I remember. I remember very clearly."

"Then tell me what's got you chewing your mustache off."

Gerrit set the comb down. Picked up a tarnished amulet. Set that down too. His hands were looking for something to do and failing.

"A woman," he said.

Reks waited.

"Asking about Frostmark rune-carvers. Not just any rune-carvers." Gerrit's voice dropped, barely audible over the Market noise. "Corway."

The word landed.

Not a physical blow—Reks had taken those, knew how to roll with them, how to breathe through the impact. This was different. This was a rope pulling taut in his chest, a line he hadn't known was attached to anything. The rigging inventory—the mental checklist of shrouds and stays and halyards that had been running in the back of his mind since he'd climbed down from the Grey-Eye's mast—stopped.

Silence. One beat. Two.

Corway.

The name his grandmother called him. The name his father forbade. The name that had been a death sentence for seventy years, carved into a writ of attainder, sung in old sailors' songs as a cautionary tale. Someone knew. Someone had found him.

The inventory slammed back into place: exits, threats, the weight of the flensing knife against his spine. But the crack had already shown. Gerrit was still talking, his voice a thin stream of words, and Reks forced himself to listen.

"—buying up every scrap of Corway carving that surfaces in the Market. She has Dominion backing. The crystals, the accent, the way she moves. You know the type. Like she owns the air."

"How long."

"Three weeks. Maybe four. She's been working through middlemen at first—people like me, people who don't ask questions—but last week she started asking by name." Gerrit's mustache was twitching so hard it looked like a small animal dying on his upper lip. "Your name. Not Reks. The other one."

Reks didn't ask which one. He already knew.

"She's got a bodyguard," Gerrit said. "Cold iron. Big. Doesn't blink. The kind of man who makes you check your exits before you sit down." He swallowed. "She's waiting at the Silver Leaf. Two streets over. Said to tell you—" He stopped. His eye tic jumped.

"Said to tell me what."

"Said to tell you the pendant remembers."

The words hit harder than the name had.

Not a rope this time. A hook. A curved thing, sinking into the soft place below his ribs, the place where the old songs lived and the old words—farmor, liten, hearth-ember—huddled like refugees in a forecastle. The pendant remembers. That was farmor's phrase. The house motto was The sea remembers, carved over the longhouse door, sung in the old songs before the soldiers came and the fire took everything. But the pendant remembers—that was private. Something she used to say when she held her whalebone charm against a storm, her voice low and steady, the words a ward against the wind.

No one outside the household should know those words.

No one should be wearing them like a key.

"Reks." Gerrit's voice was barely a whisper now. "I didn't sell you out. I want you to know that. She came to me because—because everyone knows I deal in antiquities, and Corway carvings are antiques now, and I didn't—she made it clear that not delivering the message would be worse than delivering it. She's not the kind of person you say no to. She's the kind of person who makes you forget 'no' was ever a word."

Reks looked at him. The little man was trembling—not the exaggerated shake of a con artist selling a story, but the real thing. The fine vibration of a man who'd looked into something and seen his own death looking back.

"When."

"Tonight. She said—she said you'd come. She said to tell you she'd wait until midnight. After that—" Gerrit's hands fluttered. "She didn't say what happens after that."

Reks turned toward the booth's flap. The Market noise rushed back in—the Valeris pilgrims still arguing, the money-changer still refusing, the Khaz-Moran spice trader still standing in the same spot with the same hand inside the same robe. The cold-glow crystal flickered overhead. The light was the color of Dominion eyes. The color of a woman who knew his grandmother's words.

The Captain's voice, three hours ago: Whatever she offers you—it's not worth it.

Pip's voice, at the gangplank: You've got that look.

The whale's eye, three days ago, looking at him before it died: recognition, not fear. Something waiting. Something that knew his name.

Running wouldn't help. Running had never helped. The past was a wake, but this past had oars. This past was rowing toward him with Dominion backing and a cold iron bodyguard and a pendant that remembered. He could meet it. Or he could let it catch him from behind.

Those were the only two options.

"I'll go," Reks said.

Gerrit looked like he wanted to argue. Or warn him. Or flee the city. The tic in his eye jumped twice more, and his mustache did its dying-animal twitch, and then his whole face collapsed into something that might have been relief or might have been terror.

"Reks."

"What."

"There's one more thing. She said—" Gerrit swallowed. "She said to tell you she's been looking for you for a long time. That you're harder to find than your grandmother was."

The hook twisted.

Reks didn't answer. He pushed through the canvas flap and walked into the Market, and the noise swallowed him, and Gerrit's booth disappeared behind a wall of bodies and lamplight and the particular chaos of the Accordance's neutral ground.

---

The Silver Leaf was two streets over, but Reks didn't go straight there.

He moved through the Market the way he moved through the Maze—not the shortest route but the smartest one, doubling back through the fruit-sellers' stalls, cutting through a narrow alley that smelled of cooking grease and old blood, pausing at the mouth of a canal-side square to scan the crowd. The off-season Market was always crowded, but tonight the crowd felt wrong. Too many people standing still. Too many eyes flicking toward the same corners. The air had the quality of a rope under too much strain—the twang before the snap.

Threat-scan: the fruit-seller's stall. A cleaver on the chopping block, a marline spike stuck through a coil of twine, a bottle of cheap grain alcohol that would burn if you put a flame to it. Exit: the canal-side door behind the money-changer's booth, half-hidden by a stack of empty crates. The Khaz-Moran spice trader has moved—no, he hasn't. Same spot. Same hand inside the robe. Waiting for someone.

Reks pulled his hood lower. The waxed canvas was stiff with salt and old weather, and it hid the hair, but it couldn't hide the way he moved—the sailor's sway, the feet finding deck-planks that weren't there. He was a Frostmark rigger walking through a Midden Market, and anyone who knew what to look for would see it.

The woman with the silver eyes knew what to look for.

He turned down a side street. The lamplight changed—fewer fish-oil flames, more cold-glow crystals, their Dominion light spilling across the cobblestones in pools of pale blue. The buildings here were older, pre-Accordance, their upper stories leaning toward each other until the sky was a thin grey ribbon overhead. A sign creaked in the wind: the Silver Leaf, its characters painted in silver that had flaked to grey, a tea-plant curled around the letters.

The teahouse was small. Discreet. The kind of place where information changed hands and the tea was strong enough to cover the taste of whatever you were drinking to forget.

Reks stopped at the mouth of the alley.

The cold-glow lamp above the teahouse door flickered. Inside, shapes moved behind paper screens—a figure sitting alone at a corner table, another figure standing, too large to be a server. The standing figure didn't move. Didn't shift its weight. Didn't do any of the things a person did when they were waiting.

Gerrit's voice, in his head: Doesn't blink.

His hand went to the flensing knife at the small of his back. The handle was worn to the shape of his grip—a good fit, a true fit, the kind of fit that came from years of use and the particular intimacy of a tool you'd sharpened yourself. He didn't draw it. He let his palm rest on the pommel, feeling the weight, the curve, the cold.

The whalebone amulet was heavy under his tunic. Not warm. Heavy. The way it got before a storm.

He thought about Pip. You've got that look. The boy had seen it—the moment before the harpoon left his hand, the moment when the arc was already showing in the threads and his body was already moving and the decision had been made before his mind caught up. Was this the same? Was he walking into the teahouse because he'd chosen to, or because the threads were already pulling him there?

He didn't know. He wasn't sure it mattered.

He walked forward.

The door of the Silver Leaf swung open on silent hinges. The interior was dim and warm, heated by a brazier in the corner, the air thick with the smell of black tea and something floral—jasmine, maybe, or the Dominion equivalent. The paper screens glowed from within. The figure at the corner table was a woman in a dark traveling coat, her back to the door, her hair a fall of black that caught the lamplight.

The standing figure was a man. Big. Cold iron at his belt, cold iron in the studs of his vambraces, cold iron in the hilt of the sword across his back. His eyes tracked Reks from the door to the table without blinking. His face was a stone that had learned to look like a face.

The woman turned.

Her eyes were silver. Not grey—silver. The color of Dominion cold-glow, the color of the crystals that lit the Market, the color of a winter sky over the Helspine when the ice was closing in. They met his and held.

"Ranertia Corway," she said. Not a question. "You're late."

Reks stopped three paces from the table. Close enough to see the pendant at her throat—whalebone, old, carved in a pattern that his fingers recognized before his mind did. A Corway ship-ward. The same pattern as the faded tattoo on his inner wrist. The same pattern as the amulet against his chest.

His grandmother had carved that pendant.

He didn't know how he knew. He just knew—the way he knew the stress-point in a mast before it cracked, the way he knew the whale's path before it breached. The threads were there, faint and shimmering, and the pendant was singing with a voice he hadn't heard in seven years.

He sat down.

"You have something that belongs to me," he said.

The woman smiled. It was a small smile, a careful smile, the kind of smile that had been practiced in front of a mirror until it looked real.

"No," she said. "I have something that belonged to your grandmother. You have something that belongs to me."

The cold iron bodyguard didn't blink. The brazier crackled. Outside, in the Market, someone started shouting in a language Reks didn't recognize, and the shouting was cut off mid-word, and the silence that followed was the kind of silence that meant a knife had been drawn.

The woman poured tea into a second cup and pushed it across the table.

"Drink," she said. "We have a great deal to discuss."
